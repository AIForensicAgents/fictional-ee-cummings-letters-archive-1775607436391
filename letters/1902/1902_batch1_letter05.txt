Postcard to a Window

Date: 1902-05-16
Recipient: The Room on the Third Floor
Location: Providence, Rhode Island
Year: 1902
Themes: place, memory, domesticity, tenderness

Dear Window,

Forgive the informality, but I have addressed this to you because you know me best. Chairs come and go with tenants, and even the clock forgets, but you keep the afternoon folded like a linen napkin, and you release it when the light pleads.

Do you remember the evening I moved the chair one inch and found yesterday’s sun sleeping on the floor? Your glass shivered—either from the night’s draft or because you laughed. I cannot say. I only know I felt enough happiness to set down the dusting cloth and stand there, as if I had discovered a lake shy as a teacup.

A pigeon has taken my place on the sill; treat him kindly. He will leave faint letters in his feet. When the rain rehearses its music against you, close your wooden lids slowly, in that dignified way of yours, and think of me walking under a green umbrella that has decided to be a small planet.

Keep for me the square of shadow near the stove—the one shaped like a folded map. In it I learned to boil water without scaring it. In it I allowed certain griefs to evaporate without witnesses. If anyone new sits there, lend them my patience. It is tucked behind the nail where the calendar used to hang.

With the fondness one reserves for clear things,
A Tenant Who Loved You

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
