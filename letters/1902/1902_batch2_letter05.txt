Concerning Our Machine of Stars

Date: 1902-11-19
Recipient: Mr. Horace Bell, Watchmaker
Location: Cambridge, England
Year: 1902
Themes: time, craft, friendship, science, autumn

Sir,

Concerning our machine of stars: the brass circles arrived this morning swaddled in paper that smelled faintly of lemon and ink. I laid them out on the table and the room became a quiet argument of radii. The smallest wheel spoke first, in a voice like a teaspoon tapping memory; the great wheel answered with the patience of a church door.

You will approve the tolerances, I think. The orrery will not forgive sloppiness; planets are unkind to guesswork. Yet there is mercy in the movement if we build correctly: a way in which error is absorbed and made to seem intention. I think often of your hand on my wrist when we last met, guiding me to feel the second's pulse—not as division but as promise. The histories insist that time is a thief; our bench insists it is a craftsman who loves small parts and does not mind working late.

When I was a child my father let me take apart the mantel clock (a dangerous generosity). For days it sat on old newspaper like a complicated bird that had flown to pieces and was considering reassembly. I learned that a spring is a kind of hope with instructions wound tight inside it. I learned that dust is not innocent; it is an engineer of endings. Now, as November drags its black skirts over the pavements, I think a machine that teaches patience might be a useful heresy.

I have included the diagram with revisions: Mars given a little more honesty in its lean; the Earth gifted a slightly bolder blue; the Moon kept properly in her restlessness. Tomorrow at ten, shall we begin the calibration? Bring your loupe and your habit of listening. If you put your ear to the frame, as you once taught me, you will hear the quiet correction of the world—the faintest forgiveness as teeth find each other and turn.

Forgive this being a letter rather than an invoice. A man must write to someone about the tenderness of gears or he will begin confessing to kettles and hatstands. You are the only one I know who understands that a well-made escapement can improve a Tuesday.

Until then, I remain,
Your obedient collaborator in celestial mischief,
Edwin

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
