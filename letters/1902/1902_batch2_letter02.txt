The Field Will Remember Us

Date: 1902-06-03
Recipient: Brother Amos
Location: Ellis County, Kansas
Year: 1902
Themes: family, labor, weather, grief, resilience

Brother Amos,

The wheat lifts its green wrists to the wind and learns a prayer I cannot speak without dirt in my teeth. I yoked the team at dawn, a thin blue hour—horizon like a seam you could unpick with one careful breath. By nine, the sky had hammered itself into brightness and the meadowlarks were writing cheerful scandals in the fence wires.

I say cheerful and yet there is the ledger of the clouds. We have been promised a storm all week: thunderheads shoulder one another from the southwest, tall as cathedrals nobody can afford. I sharpened the plow just to hear something answer me. If Father were here he would say keep the furrow straight and the heart crooked enough to hold rain. Strange counsel, and yet the thirsty know this trick—you tilt your cup.

Neighbors came by with talk. Dobbins put up more barbed wire as if the sky were livestock that might wander. Ellen Kaye sent bread; it tastes like late Sunday afternoons when we were boys and had no use for time but to spend it badly. I thought of the winter we buried him, your hand on my shoulder like a second coat. Grief is the heaviest harvest: we carry it into spring and it does not rot. But look—out past the cottonwoods, the air is ragged with swallows, and the cattle stand black as punctuation against the pale page of day.

I find all sorts of reasons to write: the smell of rain that is almost here; the way the creek solved a rock by singing to it for years; the fact that my fingernails are a crescent calendar of work. Mostly I write because your last letter named me brave, and I wanted to set down (honestly) that I am afraid sometimes of an empty sky, of too much future. When the wind climbs through the corn like a careful thief I count the breaths between lightning and thunder, and I hope to be precisely who you think I am before the counting ends.

Come July, if the heads are full and heavy, we will cut and sing like we used to, you with that off-key hymn and me pretending it was on purpose. If the storm breaks us, we will plant again. The field will remember us either way; it keeps the shape of our footsteps the way a bed keeps the dream.

Write soon. Bring your laugh west when you can. The prairie misses the foolishness of brothers.

Your own,
Caleb

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
