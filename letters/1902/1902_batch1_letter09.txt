On the Subject of Silence

Date: 1902-10-14
Recipient: Reverend Alistair Greene
Location: Edinburgh, Scotland
Year: 1902
Themes: faith, doubt, humility, quiet

Reverend Greene,

You asked how my prayers fare. I answer as plain as I can: they go out well dressed and come home without their hats. I still attend Sundays, and the bell sews morning to the week with admirable steadiness, yet the needle sometimes misses me.

I believe in bread and in the hand that breaks it. I believe in the coat hung over a shivering shoulder, in the stove that keeps an old kettle useful, in the way a mother says hush to a fear with no name. If the Almighty prefers a different liturgy, I hope He will forgive my homely catechism.

The other night, silence came as a guest who had read all the books and wished to speak of none. We sat. We let the lamp lose track of us. After a time, I felt my pulse resume its civil conversation. That seemed, if not revelation, at least a letter from its cousin.

Please do not think me insolent. I ask for nothing fancy—only a path that can be walked in these boots, and the occasional star when the ground forgets itself. Write me when you have time with a parable that involves mud. I trust mud; it keeps honest accounts with the rain and the foot.

Respectfully,
A Parishioner Who Is Learning to Listen

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
