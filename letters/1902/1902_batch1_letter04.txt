Lilac Smoke above the Mill

Date: 1902-04-30
Recipient: Mrs. Agnes Whitlow
Location: Lowell, Massachusetts
Year: 1902
Themes: labor, spring, hope, industrialization

Dear Mrs. Whitlow,

You asked whether the new looms purr or gnash. I must answer: both. Morning begins with the wide throat of the room clearing itself, then the belts develop their quick, complicated manners. By noon we wear a halo of lint that would be holy if it did not choke. Yet there is comfort in the repetition—the shuttle going and gone; the thread learning to trust what it cannot see.

From our windows—those tired eyes—we watch the river run under its collar of brick. Sometimes a bit of lilac smoke drifts from the dye house and the day admits it is spring. Hannah stitched a bird into the hem of her apron and says it keeps her from forgetting. I have nothing so clever, but I have pinned a scrap of blue ribbon to my sleeve, so that, if anyone should ask, I can point and say: this is a piece of sky I am saving for later.

You spoke of your garden. Please plant marigolds for me in that bald patch near the steps. The colors here are largely obedient—gray, tannin, discipline. A yellow that refuses to be reasonable would do me good.

As for wages: they answer the bell but seldom my needs. Still, I send enclosed two coins and a promise to visit in June, when the river decides to be a friend. Keep well. Eat warm things. And should the violets volunteer along your fence, give them my regards.

Your affectionate,
Clara

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
