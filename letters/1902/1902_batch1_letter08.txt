Harvest Envelope

Date: 1902-09-03
Recipient: Ruth Fielding
Location: Des Moines, Iowa
Year: 1902
Themes: rural life, love, work, hope

Dear Ruth,

The corn stands taller than last year, a congregation in green, listening hard to the wind’s sermon. We have stitched a hundred sacks and will stitch a hundred more. At dusk, chaff settles on our sleeves like fine, stubborn light. I have learned to read the sky’s face for weather—the way a bruise of purple near the west means hurry, and that righteous gold means lay down your tools and simply look.

I took your letter to the far fence where the world loosens its belt. There, your words made a small house around me. You spoke of a porch, a table, two cups—how easily I believed. The old mule consented to carry the boards from the barn as though he, too, wanted a place to rest that was not exactly sleep.

When we milked this morning, a swallow wrote its name above the door so fast it was finished before I blinked. If we marry come spring, I will hang a bell that knows when to stay quiet. And when quiet fails us, we will count the kernels left in the measure and call that arithmetic hope.

The train has begun making its evening statements. If you hear it where you are, think of me by the pump, red hands, good ache, planning the steps between this life and the next small, brave one.

Faithfully,
Jonah

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
