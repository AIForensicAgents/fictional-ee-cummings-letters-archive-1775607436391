Harbor Psalm with Coal Smoke

Date: 1902-03-27
Recipient: Mother
Location: New York, New York
Year: 1902
Themes: migration, hope, motherhood, city, identity

Dear Mother,

From the deck this morning the harbor opened like a book with a thumb between its pages. Gulls tore little white confetti from the wind. A boy beside me announced America as if he had invented it during the night. We slid past islands of rust—barges with tired backs—and the city stood up all at once, a skyline like new teeth in a child's daring smile.

I kept your thimble in my coat pocket and pressed it the way some press silver charms. It made a small true circle against all this noise. The Statue holds up her bright argument and the smoke writes complicated mathematics in the air, but the thimble says simply: mend. So I will: my courage where it frays; the places where my name breaks into syllables nobody here pronounces correctly yet.

At Ellis the world elbowed closer. We drifted in a patient river of faces, tired but shining at the edges, as if polished by the possibility of bread. A clerk asked my age and I forgot it, not out of dishonesty but because age seemed poor currency in a room where longing had the higher exchange rate. A physician peered at my eyelids like a pale botanist. Somewhere a violin attempted to comfort the floorboards.

Do not think me lost. I counted the steps to the street and kept your advice folded inside my mouth: be curious; be careful; say please practically to the weather. I found a room near South Street with a window that frames a sliver of river the color of pewter coins washed thin by too many hands. The landlady is stern but as I paid her she allowed one corner of her mouth the afternoon off.

I shall find work at the cannery or the press. The city is a stern teacher but perhaps a fair one; it already graded my posture and assigned me a shop of smells to memorize: tar, oranges, boiled coffee, rain hot on bricks. When I looked up, a line of laundry across the alley made flags of ordinary shirts, and I felt—unexpectedly—at home in the republic of what must be done.

Tell Tom the boat was brave and did not brag. Tell Elsie the pigeons here wear a kind of evening wear all day long. Keep the back gate latched; I left in such a hurry I fear even the wind will think it can come and go as it pleases.

I will write again when I have an address that belongs to me more fully. For now, trust that I am safe, that your thimble keeps true north inside an unfamiliar pocket, and that I am mending.

With love that stretches like the harbor bridges and does not break,
Ruth

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
