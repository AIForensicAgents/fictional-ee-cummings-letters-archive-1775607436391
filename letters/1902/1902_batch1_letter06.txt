Elegy with Coal-Dust

Date: 1902-06-08
Recipient: Father
Location: Scranton, Pennsylvania
Year: 1902
Themes: family, labor, aspiration, grief

Father,

The mine hoists its dark thought every morning and sets it down again at dusk. Men come up shaped like winter, their faces newly written upon by smoke. I stand in the yard and watch the wind read them clean. Your cough measures the day’s profit—each rib a rung on a ladder we all promised not to climb, yet did.

Mother says the bread will not rise if I speak of leaving. I knead in silence, and the dough listens to my worry the way a well listens to a coin. You taught me to strike a match against my boot and not my thumb—reserve the soft parts, you said, for what is meant to touch them. I try.

If I go, it will not be from shame but from a wish to breathe a broader sentence. I will take your lamp. Its glass bears a thumbprint you left the night the seam collapsed and the dark learned our names. I have polished it, but the mark refuses to vanish; perhaps it is not dirt but a small geography I must carry.

I promise to write from the first town that smells like rain. I promise to send back a handkerchief that has seen a river, a book that refuses to be closed, and a story in which a man’s lungs are not bargaining chips. Keep the chair by the porch for me. I will come home when the blackbird explains how he does it—how he sings with soot on his throat and still finds the note that opens the morning.

Your son,
Michael

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
