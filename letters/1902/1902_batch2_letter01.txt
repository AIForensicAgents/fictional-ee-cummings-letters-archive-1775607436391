A February Envelope (and other small combustions)

Date: 1902-02-14
Recipient: Eleanor Hart
Location: Boston, Massachusetts
Year: 1902
Themes: love, winter, city, longing, memory

Dear Eleanor,

February puts its cool hand on Boston and everything rings—streetcar bells, the thin laugh of the river, my name when you said it last Tuesday (how it burned quietly in my coat). I walked the Common this evening and the snow behaved like an unopened letter, bright and dangerous with possible sentences. The elms were bare telegrams; the sky kept whispering stop and the lamps kept saying no, go on.

I passed the florist's window. Paperwhites leaned their breath against the cold like children fogging glass, and I could not afford them, not even one. But I carried a warmer coin, a small gold heat stamped with your face and the date of our meeting, which I kept turning over in my pocket to make light, to make time behave. Forgive me the theatrics—I would send a pocket watch that forgets every hour but the one where you look up, or a matchbox filled with summers we have not yet spent.

I have tried to be prudent but love refuses the grammar of prudence; it invents its own commas, runs where it should walk, and leaves parenthetical remarks everywhere I look (your hand at the cuff of your glove; the brave, precise tilt of your hat against weather). Mornings I sit with coffee and the newspaper's black snow, and your name glides over columns of wars and stocks until the world looks almost bearable again.

If you keep this letter, keep it flat under your pillow for emergencies. On nights when the city turns its shoulders and forgets our names, press this page to your heart and it will remember for both of us. I have included a pressed plane leaf, caught in October and still rehearsing green in secret; perhaps this proves that some things (us, for instance) can be stubborn enough to outlast the season appointed them.

Write when you can. Or better, say my name again in the hallway when no one expects kindness—say it as if opening a window in a library where the dust has mistaken itself for the air.

Yours in a small, bright conflagration,
Arthur

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
