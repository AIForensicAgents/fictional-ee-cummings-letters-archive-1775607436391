this traveling house of steel

Date: 1945-10-03
Recipient: Mother
Location: Union Pacific Train, Somewhere in Nebraska, USA
Year: 1945
Themes: homecoming, landscape, forgiveness, quiet, family

dear mother,

the country keeps its promises in windows. cornfields unscroll like yellow prayers, and telephone poles write a music only the wires can hum. a man across the aisle sleeps inside his hat; a boy counts clouds as if they were nickels; my duffel breathes under my boots like a tired dog.

i have learned to shave on a moving thing and to dream without doors. the porter says midnight is the best hour to believe in home. i think he is right. last night the moon kept pace like a good friend who will not let you walk alone.

forgive the miles i carry. some of them have faces. i left what ghosts i could at small stations with names that sounded like promises. if father asks, tell him the dark is not as sharp as it used to be, and my hands have remembered butter from a skillet.

i will be at the depot by supper if the tracks keep their straightness. leave the porch light on like a soft truce. please do not fuss with the good plates; let us use the cracked one that knows our mouths. i want to sit where the kitchen keeps its courage and watch the kettle make a little weather for us.

your son,

ben
