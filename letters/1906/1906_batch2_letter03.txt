the country unbuttons its sky

Date: 1906-08-19
Recipient: Mother
Location: Pullman Car No. 12, Union Pacific Line
Year: 1906
Themes: travel, landscape, family, longing, modernity, movement

dear mother,

the train scissors the afternoon into bright strips and keeps them in its pocket. each window is a moving mouth that keeps trying to say prairie and never quite finishes. i have learned the grammar of telegraph poles, how they pronounce distance with a stutter.

there are windmills that sip the wind and then politely nod. small towns drift past the way a thought crosses your face when you are not looking in a mirror. a boy with a hat too old for his head waved to our compartment like he had been practicing hello all summer.

i am reading the bible of fences, the psalms of hay, the quiet chapter called weather. once a herd of cattle became a punctuation mark across the field. the engine answers every question with smoke, but i keep asking anyway in case the sky wishes to change its mind.

the porter has kindness folded with his shirts. he tucks evening around my shoulders as if i were the sort of son who does not forget to be gentle. last night i dreamed our kitchen made a nest for the kettle and it hatched a song. i woke up just when the chorus would have forgiven me for leaving.

have you eaten peaches yet this season. do not be thrifty with sugar. please tell sarah that the locust shells on the maple will release us when they are ready, not sooner. tell father that the hammer he lent me is doing God proud, even when i use it to quiet my foolishness.

i will write again when the mountains get close enough to button my coat. the sky is loosening and dusk is a bruise that does not hurt. if there is a star for every mile, then home has already been born a thousand times today.

with all my unpracticed steadiness,

eli
