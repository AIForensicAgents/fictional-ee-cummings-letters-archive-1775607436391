the envelope of sunday

Date: 1950-02-19
Recipient: My Brother Thomas
Location: Cincinnati, Ohio
Year: 1950
Themes: family, work, memory, faith, place

Tom,

sunday folded itself neatly over the river, a white shirt on the line, and every bell in the city remembered our names. i walked past the old hardware where father taught our hands the grammar of weight (how a hammer says yes, how a nail says still). the clerk keeps his ledger like a church book, squared and smudged with the coal of weekdays.

i took the late shift at the press last night—the alphabet yawned, ink slept until we woke it, then the metal mouths kissed paper until dawn. there is a mercy in repetition: click, close, release; like the heart if it believed in schedules. i thought of you, your two daughters tangling their ribbons in morning, and i wanted to mail them the smell of new paper—clean as a held breath.

going home i counted pigeons on the courthouse roof (there are not as many as when we were brave). maybe we were never brave, only small enough to fit into the spaces between fear and its shadow. mother writes that her hands ache with the late winter; send her the woolen gloves we laughed about, the green ones. they are ugly enough to be loyal.

write me the sound of the river at noon. mine keeps coughing coal at its edges; it forgets how to be blue. but once in a while, a barge passes and the water smooths its hair and pretends it is the sea.

your brother,

eli

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
