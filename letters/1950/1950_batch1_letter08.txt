the grammar of forgiveness

Date: 1950-10-04
Recipient: Mr. Halberd, my former teacher
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1950
Themes: forgiveness, learning, humility, time, atonement

Mr. Halberd,

i am writing at a kitchen table that lists slightly to starboard, which suits an apology: everything worth saying slides to the edge. do you remember the essay i didn't write and the book i didn't return? it was thin and red and full of courage disguised as quiet. i carried it through a spring storm, then a summer of jobs (frying eggs at dawn, folding shirts that believed in squares), and by autumn it began to believe it lived with me.

i make no noble excuses. i was young in the noisy way, and fear wore a clever mask labeled busy. when you asked me why commas, i said for breathing and you nodded as if i had learned something. i forgot how to breathe for a while, and the sentences between us went out to the porch and did not come back.

today i found the book on the back of my closet's top shelf where dust writes its slow epic. inside, your note: be brave enough to be precise. sir, i am trying. i return the book with a letter pressed like a leaf inside it and a library fine i invented for myself (payable in errands, in honest work, in coffee brought hot without being asked).

if there is a grammar for forgiveness, maybe it is this: a subject (i) meets a verb (ask) and an object (you), and somewhere a period becomes a door.

respectfully,

ada
