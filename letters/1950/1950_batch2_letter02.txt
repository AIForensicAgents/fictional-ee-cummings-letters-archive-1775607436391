a kite made of desert and sunday

Date: 1950-03-05
Recipient: Brother Eli
Location: Santa Fe, NM
Year: 1950
Themes: family, art, memory, desert, homecoming

eli—

the sky here is a blue shirt on the clothesline, snapped taut by invisible knuckles. i walk out where the brown begins to hum and let the wind unbutton my thoughts. the adobe walls remember more summers than i have pockets to keep. their shadows are cool and moral.

you asked whether paint still listens. it does. i tap my brush against the coffee tin and the sound says: be patient. i make a church of every uncooperative color; i kneel; i rise; i am forgiven by an accidental orange in the corner. once, a lizard paused on the windowsill and cocked its head as if to judge composition. (we compromised: i left a sun crooked and it left me the afternoon.)

i dream sometimes of the winter in '44, of the radio that spoke in iron voices, of mother polishing the same spoon until it confessed. you remember the way we tied a string to a paper bag and made a kite out of hunger. we taught it to argue with the sky. brother, i am still arguing, but i have better string.

the postmaster unfolds his newspaper with a priest's seriousness. i like him. he smells of paste and eucalyptus. every stamp is a road; every cancellation is a bootprint. i am sending you three sketches wrapped in yesterday's weather. one is a fence thinking of escape. one is a saintly chair. one is simply the wind holding its breath.

write when your hands are tired of lifting other people's furniture. write when they are not. i will put a nail in the biggest part of the day and hang your words like a coat.

your brother (sunburned and soft about the edges),

aaron
