coal-dust prayer

Date: 1950-06-01
Recipient: Mother
Location: Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania
Year: 1950
Themes: labor, hope, education, family, resilience

Ma,

the mill whistled me awake again at five, its breath like a sermon nobody asked for. the men at the gate counted themselves by coughs and cigarette tips. i keep a pocket of clean air for when the furnace opens its dark mouth; it is small but it is mine.

night classes begin tonight—typing and drafting on the third floor above the shoe shop (the stairs smell like glue and rain). the teacher says the GI Bill is a bridge; i keep stepping, even when the fog says be careful, even when the river jokes about swallowing bridges whole. my hands, which have learned the psalms of grease, are willing to learn a new hymn: the soft drum of keys, the blueprint's shy blue.

we knelt to plant tomatoes behind the boardinghouse and a train passed, and all our seedlings turned their leaves as if the iron were the sun. it made me laugh, and then it made me sad, which is how laughter behaves around here. i washed my nails in a bowl of milk and they came out less honest but more polite.

i will come sunday with a loaf of rye and that photograph of you in the polka-dot dress so you can tell me again about the county fair and the cake that rose like a miracle. i need a miracle i can slice and butter. until then, kiss the porch cat for me and keep the radio low. a man's heart needs silence to hear itself get better.

your son,

michael
