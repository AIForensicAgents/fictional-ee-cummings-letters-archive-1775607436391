the almost-morning before peace

Date: 1918-11-10
Recipient: Samuel Reed
Location: Railcar westbound, Nebraska, USA
Year: 1918
Themes: armistice, travel, brotherhood, grief, hope

sam,

the prairie is a low hymn outside the window and every fencepost a listener. we are a string of strangers traveling together, holding our breath in the same way. the porter whispers that tomorrow the war may fold itself up like a tired map. the car breathes steam and hope (uneven, but brave).

i bought a newspaper that smelled of wet iron; the headlines were nearly a promise. across from me a woman knits so quickly you would think she was mending time. a boy sleeps with his cheek on a biscuit tin. the world, for once, has forgotten to growl.

do you remember how in august we learned a new word for silence? we pronounced it with our backs. today i think we may learn a new word for morning. perhaps it sounds like a latch lifting. perhaps like boots set down very carefully by a door.

of course, i am thinking of those who will not meet this almost-morning. i keep their names in my coat, and when the train turns, they ring together softly, gentle coins. we will spend them wisely: a garden spaded, a porch whistled upon, a lamp kept lit too long for the pleasure of it.

when i arrive i will bring bread, and we will eat until our shadows are friendly. then we will plant something small and stubborn before the frost. if peace delays, we will not; if it arrives, we will remember to say thank you out loud.

write me when your shadow grows shorter. i would like to measure it against the window and call the difference future.

your brother,
isaac

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
