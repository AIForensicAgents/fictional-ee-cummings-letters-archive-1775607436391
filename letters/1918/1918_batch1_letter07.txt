gauze and thunder

Date: 1918-07-20
Recipient: Reed
Location: Field Hospital near Château-Thierry, France
Year: 1918
Themes: war, nursing, names, compassion, resilience

reed—

today we stitched the world to itself as best we could. the thunder did an imitation of artillery and the sky did not protest. i learned to fold gauze into swans that collapse beautifully; the men like to watch the beaks soften in their palms as the ether climbs a ladder made of breath.

what i'm trying to tell you is: names are everything. when i lean to a mouth blooming red and ask, who shall i write to, the answer is sometimes a river or a street or mary. i write them all, reed. i write the rivers back to their banks and the streets to their doorsteps and mary to a pair of knitting needles shaking slightly in ohio.

there was a child in the village today who traded me three plums for a song. we sang wildly off-key and were perfect for a minute. afterward i washed my hands and the stains became small, obedient ghosts.

when i close my eyes i can see your terrible hat. it is a mercy to think of it. please keep it terrible. keep the dog from sympathizing with your melancholy. teach it jokes. if anyone asks what i need, tell them more light, more paper, more of the world admitting it is breakable and also worth the bother of mending.

with a forehead full of night and a pocket full of dawn,
—m.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
