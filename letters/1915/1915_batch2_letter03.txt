Field-Post (unmailed)

Date: 1915-10-03
Recipient: Brother Thomas
Location: Ambulance Depot, Flanders
Year: 1915
Themes: war, compassion, night, distance, brotherhood

thomas,

the road is all consonants tonight; our wagon spells itself in rattles and damp wrists. the moon, that disciplined enamel button, has been fastened to a torn sleeve of cloud and looks at us as if we were its last available music. we pass farms that have forgotten their cows and churches that whisper in a language of black rafters.

i am learning the weight of another man’s breath. it is not heavy—it is exact. we lift him, and the air becomes a careful rumor. the sheets try to become wings, the morphine tries to outsing pain, and my heartbeat counts like a bookkeeper desperate for holiness.

there are puddles with stars in them; there are boots that invent new maps of mud. each hour rehearses the same impossible question: how can a night be both an animal and a prayer?

i have a joke folded in my pocket (it helps the wheels), and a photograph of home—which continues to grow in the dark like yeast, swelling with our mother’s hands, your loud patience, the white clapboard that remembers us even while it peels.

if this letter never finds you, imagine it walking barefoot across the sea; imagine its small lantern. know that in the narrowish places—those cruel corridors of almost—i carry your name like a warm stone.

wake early. save me a corner of the porch where the sun stutters first.

your brother,
S.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
