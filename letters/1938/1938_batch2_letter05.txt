stationery for a moving train

Date: 1938-12-02
Recipient: Future me (opened when the snow forgets the roads)
Location: Night train between Chicago and Cincinnati
Year: 1938
Themes: travel, time, winter, rails, self

Original fictional letter in a modernist, experimental, emotionally vivid vein; not written in the style of any specific author.

Dear Future Me—

I am writing on a lap that keeps changing its mind. The Pullman breathes like a great animal sleeping politely. In the corridor the porter folds night into neat squares; his hands are the quietest clocks I have ever seen. Out the window the fields wear moon the way a widow wears laughter—beautiful, not entirely allowed.

If you read this when the snow has forgotten the roads, remember the lamp that trembled over this paper and the whistle that shaved the dark smooth. Remember the faces in the car: the salesman kissing his sample case as if it were a wife, the girl with a green ribbon measuring America one town at a time, the farmer counting in his sleep and arriving at prayer. We are all luggage that pretends legs.

I think of the stations we skip—small platforms held together by one man’s patience and a broom. Sometimes the engineer does not slow, but I feel the town anyway: a store that sells only hope and nails, a dog that knows the exact weight of waiting, a kitchen where a woman sets down a plate as if setting down the world.

Future Me, be tender with the last good shirts. Let the collar fray with dignity. When you open a window, don’t forget the invisible hands that closed it before you. Keep a coin in your pocket for luck and for people who do not ask.

I would like to bring you news of the heart: it is still a station with too many tracks. Trains arrive with names like Mercy and Mischief; sometimes they are the same train, mislabeled. If I could send you one ticket, it would be for Courage, third class, because that is where the singing is.

We are slowing for a town whose name is a question I can’t hear. The snow will come. The roads will be surprised and then remember. Wake me when the window is a book again.

With a conductor’s punch through the margin,

M.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
