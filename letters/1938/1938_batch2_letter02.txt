ink that learns my breath

Date: 1938-03-21
Recipient: Rose
Location: Greenwich Village, New York
Year: 1938
Themes: love, city, spring, art, hunger

Original fictional letter in a modernist, experimental, emotionally vivid vein; not written in the style of any specific author.

Rose—

The city is a tall pocket with our names in the lint. Tonight the elevated pauses above Bleecker like a steel dragon forgetting its roar, and I walk under it, a moth in a hat. I am writing at a kitchen table as thin as my rent; the lamp hums a yellow hymn and my pen keeps surprising me with what your absence knows.

Can you feel March looking over our shoulders? The stoops are rehearsing for knees and laughter, and somewhere a trumpet polishes its hunger. I saw a man chalking a dream on the sidewalk: blue sun, red fish, a girl whose hair untied the sky. A gust took it and for a moment everyone walking wore art on their ankles.

When I close my eyes, you arrive—the good kind of thunder. Your mouth, a quiet bell; your hand, a letter that keeps opening. How close the river seems when I think of you. I stand at the pier and the water becomes cursive, spelling out every almost we have ever almost dared.

There is a WPA poster in the grocer’s window, the colors blunt and brave as a child’s. Work is not a stranger here, only a late guest; I set out an extra plate of patience. In the afternoons I read to an old neighbor who mistakes my hair for his youth; he laughs and says all sorrows look handsome from far away. He’s right, but I prefer the ugly nearness of now, where I can count your freckles even when you are not here.

Will you come when the tulips finally remember their red? Bring the shawl with the torn fringe; we will let the wind finish it. We will sit on the church steps and unwrap a sandwich like a small, edible blessing. I want to tell you in person how your name makes a room I didn’t know I was missing.

Write soon, even if soon is only one brave word.

Yours (ink and breath conspiring),

L.
