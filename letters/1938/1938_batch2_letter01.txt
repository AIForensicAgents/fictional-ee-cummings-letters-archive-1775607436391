the house listens

Date: 1938-01-12
Recipient: Mother
Location: Providence, Rhode Island
Year: 1938
Themes: winter, family, work, poverty, hope, radio

Original fictional letter in a modernist, experimental, emotionally vivid vein; not written in the style of any specific author.

Dear Mother,

Tonight the kettle sings like a sparrow in the stovepipe and the windows cloud until even the moon forgets us. I keep the lights low to spare the bill, and in that gentle dimness the wallpaper turns to winter trees. Do you hear the boards learning our footsteps? The house remembers every prayer we were too proud to say.

I have been walking to the paper on South Main, a hopeful hat in my hands. The editor is a thin man with a thin pencil; he rubs his lip and says perhaps a column if spring behaves. I tell him I can make a sentence climb a ladder and wave to the neighbors. He smiles as if it is December’s last coin. We will see. Between his door and home I count the cracks that have aged since last week. Sidewalk arithmetic keeps me from thinking of hunger, and hunger keeps me from counting too slowly.

On the radio this morning a voice warmer than coffee spoke of jobs and bridges. I closed my eyes and saw men hanging from steel like spider-sunlight, and for a breath it seemed the country was knitting itself a new sweater. When the set lost its station, the static turned to snow and the snow to silence, and there was the clatter of our own quiet, brave and thin.

I have mended the coat you gave me. On the sleeve, the stitch runs like a river that forgot where it began; no one will see it unless they love me. The grocer by the corner let me carry home a loaf on trust. I paid him with promises and he made change in kindness.

Do not be troubled by Father’s cough; it is an old visitor who forgets his hat and keeps returning for it. I warm the teacup before pouring and hold it like a small town in my palms. When he dozes, his mouth trembles into a leftover lullaby. I think of you then, in the chair by the curtain, needle and thread waking the torn world.

If I write too softly, it is only because January has a hand on my shoulder. But Mother, listen: even in this stingy light, I hear sap rehearsing in the maples. A rehearsal is not the show, but it’s the truth of what is coming. I will bring you good news as soon as good news knows my name.

Your child (pocketing the warmth of your voice),

A.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
