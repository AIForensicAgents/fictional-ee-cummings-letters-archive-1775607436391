postcard that grew longer than a street

Date: 1927-03-16
Recipient: Miss LeJeune
Location: New Orleans, Louisiana
Year: 1927
Themes: music, desire, city, spring, chance

Dear Miss LeJeune,

i borrowed the city's brass lungs last night and let them breathe through me: trumpets curving their hips, drums slapping the moon's ankles until she danced nearer. your laugh was the small sugar hurricane at the corner of Dauphine and Desire, where even the streetlamps pretended they were warm-blooded.

what am i to do with a hand that keeps remembering your shoulderblade as if it were a secret door? i knocked once and the room behind it was entirely made of lilac and sweat and bread. i do not suppose they sell maps to such addresses. the trolley shrugged, pulled us into the long green sentence of the park, and i spelled your name in magnolia pollen against my sleeve like a thief learning his first honest word.

the river winked its wide eye, and a tugboat misplaced the horizon for a minute. during that mistake i decided to write you this very sincere not-postcard that thinks it is longer than a street. perhaps you will forgive its furniture of commas and its balcony of sighs. perhaps you will read it to your mirror and both of you will smile the way wet shoes smile when they finally dry by the stove.

if wednesday has any kindness left, come with it. bring your rain hat and the scandal of your patience.

yours at the edge of a bright spill,

A.
