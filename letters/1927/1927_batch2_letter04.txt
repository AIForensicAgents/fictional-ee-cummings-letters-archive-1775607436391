after the lecture on electricity

Date: 1927-11-08
Recipient: Elias Hart
Location: Cambridge, Massachusetts
Year: 1927
Themes: technology, friendship, anxiety, modernity, night

Elias,

I confess: I attended the lecture only to count the moths. They invented a dance between the lamp and my patience and were beautiful at it. The speaker, thin as a question mark, explained the newest trick: how a wire can whistle a person’s voice across air you cannot even remember owning. The room hummed politely. Then the demonstrator tuned a box and from its wooden mouth leapt a hymn—flat, tin, sincere—and there we were, all of us, briefly translated into weather.

On the walk back I passed a diner that wore its neon like a drunken halo, and people with newspapers tucked like obedient geese under their arms. Everyone seems to keep a private spark now, a little invisible coin they spend by thinking. It is thrilling and it is terrible. Must a prayer compete with a switch?

I want to speak to you in an older voltage: hand to hand, lamp to window, letter to night. You will say I am romantic about kerosene, and perhaps I am, but the new brightness shows the dust on the mantel with such conviction I’m afraid to breathe. Still, I bought a small wireless set. It is patient with my clumsy soldering and sounds like rain rehearsing when I coax it awake. If, one evening, a voice reaches you that seems to belong to no one, consider me, skulking on a frequency like a borrowed coat.

Do you sleep well? Do you let your heart shut its green door? I try; yet all these inventions unlatch something. I heard the election returns tumble through the walls of boardinghouses like busy coins and imagined the country counting itself by touch.

Come visit. We will drink coffee that has practiced all night and then go borrow a lighthouse from the library—any thick book will do—and read by it until the dawn forgives us.

With a handshake the length of winter,

Isaac
