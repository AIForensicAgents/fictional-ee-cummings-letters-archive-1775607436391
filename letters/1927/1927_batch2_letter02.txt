on the back of a train ticket

Date: 1927-05-03
Recipient: Thomas Avery
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1927
Themes: travel, family, memory, music, modernity

Tom,

This will be brief; the station breathes like a giant dog and the benches are ribs you can sit between. I found a blank inch on the back of a ticket and decided it was enough prairie for a letter. Outside, the rain climbs the switchyard ladders and then jumps, laughing. How Chicago loves its own clatter.

There is a girl selling oranges, her apron a torn April. A porter tells me the word west as if it were a loaf he baked himself. The clock refuses to explain anything. I keep thinking of the old pump behind our house with its elbow of iron, how it coughed and gossiped, and of you; of the way we learned distance by measuring it against our lungs.

Last night I listened to a trumpet in a hall that smelled like varnish and someone’s quiet grief. The player stood very still as if a moth had pressed a white thumb to his back, and his sound made the ceiling forget itself. I wanted to send you that exact ceiling, careless and wide, but all I have is this ticket and the swift pencil you left in my coat.

Tell Ma I have not misplaced my good shoes. Tell her I button my thrift like a waistcoat and keep it clean. Send word about the wheat, and whether the river has forgiven the bridge we threw at it, summer of ’12. I told a waitress this morning how you once lit a lantern inside a clay jar and swore the stars had come to sit on our porch. She brought me extra butter and did not say why.

When the whistle calls another kind of blue from the metal, I will go where it sends me. Yet I travel also toward you, even away from you; the world contains that sort of map.

Your brother,

Sam

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
