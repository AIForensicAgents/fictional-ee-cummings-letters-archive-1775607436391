sea-salt and lily stamps

Date: 1927-07-21
Recipient: Mother
Location: Marseille, France
Year: 1927
Themes: sea, home, work, language, comfort

Mother,

There are lemons here that have learned the private grammar of light. I carry one in my pocket like a small yellow priest, and for the price of a coin it absolves the mouth of any darkness. The harbor shakes loose its morning—ropes wake with salt in their throats, gulls argue theology on the mastheads, and every hull remembers a storm it pretends to have outlived.

Do not worry for me; the work is honest. I translate for a fishmonger whose fingers are a rosary of scars. He says the sea teaches politeness because it slaps you and then invites you back, and I repeat the lesson until my tongue smells of tin and forgiveness. At noon we eat bread that has loved the oven too much and apricots that have forgotten their modesty.

I saw a woman in a lavender dress walking a bicycle up a cobbled ribbon of street, and a little dog trotting beside her like an adverb. Perhaps this is how happiness arrives in a foreign language: with wheels, slowly, and you do not ride it at first but keep pace until it trusts you.

I place lilies on envelopes as if they were addresses. Inside, I tuck the news: my cough has taken a vacation; the room I rent leans, but kindly; nights are stitched with masts and barefoot singing. Sometimes the wind names you, quietly; sometimes it merely rearranges my hair and I pretend your hand did it.

Please water the fern in my window as if it were a messenger. Kiss Father’s forehead where the wrinkle begins to think too much. I send a handful of sand trapped in the hem of this letter, and a coin that pretends to be silver—the both of them honest liars.

Your son with two shirts and a good appetite,

N.
