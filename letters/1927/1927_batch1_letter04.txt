small prayer to the engine

Date: 1927-04-09
Recipient: The Editor of Daring
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1927
Themes: ambition, modernity, art, industry, hope

Editor,

please accept these sentences which do not walk correctly but manage to arrive, smudged and grinning. i have stapled the wind to their margins and slipped in a freight car's cough for punctuation. chicago taught me this: every window is a throat, and smokestacks keep singing until the sky remembers the tune.

my poem (enclosed, trembling slightly) believes in sparks more than arguments. it trusts that a nail is a tiny star that fell into our pockets to help. i am aware that my lines refuse proper chores. they jump fences, they come home muddy, they hide dice beneath their tongues. but when the train heaves its black shoulder through the yard at five, those same lines kneel politely and wash. please do not punish them for their heartbeats.

i am trying to write the body of the century the way a mechanic learns the body of a car: by listening to where the hum forgets itself. perhaps your magazine has a cupboard where unruly words are kept until they are useful. let this one sleep there a week. it will wake with trackdust in its hair and a good appetite.

if you find no room, i shall not sulk. the alley has always been generous to me. still, i confess it would be handsome to see my engine warm your pages.

respectfully untidy,

R.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
