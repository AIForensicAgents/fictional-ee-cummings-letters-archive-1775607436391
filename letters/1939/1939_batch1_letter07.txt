application written to a passport clerk after the morning papers

Date: 1939-09-02
Recipient: Mr. Green, Passport Clerk
Location: Washington, District of Columbia
Year: 1939
Themes: war, borders, identity, paperwork, helplessness

mr. green,
today the newspapers broke like black water over breakfast. a country i have never
held except by map went white as a knocked-out tooth. somewhere a boy named
wladek folds his mother into the cellar and calls it safety; somewhere a train
forgets how to stop.

i have come to your window with pockets full of names. they are all mine and also
borrowed: son, witness, amateur of sunlight, friend-of-a-friend in warsaw.
you ask for proofs (certificates, photographs that do not blink). i bring instead
a heartbeat and a pencil. does that count. can a stamp convince the ocean to make room.

if the ship must leave before my papers learn to walk, i will write a letter so loud
it boards without me. print my name anywhere it will not be erased by boot or rumor.
this is not politics, sir; it is a geography of hands—yours, mine, reaching for a counter
that keeps becoming a border.

i will wait in the hallway where sunlight tries to pass for mercy. please answer
in ink that cannot be unspilled.

respectfully (and otherwise),
h.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
