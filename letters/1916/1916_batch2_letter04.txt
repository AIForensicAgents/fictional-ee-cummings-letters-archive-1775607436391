Yes, commas can waltz if you let them

Date: 1916-02-11
Recipient: Mr. Harridan, Editor of The Lark
Location: New York City, New York
Year: 1916
Themes: art, experiment, city, publishing, modernity

Sir—

Permit me to introduce a small bundle of poems in their traveling coats. They are shy and somewhat impolite; they refuse to sit straight at a table, they dance with cutlery. Yet they tell the truth the way a lamp tells it—by making a little room for seeing and letting the darkness keep its dignities.

You will notice a certain rudeness toward punctuation. I have asked commas to be more than traffic policemen; they are dancers now, and sometimes they trip, but how else shall we learn the floor’s nerve? Capitals, too, have been invited to rest. I find the lowercase to be more companionable, like a cat who admits you to her window after midnight.

New York, meanwhile, applauds itself with hooves of rain and the subways practice their subterranean opera. Painters argue over soup; a violin climbs a fire escape and refuses to come down; even the pigeons are avant-garde, arranging themselves into a question mark that follows me from Union Square to Eighth Street. I assure you the city is not exaggerated in these lines; it is only translated into a dialect of heartbeat.

If these poems are unfit for your columns, please return them unbruised. If they are fit, let us allow the words to go hatless in winter and see if they catch a useful fever. I am not interested in scandal except the scandal of surprise—where a reader suddenly discovers their own mouth open, as if at the dentist, but with joy instead of drills.

I remain obedient to gusts, grateful to margins, and ready to be corrected without being cured.

Respectfully (and otherwise),
A. R.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
