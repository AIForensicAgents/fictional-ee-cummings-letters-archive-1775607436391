on the dignity of small letters (and large hearts)

Date: 1916-03-03
Recipient: Father
Location: New York City, New York
Year: 1916
Themes: family, city life, art, war shadows, humility

Dear Father,

i have arrived among the verticals. the city keeps its collar starched by swallowing light and whistling. between the stone shoulders i carry a satchel of unruly words that refuse to march in straight lines. they are my apprentices and i their bashful broom.

you once taught me that a man should stand up like a doorframe and let the world enter. i have tried. sometimes i am only a hinge, creaking, but sometimes the room appears and my astonishment swings wide.

there is talk of Europe everywhere, of the stern drum that orders young men into smoke. on certain mornings the newspapers wear black gloves. i gaze north up Fifth Avenue and try to forgive the sky for its clean blue.

i wish to say this: some letters must be small because humility is taller than pride. if i write i in the company of giants, it is only to leave more space for breath. and breath, as you know, is the oldest kind of courage.

i keep faith with your pocket watch. i wind it nightly and listen to it argue time back into kindness. when it stops (it will not) i shall ask silence to keep the minutes until we meet.

your son,
A.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
