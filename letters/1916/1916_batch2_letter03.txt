a pocket of sky stitched to my sleeve

Date: 1916-09-03
Recipient: Brother Elias
Location: Near the Somme, France
Year: 1916
Themes: war, brotherhood, memory, ordinary-heroism, hope

Brother—

At noon the road steamed like a kettle set to think. We drove slowly, the ambulance a white thought trying to complete itself. Mud here is democratic; it knows no rank and shakes hands with every boot. In the side mirror a tree argued with the wind and lost its hat.

I have learned the courage of ordinary things: a buckle that refuses to fail, the clean insistence of a knot, the way water does not ask permission to be water. We wash instruments as if they were small, tired birds. The men come in carrying their mornings on their faces; we give them afternoons stitched as carefully as we can.

Last night a shell practiced becoming thunder and forgot to stop. After the shaking, the stars returned to their seats and pretended nothing had happened. I pictured you on the porch at home with your sleeves rolled, humming in the key of tomato vines. That image was a door I could open without waking the house.

There is a boy from Cork who keeps a pebble in his cheek for luck. He spits it into his hand between bandages, wipes it, and replaces it, polite as a gentleman with a visiting card. Do not laugh. It seems to work; he walks between storms as if they were merely curtain tassels brushing his shoulder.

I write this wearing a square of blue the sky forgot. It snagged on a nail near the supply tent and would not go on. I tucked it into my sleeve. If a plane looks down, perhaps it will see a single foolish soldier with a bright cuff and decide to aim for a kinder horizon.

Tell Mother that my boots and I have an understanding. Tell Father his old hammer would be a priest here—everything wants blessing and most of it will accept a good tap instead. As for you, keep the porch light in your pocket, and when I am empty, throw it across the ocean like a rope.

With breath and stubbornness,
Jonah

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
