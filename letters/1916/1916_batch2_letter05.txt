low tide carries a pocketwatch of silence

Date: 1916-11-17
Recipient: Agnes
Location: Portsmouth, New Hampshire
Year: 1916
Themes: sea, waiting, family, faith, friendship

Dear Agnes,

The lighthouse has been practicing its particular alphabet: one blink for patience, two for merely weather, three for the thought that refuses to put on its coat. I walk the shore with my skirt held in a fist, gathering shells and the rumors they overheard from the sea. Each one is a telegram from nowhere: stop. remember. stop. breathe.

Father mends nets in the kitchen where the light is brave. The twine knows his hands and does not argue. He hums the hymn about rescue that we pretended not to love. Outside, the boats cough importantly in their sleep and the gulls polish the wind with their wide knives.

I have not told you about last Sunday’s church because it refuses to be last. Mrs. Hale wept into her gloves and everyone pretended to study the ceiling. We prayed for sailors, for harvests, for the climate of the heart. The minister’s voice was a coat hung neatly on a hook; the draft still came in, but at least it had manners.

There is talk (isn’t there always) of distant battles turning the world into complicated bread. I knead my days with quiet fists. Sometimes the dough rises and sometimes it sulks. On the worst afternoons I wind the old pocketwatch that does not tick; it keeps perfect silence, which can be just the hour one needs.

Come when the frost is decisive. I will make chowder thick enough to forgive almost anything. We will sit on the back steps and let our breath become two small, astonished horses.

Yours from the edge where maps grow polite,
Clara
