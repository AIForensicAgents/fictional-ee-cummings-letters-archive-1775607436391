On the practice of breathing slowly

Date: 1916-07-05
Recipient: Mother
Location: Cambridge, Massachusetts
Year: 1916
Themes: family, home, suffrage, everyday-courage, writing

Dearest Mother,

The lilacs finally conceded and became summer. I bicycled past the Common (bells, children, a dog convinced of its own eloquence) and thought of your recipe for calm—stir once clockwise, once widdershins, then open the window and let the kitchen teach the pie to be wind.

The town is a rumor of newspapers. Every step smells of ink and thunder. We are asked to be brave in a hundred small domestic ways: to save string, to swallow our opinions like aspirin, to practice looking futureward without squinting. Yesterday Mrs. Pritchard pinned a white ribbon to my coat before I could protest; it felt like an extra rib. The parade down Tremont Street was not so much marching as breathing together—with banners for votes, for bread, for the plain dignity of standing upright in one’s own name.

I spend afternoons at the settlement house reading letters aloud to those whose sons have gone north to mills or east to ships. The words burn a little even through the envelopes. When I come home I sew on buttons that pretend to be moons: small, obedient satellites keeping coats from unfastening at the wrong time.

You once told me to count backward when frightened. I do. Ten is your voice in the garden, nine is Father’s hat finding the peg, eight the kettle’s busy applause, seven the window turning street into painting, six my breath, five my breath again. By the time I arrive at one the world has made room for me, like a careful host with a chair that was not there a moment ago.

I have been writing the sort of lines you dislike—untidy, full of startled commas. Still, they keep me companioned while the evening leans its cheek against the Charles and asks, very politely, for a story. I tell it about you shelling peas, the bright green shrapnel in your lap; the river sighs and gives me back a fish made of moonlight.

Do not worry over the cracked teapot. It pours better with a history.

Your loving daughter,
M.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
