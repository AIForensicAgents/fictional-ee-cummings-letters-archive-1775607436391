rain learns my name (and forgets it gently)

Date: 1916-04-22
Recipient: R.
Location: Paris, France
Year: 1916
Themes: love, distance, city, war-shadow, rain

Dear R,

Tonight the rain makes its small grammar on the zinc roofs and I am the sentence it refuses to finish. There is a tram bell—one silver eyelash of sound—then the street exhales and becomes a wet ribbon where a boy chases his hat as if it were a runaway moon.

I walked under the dripping chestnuts until the umbrella became a quiet cathedral. In that hush I practiced your name (softly between teeth the way cream turns to butter) and the air turned warm as a pocket. Do you remember the stair in your building that always sighed? I keep its music in my coat as if it were a handkerchief perfumed with Sunday.

The cafés are full of smoke that draws pictures of saints with dirty haloes. I sit and write unimportant vows to crumbs and teaspoons, a citizen of candled tables. War prowls the edges of conversation—never a face, only a shoulder slipping past a curtain. On the quay a man counted sandbags like prayer beads and a woman stitched an invisible seam through the afternoon; she left behind a thread of sunlight that followed her the whole block like a very good dog.

I have volunteered to lift boxes of bandages, to learn the language of creak and wheel. It is a modest grammar, but kind; a stretcher says please, and a door says thank you. Between trips I stand under the rain and listen to it forget me, then remember, then forget. This is how distance behaves, I think: a rhythm you can hum until it hums you.

If you were here I would give you the city in a paper cone, hot and salted. We would sit on the Pont Neuf and feed it to the river piece by shining piece. Your laughter would be the only arithmetic I trusted.

Write me a small weather from your window. I promise to spend it wisely.

Your sometimes and always,
A.
