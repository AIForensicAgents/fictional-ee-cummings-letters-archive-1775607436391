after the newspapers: address to an unknown soldier

Date: 1916-11-12
Recipient: An Unknown Soldier
Location: Dover, England
Year: 1916
Themes: war, compassion, loss, sea, memory

Dear You,

i stood where the chalk cliffs think aloud and listened to the sea tear its envelopes open. the papers today were a field of names without faces and faces without sleep. somewhere you have become a pronoun, and i keep trying to give it back its shoes.

the harbor gnawed gently at the hulls while gulls practiced being sirens. fog came ashore on slow feet, took my hand, and called itself mercy. i believed it for a minute.

i have nothing to send you but a borrowed heartbeat. pin it inside your coat where the wind keeps its tickets. if you must go forward, take with you this useless advice: when fear closes both fists, open your mouth and say your earliest word. it will be milk or mother or light, which are all the same word in the beginning.

if you do not return, we will continue to address you in present tense until the grammar of the world improves.

from a stranger who insisted on being kin,
A.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
