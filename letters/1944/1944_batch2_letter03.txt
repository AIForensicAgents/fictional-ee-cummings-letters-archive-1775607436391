small prayer sewn into a hem

Date: 1944-03-04
Recipient: Mother
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1944
Themes: labor, family, faith, homefront, factory life, care

dear mother,

the factory whistle lifted the dawn like a lid and the day steamed out, hot with oil and good intentions. i sit at machine sixteen where the needle sings its thin bright river through nylon (we have learned to say nylon instead of silk; progress tastes like pennies and rain). there is a chalk line on the floor where our lunch pails gather like obedient crows.

we stitch parachutes, flowering white rooms a man might open in a wild sky. the foreman says a straight seam is the difference between landing and falling; i keep my hands steady as sunday. when the bolt unwinds it sounds like someone exhaling for the first time. i bury a small prayer in each hem—nobody notices; prayer is weightless and passes inspection.

my friends here are women with shoulders like patience. josie pins the panels fast as laughter. ruby hums a hymn that refuses to end; the tune finds its way into the fabric, so whoever wears it will hear home at 10,000 feet. our pay envelopes are light, but there is a war bond folded inside like a promise that knows its own shoes.

sugar is scarce; i sweeten tea with memory. last night i dreamt our kitchen table grew wings, and we climbed on as if it were the most reasonable chair in the world. we circled the el tracks and came back before the kettle missed us.

if i sometimes come home with thread nesting in my hair, do not scold (you who once braided lightning into my braids, patient, deliberate). i am only learning to tie the world together by small obedient knots.

love to you and to the geraniums that keep summer burning on the sill,

your agnes

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
