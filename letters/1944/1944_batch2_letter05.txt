pacific moon with coconut milk & gunmetal

Date: 1944-11-02
Recipient: Brother James
Location: Aboard U.S. Navy transport, Central Pacific
Year: 1944
Themes: naval life, brotherhood, distance, censorship, nature, longing

dear james,

night has the clean taste of tin and salt. the ship shudders like a horse in sleep; the screws write their round, stubborn sentences and the ocean edits them with its long blue pencil. i have squared your last letter into the size of a lucky stone. during mail call the whole deck is a small cathedral made of torn paper and held breath.

we pass atolls that look like commas someone forgot to finish. stars crowd the rigging until it seems we are sailing through a dry river. on the mess deck, coffee performs its dark miracles and a tin of peaches teaches patience to twelve men. our destination is [censored], our schedule [censored], our purpose sharpened to a single quiet word we never say aloud.

i am learning the alphabet of flags: colors trying to be verbs. at drill the ropes burn the palms into remembrance. the moon climbs the mast and hangs there like a sailor with excellent balance. somewhere off port a flying fish rehearses the rumor of angels.

do not worry over me more than a stove worries over a kettle. i keep a small island of laughter in my shirt pocket; it is the one you sent last winter, folded in plaid. when homesickness bites, i give it a biscuit named after your handwriting and it sleeps.

if i am allowed a souvenir, i will bring you the way the dawn tastes when it spills between gun barrels. if i am allowed a wish, it is the ordinary miracle: a front porch with you on it, september, nobody in a hurry except the kettle.

write when the corn dries to a good whisper. tell me whether the dog still patrols the fence as if the moon were a trespasser.

your brother (salt-slow, sky-loud),

will
