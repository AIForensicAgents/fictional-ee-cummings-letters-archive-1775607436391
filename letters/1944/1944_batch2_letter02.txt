beachglass after the wireless

Date: 1944-06-07
Recipient: Lt. Thomas Avery
Location: Brooklyn, New York
Year: 1944
Themes: D-Day, anxiety, hope, homefront, radio, love

dear lieutenant thomas (permit me the lack of armor; language is metal enough),

yesterday the radio cracked like a shell and inside it: dawn spilling over places we only pronounce by guesswork. the word was invasion. whole kitchens tilted toward that sound, our spoons suspended like thin moons above the coffee. neighbors came to the doorway and we were a small harbor made of mouths and breath.

i spread the map on the table and mother stuck pins in it the way one might sew up a rip—tenderly, urgently, knowing no stitch is the sea. the announcer's voice kept washing in and out, like someone learning to breathe on two continents. under the table our dog slept with his paws running, perhaps to meet you at the edge of some wheat field nobody knows is holy yet.

i have saved a sliver of beachglass from winter (coney island sun distilled into smooth green). if this envelope is not scolded by the censor, it will reach your pocket and teach it to be a window. hold it against the night and you will see our kitchen: the map, the pins, my hand discovering its own steadiness.

do you remember the summer we practiced silence by the ferris wheel, letting the wind say yes for us? i am keeping that yes, polished and warm, for when your boots and my heartbeat arrive at the same doorstep.

hold whatever hand the dark offers—a rope, a word, your own pulse. i am with you at once in all the time zones of bravery. when the papers are full of arrows and clouds, remember: there is a small street in brooklyn where a window refuses to close; it stays open just enough for your name to slip through.

yours (until ordinary light returns, and then still),

eva

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
