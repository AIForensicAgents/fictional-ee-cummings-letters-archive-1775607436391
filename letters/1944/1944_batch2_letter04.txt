nightingale blackout: addressed to the city

Date: 1944-09-12
Recipient: Clara Hart
Location: London, England
Year: 1944
Themes: blackout, resilience, friendship, aerial bombardment, city life, hope

dear clara,

london wears her ink well. the blackout wraps the streets like a careful bandage and every window learns to breathe through cloth. sometimes the siren tests its silver lungs; sometimes it means it. when the new engines buzz low and then go silent, we count—one two three—and listen for the ground to read us a violent postcard. i have become a counter of invisible things: breaths, footsteps, the inches between two strangers sharing a shelter bench as if it were a pew for weather.

the vicar serves tea in chipped cups and the shelter smells of wool and bravery. a child with chalk draws a door on the platform wall and opens it into a field; we step through just long enough to remember the grammar of birds. above, whole streets practice not-flinching. even the sparrows carry soot like a minor title.

i cycle over the bridge at dawn when the city forgets to perform. fog and river decide they are the same soft stranger. a milk cart passes (the horse's hooves a four-beat lullaby) and for a moment the century behaves. then a distant thud revises us and we remember to be alive on purpose.

i think of you at your desk in oxford, or plymouth, or wherever the envelope will find you—your thin, quick ink that refuses to surrender adjectives. write me a word for this courage that happens when no one is looking. i will pin it to my coat before the all-clear yawns its tired green.

if the city is a patient, we are the stubborn pulse. if the sky is a mouth, we are not its meal. come when you can; the moon keeps your seat in the ruins of st. botolph's choir, where someone left a single orange like an argument for sweetness.

affection always,

l.
