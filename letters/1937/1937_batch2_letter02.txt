wind with a mouth full of wheat

Date: 1937-04-03
Recipient: Sister Ruth
Location: Dodge City, Kansas
Year: 1937
Themes: family, resilience, dust, home, hope

dear Ruth,

i am writing with dust in my teeth and a sun the color of brass doorknobs. these last days the fields keep walking into town, patient as mules. we hang wet sheets over the windows and pretend they are sea—listen, the wind tries to learn our lullabies through the cloth and coughs.

yesterday the bank man came with a hat full of drought and said numbers like dry beans. i told him beans still make a congregation when you shake the jar. he didn’t laugh, but the radio did—a gust of swing leapt out and jittered the parlor. for a minute the floorboards remembered dancing, and Ma smiled the way flour smiles when it’s about to become bread.

how is your kitchen? do you keep the coffee tin in the icebox like a tiny winter? i found a violet skulking in the ditch (it wore its purple like a secret). pressed it in the almanac—will send once the postman stops being a small brown storm.

we are fine enough to write this, which is to say: not done. at night i oil the plow and speak to it softly; a tool is a stubborn hymn and will answer if you ask right. tell the boys their kites can wait (the sky is on loan), and keep the marbles in a jar so blue can remember itself.

hold fast, as if hands were ropes were vines were grandmothers.

always,

eli
