Cornfield Elegy (with sparrows)

Date: 1946-09-03
Recipient: Mother
Location: Omaha, Nebraska
Year: 1946
Themes: grief, harvest, family, rural life, acceptance

dear mother,

i am writing from the edge of the field where the corn stands like a congregation of tall, whispering listeners. september has put its gold hands on everything. there is a breeze with dirt on its knees. your pie cooled on the sill smelling like a story someone forgot to finish.

i mended the gate this morning—two nails, an apology, and a swear the wind will forgive. the scarecrow leans the way old men lean when they remember dances. i gave him a hat with a hole where sky can pass through; he is delighted with weather.

you asked if grief changes the color of milk. yes and no. the pail reflects absence like a lake reflects a cloud; you cannot drink a cloud but still it follows your throat. i think father is now part of the arithmetic of insects. last night a cricket rehearsed a sermon beneath the porch and i believed every word i could not understand.

on the radio a voice thanked us for our patience. somewhere far away a city learns to be glad without sirens. here the tomatoes redden as if blushing when we look. i saved the first two for you, small and ridiculous with pride.

i know you dislike sentimental weather, but autumn insists. let it. stack the wood as if letters in a name. promise to use the good kettle on cool nights. when frost arrives it will knock politely.

take the blue shawl. take my stubbornness, fold it by the window.

your daughter who listens for sparrows,

marian

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
