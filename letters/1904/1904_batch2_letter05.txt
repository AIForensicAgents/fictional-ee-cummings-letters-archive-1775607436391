magnolia & the ink-thin moon

Date: 1904-06-19
Recipient: Evelyn Moore
Location: Savannah, Georgia
Year: 1904
Themes: independence, women’s work, southern summer, choice, ambition, friendship, home

dear evelyn,

the night is a porch that keeps rocking long after anyone has left it. magnolia breath clings to my hair as if intending marriage, and the mosquitoes write hymns upon my ankles, uninvited clerks of summer. i have said no to mr. talbott, which is to say yes to a room with a window that looks the way i do when i am most myself.

mother counted my refusal like a handful of buttons—this one sensible, this one lost. she worries for my future (a small dowry, a sewing machine, a temper that trips its own feet). i told her that i will make a shop of mornings: hems, cuffs, a collar like a fresh page. women will arrive with their tired dresses and leave wearing a different sentence.

you, who understand the courage of thread, know why i could not promise my name to a door that opens inward only. the city whispers spendthrift rumors—storms brewing in the sea, a church raising money for new stained glass, a boy who climbed a pine to see beyond the year. i admire him; perhaps i have done the same, only with scissors.

send me the pattern for that clever sleeve you invented, the one that sits on the shoulder like a polite bird. also send news of your laugh. i require it, as sailors require a star they can point to and say home.

the moon is a nib just dipped; it writes a silver address upon the river. i will answer, tomorrow, with work. the good kind.

ever your cousin & confederate in hopeful defiance,

margaret
