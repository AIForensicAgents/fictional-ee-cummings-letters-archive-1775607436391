To the Editor (a quiet mutiny)

Date: 1900-11-03
Recipient: Mr. Percival Dent, Editor of The Evening Star
Location: Providence, Rhode Island
Year: 1900
Themes: art, rebellion, newspapers, voice, modernity

Sir,
permit a letter that refuses the corset of straight lines. I have set type till midnight when the town unclenches, and know by touch the grammar of lead. It occurs to me that our paper, dutiful as a clerk, could dare to breathe.

Imagine a column that walks instead of marches. In it, days would arrive unruly and honest: a coal boy whistling broken constellations; a widow who buys two apples, one for her pocket; the mayor asleep in a sermon he commissioned. The city keeps its own diary in the gutter and on the stoop; we need only to take dictation.

I submit, for your disapproval and secret delight, small paragraphs with knees and elbows. Let them climb the page and sometimes fall. Give them room to leave fingerprints. And when they forget their hats in our office, we will pretend not to notice, for fashion is an enemy of truth, and truth rarely dresses for company.

If this is insurrection, it is the polite kind, with ink under its fingernails. Should you wish a sample, I will send the first weather that fits in an envelope. It will not behave. That is the point.

With professional affection,
J. M. Kettle
