ink bones

Date: 1900-09-05
Recipient: Mrs. Eleanor Pike
Location: St. Louis, Missouri
Year: 1900
Themes: grief, friendship, memory, ritual, resilience

Dear Eleanor,

your letter reached me in a quiet hour when the lamp had decided the room would be a small country ruled by its warm thumbprint of light. you asked how grief can be carried without breaking the back. i think we do not carry it. we invite it to walk beside us and it, surprised by courtesy, behaves sometimes like a companion and sometimes like weather.

i have put his spectacles on my desk. they hold the shape of his looking, though not his eyes. maybe we keep such things the way a tree keeps last year's nest, not from use but from the principle of shelter.

church bells tell me the exact weight of each afternoon and i am neither comforted nor scolded. i cook soup. i polish the spoons. i write his name until the letters have bones.

you may bring me a little laughter when you come. not the loud kind that insists but the gentle one that remembers tears and chooses them anyway.

until then, let us be undramatic as bread and just as necessary.

affectionately,

Clara

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
