skyletter from a barn roof

Date: 1929-07-21
Recipient: My Father
Location: Amherst, Massachusetts
Year: 1929
Themes: family, labor, summer, rural, reconciliation

father—
i have climbed the barn again to nail the heat in place. it keeps slipping.
august rehearses its thunder behind the hills; the swallows write their quick blue
sentences and refuse to stop for grammar.

i know you prefer plain talk: the corn is tall as a promise no man should make,
the roof leaks only where i cannot reach, and i have put by enough courage to
argue with the moon if it meddles in our haying.

you always said a hammer is a word in the right hand. today mine said hush to a
crooked shingle and it listened. perhaps i am learning: not loudness, but the slow
agreement between palm and nail.

i remember your back bent over winter, your breath cloud by cloud explaining
fences to snow. i think i wanted to be the snow—brief, pure, forgiven. instead i am
this roof, loud with sun, willing to hold.

when evening fits over us like a clean shirt, come up here and sit. we'll watch the
cows rewrite the field with their patient mouths. you'll tell me of the year you
nearly left, and i will answer with the year i almost stayed.

your son, barefooted and taller by a splinter,
aj

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
