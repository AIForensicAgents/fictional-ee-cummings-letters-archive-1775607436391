Paint Box Prayer

Date: 1929-05-03
Recipient: Father
Location: Montparnasse, Paris
Year: 1929
Themes: art, family, becoming, city abroad

Father,

Please accept this clumsy envelope of color. I have been living in an attic the size of a held breath; the window is a stubborn eyelid opening only to shin-bone rooftops and a cathedral that rehearses thunder daily. Here, light takes the long way through dust and becomes holy by accident. I have discovered that vermilion is a verb and that canvas, when you ask politely, grows weather.

You did not send me to Paris to become a vagabond of brushstrokes. You sent me to learn patience, proportion, bread. I am trying. Every morning i descend the crooked spine of the building, exchange bonjours with the woman who sells onions and promises to die only after she has finished counting them, and i buy a heel of yesterday that i butter with today. The studio has no stove; everything cooks by faith and turpentine.

Do you remember your hands lifting the barn’s broken hinge and making it honest again? I have that gesture in me like a pocket knife. When the model turns her cheek and the afternoon falls off it, i open that knife in the air and carving begins—soon there are planes, then wind, then a shore that didn’t exist before but is crowded with our old silences.

At night we argue in three languages about whether art should lean against the future or kiss the present. The saxophone downstairs ruins and saves us alternately. Someone reads aloud a poem that bends the word yes until it becomes a door; someone else lights a cigarette from a candle and the room believes for one second in miracles that are mostly smoke. I think of your workshop, the square-headed nails, the sermons of walnut and oil, and i am less lost.

If i am disobedient, it is only to gravity. Forgive the unsteady line. I am sending with this letter a sketch of the bridge at dawn (blue devoured by milk). It doesn’t look like the bridge, but it looks like the hour, which is all i can promise: i will keep painting the hours until they resemble themselves and, someday, us.

Your son (wiping sky from his fingers),
L.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
