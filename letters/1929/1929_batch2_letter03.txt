Thunderhead and Apricot Light

Date: 1929-07-19
Recipient: Mrs. Inez Calder
Location: Santa Fe, New Mexico
Year: 1929
Themes: independence, landscape, mentorship, self-reliance

Dear Mrs. Calder,

If you could see the afternoon unbuttoning itself over the mesa, you would forgive me everything i have neglected to be. The air here walks around without a hat. We live in a casita the color of dried river, where doors are short and silence is tall; in the courtyard a lizard performs its daily applause and a blue bottle winks sober wisdom at the sun. I have learned to begin my letters in the shade and end them in the thunder.

I am told by those who own clocks that summer is half-spent, but time out here refuses such arithmetic. A storm arrives as a polite rumor, becomes a scandal, and leaves its gossip in the arroyo. Meanwhile, apricots fall with the plop of satisfied punctuation. I eat them greedily, lips learning the honest grammar of juice, and then i paint with the pit’s memory.

You worried i was too slight for solitude—your word was tender, mine is truer: hunger. I was starving among full rooms. Here, the edges are honest. Yesterday i bicycled three miles of uphill breath. At the crest i found a cross of old wood shouldering the sky. The wind advised me to let it carry my smaller troubles and i tried. I tied to it a thread from my skirt (blue as a forgiven bruise) and came home lighter by exactly one fear.

A man at the market promised rain for a nickel and laughter for free. I paid both. The rain arrived in a tin cup and we shared it, four strangers and an old dog. We corrected each other’s names gently until we had invented a family long enough to finish drinking.

Please write me of your garden: has the foxglove learned its manners; has the bee returned with news; are the tomatoes still practicing being moons? Send your disapproval if you must, but tuck in also a recipe for shade. I will keep the envelope on my nightstand to remind me that the world includes you—that fierce-browed mercy of a woman who taught me that a pot should never be filled to its brim because water likes room to remember the sea.

Your errant pupil (full of dust, singing anyway),
R.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
