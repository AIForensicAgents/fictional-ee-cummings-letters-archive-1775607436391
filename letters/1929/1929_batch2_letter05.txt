Midwinter, with Matches

Date: 1929-12-24
Recipient: Clara
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1929
Themes: winter, hope, work, music, letters

Dear Clara,

Christmas Eve has its hand over the city’s mouth; what gets out is mostly steam and carols practiced by radiators. The lake has disguised itself as a serious conversation. I walked past the church where the choir’s breath made small white amendments to the hymns, and i thought of your throat—how it turns simple syllables into a kind of geography, a place to live.

I have been working late at the press. Ink under the fingernails is a stubborn kind of biography. We printed a run of small posters for a dance hall on Wabash where the trumpet writes its manifesto nightly. The foreman pretends not to like music, but i saw him polishing the commas of the announcement as if punctuation could swing.

Money is thin as the ice on the windows. All the same, i bought a box of matches with extravagant red heads and have been striking them one by one just to watch the room remember the sun. It feels like a rehearsal for something kinder. The kettle approves with a throat-clearing. I have folded a paper star for you (clumsy corners, earnest angles) and pinned it to the cord of the bare bulb: now we have our own small winter constellation.

I wish you could be here to help the tea believe in warmth. We would practice the old game: name five things the night cannot take. I would say: your laughter; the way snow fixes a street so the past can walk on it; the stubborn mercy of bread; a letter that arrives out of breath; and the blush of a match, briefly a planet.

If you can, write me the names of the trains that still run after midnight. I keep thinking of departures as pocket-sized salvations. If you cannot come soon, send a word and i will tie it to my wrist with twine. Tomorrow i mean to carry a small bowl of oranges to Mrs. Kapur upstairs; she lost her husband in spring but keeps finding him in the coat sleeves of winter. We must go on like that: discovering what we did not lose.

With the wide cold outside and a little fire in my mouth for you,
H.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
