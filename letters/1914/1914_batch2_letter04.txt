letter for the window at midnight

Date: 1914-09-04
Recipient: Ruth, my sister
Location: Le Havre, France
Year: 1914
Themes: war, night, tenderness, exile, hope

Ruth,—

midnight here has the slow, bruised breath of someone carrying too many names. the quay keeps counting footsteps and loses track, then begins again. there are trains made almost entirely of faces; the luggage is mostly silence. i have learned that bread tastes different when the town is listening for news.

i spend my days gathering small usefulness: directions to water, the comfort of a chair, the grammar of nods. i learned the word ration—how it feels like a fence in the mouth. but there is also a child with a tin whistle who refuses all fences and invents, at intervals, a parade of two.

if worry wakes you, set it politely on the edge of the bed and let it watch you sleep. i keep a candle in a teacup for company; its flame practices being brave by being small. the harbor coughs, the moon buttons her coat, and somewhere a clock remembers its duty.

i am safe enough to write and foolish enough to hope. tell mother i have found kindness in odd corners, and that i am learning to fold my fear the way she folded linens—firmly, with hands that believe in drawers.

your brother,
A.
