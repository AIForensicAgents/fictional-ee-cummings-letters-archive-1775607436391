the sudden geography of spring

Date: 1914-03-15
Recipient: Lillian (dear L—)
Location: Cambridge, Massachusetts
Year: 1914
Themes: spring, longing, youth, letters, river

dear L—

the river this morning unbuttoned a little and let the sky breathe through it. somewhere between the bridge and the bakery a boy whistled an argument with a sparrow and lost (sweetly). i walked with my coat unfastened, as if thoughts needed wind to keep from falling asleep. if i had a pocket wide enough i would keep there the clatter of spoons, the shy cough of horses, the small steam of people waiting for the post.

your last note arrived folded like a gull—white with the small thunder of your hand. i have read it until the corners grew warm. each sentence of yours steps softly and yet the pavement remembers its foot. i am practicing how not to hurry. the mirror tries to teach me patience (it fails, but charmingly), and i am learning to make sentences that stand on tiptoe to see whether the sun has grown taller than yesterday.

promise me you will walk slowly past the elms at dusk so the evening may catch you and remember your name. and if you should hear my unspoken word leaning against the lamplight, do not scold it; lend it your glove and send it home.

with a hand that has almost learned patience,
—A.
