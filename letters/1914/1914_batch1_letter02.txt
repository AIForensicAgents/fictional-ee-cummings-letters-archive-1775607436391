Small Revolutions on MacDougal Street

Date: 1914-04-10
Recipient: Aunt Eleanor
Location: Greenwich Village, New York
Year: 1914
Themes: bohemian life, family, art, urbanity, hope

dearest Aunt,
forgive the ink which keeps dancing. a violinist in the next building practices scales so bravely that the letters cannot help but waltz.

i have found a room with a window grand enough to let in the gossip of all the spring. the street below is a parade of hats and contradictions. a painter invited me to sit for a study and then forgot me entirely, which was perfect; i watched his canvases try to invent an orange that never existed before.

in the cafés, people say the future as if it were a small bird that might land on the table if we hold our cups very still. some afternoons we do not speak at all. we just let our boots learn the dictionary of rain. the other day a dancer taught me how to fold silence into a pocket handkerchief and unfold it later when the room is too crowded.

you asked will i eat. i will (eventually). the baker on the corner dusts his loaves with a sweetness that forgives my lateness for supper. i am not lost, only rerouted by a light i do not yet understand.

i send you the streetlamps in their hand-me-down dusk, the small revolutions of pigeons, and the certainty that tomorrow has freckles. please write soon of lilacs and your patient roses.

your wayward but honest,
—

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
