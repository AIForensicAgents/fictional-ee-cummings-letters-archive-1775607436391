Invoice for an invisible painting

Date: 1932-10-27
Recipient: Mr. Harkness, Gallery Owner
Location: Greenwich Village, New York, NY
Year: 1932
Themes: art, commerce, imagination, modernity, playfulness

Mr. Harkness,

please find enclosed the invoice for my latest work, which is a painting that refuses to weigh more than breath. its subject is a door slightly ajar on an afternoon that has learned how to be quiet. the colors are convinced they exist, and I have decided not to contradict them.

the canvas began as a straight line walking home; at the corner it discovered rain. subsequently the line became two lines, then a question, then a window in a hat. I intervened only to dip three moments in a little jar of astonishment and release them at eye level.

viewing instructions: stand near the wall and remember something you have never owned. close one eye so the picture can step forward without embarrassment. count to almost. open your mouth as if tasting a letter you have not yet mailed. you will notice the air becoming aware of its posture.

as to price, I suggest five days of listening and two good chairs. if unavoidable, we can translate this into money, but I warn you that money tends to misunderstand what it touches.

respectfully disobedient,

N.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
