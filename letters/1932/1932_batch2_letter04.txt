after the parade that never came

Date: 1932-11-09
Recipient: Mother
Location: Albuquerque, New Mexico
Year: 1932
Themes: family, radio era, southwest, work, hope

dear mother—

the radio last night rustled like a paper moth and all the voices lived inside its small warm cave. they kept talking about a country turning its pocket inside out to see if any stars fell through. even the moon seemed to lean closer, as if counting.

this morning the streets were ordinary again: dust arguing softly with the wind, chile ristras blazing from porches like red certainty. i walked to the filling station where i am the evening man. gasoline shines like dangerous honey in the glass jar. customers arrive with their lives strapped to fenders—trunks, quilts, a piano key or two that escaped.

i have learned the smell of engines that are nearly prayers. sometimes a child watches from a backseat and waves a hand that does not yet understand goodbye. i wave back with both of mine.

your handkerchief (the lace one that looks like snow remembering) rides in my breast pocket. when the day caves in at the edges, i unfold it to prove softness still occurs. i have not given up on sleep or peaches or the way you say my name as if it were a chair i could sit in.

if promises are coins, we will spend them carefully. if hope is a road, we will walk it with the heels of our hands when our feet get tired. i send a small tin of piñon nuts; toast them until the kitchen invents a forest.

i will be home for christmas unless the desert swallows time again. tell the cat i remember his foolish face. tell yourself i am well, since i am practicing.

your son,
paul

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
