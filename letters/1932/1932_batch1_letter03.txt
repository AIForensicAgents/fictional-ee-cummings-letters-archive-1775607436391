On the matter of bread and bells

Date: 1932-03-16
Recipient: Mother
Location: Boston, MA
Year: 1932
Themes: family, domestic, hope, poverty, ritual

Mother,

this noon I learned the precise weight of a loaf: it is equal to three coins and the promise not to be afraid before supper. the baker’s apron was a snowfield where small avalanches had forgotten to fall. he wrapped the bread in paper that sounded like Sunday dresses.

the streets were damp with church bells, a slow silver rain. boys skipped through puddles carrying something called tomorrow in tin pails; it splashed their knees, but they did not spill it. I wanted to tell you about the woman who swept her stoop as if she were erasing a sadness from the alphabet.

I am careful with the stove and my shadow and the last onion. there is a kind of thrift that feels like prayer; the kettle lifts its sweet throat and suddenly the cheap tea is holy. I set your recipe on the sill where the weak sun could visit it and the page curled into a smile.

when I do not know what to do, I polish the knob of the front door until it shines a small map: in it I see our kitchen, your hands, and the way you open jars without asking permission from the lid. Mother, keep the knitting near the window; the afternoon light has been missing you.

your child (still learning how to be both brave and gentle),

E.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
