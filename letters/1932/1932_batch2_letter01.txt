telephone moons & other small braveries

Date: 1932-02-11
Recipient: Iris Davenport
Location: New York City, New York
Year: 1932
Themes: urban winter, longing, everyday magic, intimacy, hope

dear iris—

tonight the city buttons its gray coat and pretends not to shiver. the elevated clatters its tin laughter, and my breath learns geometry against the glass. i kept your postcard folded in the left pocket; it warms there like a little stove the size of a word.

a clerk with winter eyebrows counted nickels into a paper cup. we were all rich with steam and soup at the automat, every spoon a comet in the orbit of a hungry hand. i thought of you as the door sighed open, and the night came in like a rumor with snow on its lapel.

your name climbs stairwells only i can see. some nights the telephone moons ring above rooftops and the wires answer in bright whispers. if we meet on lexington, bring your quietest smile; i will teach it to dance upon its own shadow. we will invent an afternoon so delicate even the pigeons will tiptoe.

what i mean is (and do not laugh unless it helps you breathe) the city grows taller when i remember the way you looked at a window and made it a lake. when the snow begins, every street mispronounces you, yet means you all the same.

sleep, iris. the radiators murmur their iron lullabies. i am here, putting courage in a cup to keep for later. goodnight to you, who teach the dark to blink.

always,
-m

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
