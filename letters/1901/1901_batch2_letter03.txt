Monsoon Letter, Concerning Rain and Remembering

Date: 1901-08-29
Recipient: Mother
Location: Calcutta
Year: 1901
Themes: homesickness, rain, work, letters, earth

Dear Mother,

The rain has been speaking for three days without punctuation. Every street forgets its shoes and walks barefoot; every gutter invents a river and believes it. From my room I can see the banyan at the corner lowering its lacework of roots like a grandmother hemming the air. The fan above me ticks a patient metronome for the heat—one, two, three—and yet the afternoons go on inventing longer hours.

In the office the ledgers swell like bread. Ink blooms where it ought to obey. Mr. Sloan pretends not to notice because his patience is a dry umbrella, but we all grow a little waterlogged and gentle, as if argument could not keep a fire. On the way home I pass the flower-sellers; their marigolds are small suns stitched together, a courage you can carry. I think you would approve of such thrift.

Do not imagine me sorrowful beyond repair. Please think instead of the ferry I take at dusk, two oars knocking the river's memory awake. The boatman has a way of laughing that lifts the boat an inch. People speak here in several musics at once; I do not understand them all, but their kindness is legible. A boy pressed a mango into my hand and ran away; I could not thank him quickly enough, so I ate slowly and sent the thanks backward through time.

I try to keep the Sabbath as you taught me, not by refusing work but by remembering the world's first breath. I put your last letter on the table and read it until the page warms. Your words smell faintly of soap and attic. If your knee troubles you, tie a blue ribbon just above it; the old woman at the corner insists this arrests the moon's mischief. I am willing to believe her.

Your son, made practical by rain and made foolish by love,

Thomas
