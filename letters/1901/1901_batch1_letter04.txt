The clock learns to breathe

Date: 1901-04-09
Recipient: Father
Location: Cleveland, Ohio
Year: 1901
Themes: family, work, art, courage, industrial

Father,

The foundry coughs its black confetti into noon and the river pretends not to notice. I have kept your ledgers in neat rows, soldiers in their barracks, numbers polishing their boots. Yet the hours seem to forget me unless I breathe for them.

Forgive this small rebellion: I have rented a room with a north window and a table wide as a patient. On it I keep paper, unruly as grass, and a pen that insists upon thunder though it makes only scratches. I am trying to learn the contours of silence by tracing it repeatedly.

You taught me that a tool earns its meaning by use. Then let me use this trembling instrument until it knows my hand, and afterward knows my heart. If I fail, I will return to the precision of sums and the tidy anthem of columns. But tonight the clock in my room learns to breathe, and I learn with it.

Do not think me ungrateful. Your discipline has built the bridge I now hazard to cross. Below it runs a risky water whose name is mine.

Your son, with ink-stains like small honest wounds,
M.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
