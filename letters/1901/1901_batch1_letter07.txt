Late summer telegram of silence

Date: 1901-08-12
Recipient: Mrs. Beatrice Harrow
Location: Savannah, Georgia
Year: 1901
Themes: grief, friendship, summer, silence, consolation

Dear Beatrice,

The heat lies down like a tame lion and dreams among the roots. Spanish moss drips its slow handwriting from every branch; even the squares seem to read it and nod. Cicadas stitch a bright racket through the noon, and somewhere a boy runs with a kite made of yesterday's newspaper, forgiveness fluttering behind him.

I took the telegram from the wire-house and carried it folded, small and white, a bright tooth in my pocket. Its words were few and exact and did not know your garden or your brave apparel of grief. I offered them water. They would not drink.

He has gone, and the world will keep trying to go on. Between those two facts there is a room where you may sit as long as you need. I will sit on the step outside and keep the sun from interfering, and the neighbors from improving upon sorrow with advice.

When evening relieves us, bring out the peaches. We will bruise them with our thumbs and let sweetness make a brief faith. Should you wish to speak his name, I will hold the bowl. Should you wish to be silent, I will listen exactly.

Your friend and quiet conspirator,
L.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
