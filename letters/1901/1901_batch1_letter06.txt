Railway with swallows

Date: 1901-06-28
Recipient: Mr. Lionel Graves
Location: York, England
Year: 1901
Themes: travel, writing, nature, solitude, cathedral

Dear Mr. Graves,

The rails arrive with their usual impatience, then depart having told us everything and nothing. Swallows embroider the station roof in a quick black cursive I cannot quite translate. I have been traveling toward a sentence all month; it keeps moving one station ahead.

I visited the Minster at the good hour when stained glass rehearses its miracles against the stone. A verger dusted a pew with the mood of a metronome. I sat and confessed to the air that I am building a book from weather and doors and the private name of rain.

You asked for pages. I enclose four, their corners softened by my coat, their margins curious. Do not judge them sternly. They are young instruments, and the world is large. If any phrase finds your favor, lend it a roof and a little coal for winter; it will earn its keep.

The countryside performs an undress of hedges and sheep. Even the telegraph poles speak in quick square syllables. I confess to loneliness, but it is a useful kind, like an empty teacup asking to be filled with something true.

Yours in motion and in hope,
E.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
