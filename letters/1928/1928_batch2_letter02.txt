for my sister, under an umbrella of lilacs

Date: 1928-05-17
Recipient: Clara
Location: Paris, Rue de l’Odéon
Year: 1928
Themes: spring, sibling, exile, art, language

my dearest Clara,

paris is a book left open in the rain and somehow the words do not wash away. i breakfast on a crust and a mistake in grammar, and both are delicious. the woman at the corner café says bonjour as if she were winding a small silver watch; i answer with my best pocket of vowels, which spill shyly onto the saucer and make the spoon blush.

i carry spring like a scandal in my coat. lilacs lean out of courtyards, whispering purple rumors, and the river keeps pretending not to listen while listening. today a boy launched a paper boat that believed it was a cathedral; we all applauded when it docked inside the sun.

do not worry about my purse (it is mostly full of promises). the landlord accepts my rent in poems and sweeping; you know how i adore brooms that dance. last night i sat with two painters who argued whether blue is a verb. i said yes, because you have always been able to blue a room by simply entering it. they asked who you were. i said the sister who taught me thunder is just weather learning to play the drum.

write me about Ohio's green heart—are the porches yawning yet? does mother still scold the roses for immodesty? tell father i have learned to tie my courage with a sailor's knot; sometimes it slips but the bow looks brave.

i suppose you want news of my heart. it wears a beret and walks carefully over cobbles. it drinks too much coffee and refuses to come home before dawn. when the bells begin inventing morning on the hill, my heart sits down in the street and applauds. i let it. we are practicing a new politeness toward ourselves.

kiss the baby for me, that magnificent moon-chewer. and if you see a lilac by the fence, ask it quietly to save one fragrance for my return.

your forever tumbledown,

R.
