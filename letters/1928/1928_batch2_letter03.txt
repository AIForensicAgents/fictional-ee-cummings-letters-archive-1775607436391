to the River (addressed to Mother)

Date: 1928-07-29
Recipient: Mother
Location: Aboard the Missouri Pacific, near Hannibal
Year: 1928
Themes: family, memory, travel, river, homecoming

Mother,

i am writing with my elbow steady on a suitcase and my eyes surprised by the way a river keeps saying yes. we are traveling beside its shoulder, cornfields combed back neat as a boy's hair, and the telegraph poles stepping, stepping, like tall wooden birds. the window is a square of heat that refuses to blink; it is July enough to iron yesterday flat.

when we passed the quarry i smelled my childhood (chalk and daring). do you remember how i used to hide a slice of cake in the pocket of the maple and swear it tasted better for the birds' gossip? you would come out with your hands full of soap and sunlight and find me sticky with the future.

the porter hums a hymn that keeps mending the air. a woman across the aisle is traveling with a jar of lightning bugs and a hat that could explain everything. each time the rails cry, i think of father standing on the porch with his watch, not because of time, but because of punctual hope.

i wanted to tell you: sometimes the world arrives all at once. today it is a boy in the next car tapping out Morse upon the luggage rack, his message says hello to whoever will admit to being whoever. i answered by closing my eyes and thinking of your kitchen, the one clean square of cool on the table, and how you would put my hot forehead there and say drink some water, as if water were a gentle law.

we will stop soon by a town whose name i am not ready to forgive. i will send this letter from its post office anyway. forgive me for the small untruths i have mailed you before: i am well, i am brave, i am not afraid. today i am well enough to be afraid, and that feels like the truer courage.

when i come home, save me a chair by the back steps. let the river into the pitcher, and i will drink until the long grass remembers my knees.

your son,

T.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
