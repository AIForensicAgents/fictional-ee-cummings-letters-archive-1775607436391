Concerning the Smallness of Stars (and Rent)

Date: 1912-09-03
Recipient: Mr. Avery Dunn, Landlord
Location: New York City, New York
Year: 1912
Themes: city, poverty, humor, stars, negotiation

Sir—

Permit a tenant to submit the following astronomical observation: there are three coins in my pocket (two minor planets and perhaps a meteor). They circle my need with admirable diligence but refuse to eclipse it. The month has been an alleyway of unprofitable sunsets; the poems I sold were paid in applause, which, though generous, is a currency prone to vanishing at breakfast.

I offer you, instead, a ledger of good faith: 1) a broomed floor; even the corners salute you. 2) silence after eleven, the piano muzzled like a polite wolf. 3) a promise, which is heavier than it appears—by Thursday night the moon will grow more certain and so shall I; your due in full, with an apologetic shine.

If you must scowl, do it handsomely so I may learn from a master. I will stand still and accept your weather. But know (please) that I am not negligent, only temporarily under the staircase of fortune, listening for her late feet. This city breaks me into useful pieces; I am trying to arrange them into rent.

With respectful haste,
J. Calder (third floor, south window that leaks when it thinks no one is looking)

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
