Midnight Telegram That Became a Prayer

Date: 1912-12-22
Recipient: Ruth Miriam Elson
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1912
Themes: illness, friendship, winter, prayer, trains

Dear Ruth,

Your telegram arrived like a clipped breath: FEVER PERSISTS STOP COME IF ABLE STOP. I held those metal syllables and heard only the stubborn drum of your name beneath them. I tried to answer with equal brevity and failed; the night kept growing new rooms for my worry and they all required words.

Listen: the station’s iron throat keeps clearing itself; trains arrive coated in other people’s distances. Snow invents a softer arithmetic upon everything, even grief. I want to bring you its pale addition—how each flake quietly insists: still, still, still. If you sleep, let it be under that arithmetic. If you cannot, I will sit with you in the bright hiss of the kettle, counting the blinks of the stove’s little red eye until it is morning somewhere inside us.

Tonight is the long hinge of the year; darkness lingers, but the hinge means a door. I will be on the first train; if I am later than hope, take this stubborn letter as a proxy hand. Close your eyes and feel the practiced clumsiness of my comfort. Let it be enough until I lay my coat over your cold toes and call you by the nickname you said I invented (and which, in truth, invented me).

Until the whistle becomes a key,
E.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
