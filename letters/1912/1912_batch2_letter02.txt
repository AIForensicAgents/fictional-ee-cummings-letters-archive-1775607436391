To My Father, With Streetcar Bells

Date: 1912-04-05
Recipient: Mr. Nathaniel Brooks (Father)
Location: Boston, Massachusetts
Year: 1912
Themes: family, ambition, city, duty, art

Dear Father,

The streetcar clangs its obstinate hymn and the whole city seems to bow and rise with each bell. I have tried to be faithful to the sensible music you taught me—coins stacked solemnly, shirtsleeves in honest daylight—but even the ledger’s patient lines tilt, sometimes, into skylines. And there, somewhere above the soot and punctual roofs, something with wings waits (it is not the devil; it is simply a question).

You ask what I do so late with the lamp crowding my shadow. I listen for that winged question and answer with a stub of pencil. The answers are not profitable, perhaps, but they have a way of mending me in places a paycheck cannot find. You wrote that a man should be dependable as the morning roll. I agree. Only—allow me to be a bread you have not yet tasted: crust a little strange, seed scattered, but warm when broken.

I will visit Sunday, bringing oranges, and you may scold my ink-stained cuffs. Scold them kindly. Even my stubbornness speaks in your voice, which is to say it hopes to be decent. If I am to build anything in this unruly century, let it be a door that opens both ways: duty and dream, handshake and sky.

Your devoted son,
Thomas

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
