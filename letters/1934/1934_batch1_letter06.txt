On the price of peaches

Date: 1934-08-03
Recipient: Mr. Whitlow
Location: Asheville, North Carolina
Year: 1934
Themes: poverty, dignity, negotiation, work

Dear Mr. Whitlow,
there are three peaches on the sill this morning and their patience is a sermon. they wait for the soft ache of ripeness the way rent waits for payday. may i be a day late.

you know i make shirts at Mrs. Deal’s—buttonholes steady as prayer, seams straighter than the rumor of me. yesterday she paid me in cash and apricots of coin; i sent half to my sister whose baby coughs at night like a small engine that can’t start. the rest went honest places: bread, soap, a spool of blue thread to mend the sky of my one good dress.

i will bring you the balance on Monday. between now and then i will hum, work, and pretend the porch is a lake. i will not drink. i will not quit. i will keep the plants from fainting by telling them good jokes and offering them the dregs of my tea.

your patience would not be charity but an excellent investment in August, which has a way of paying interest in peaches, and in people who do not stay small when the world tries to fold them.

respectfully (and with ink-stained fingers),
June Avery, upstairs front

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
