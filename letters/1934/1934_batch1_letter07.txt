Five-cent serenade

Date: 1934-10-11
Recipient: Sam
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1934
Themes: city, labor, brotherhood, music

Sam,
the wind here practices trumpet scales around every corner, and the elevated trains keep time like a stubborn drummer. i whistle back and buy a paper from the kid with the newsprint eyebrows—he winks as if the headlines were a private joke we’re almost brave enough to laugh at.

your boots would like this town. pavements are a kind of argument you can win by walking. i sweep at the warehouse till noon, haul boxes of shoes whose laces dream of feet. at lunch we pile into the alley and eat from wax paper, the sun pouring its soup over all of us equally.

the talk is of raises that can’t lift, men with hands like doorknobs, a strike that may or may not learn to spell itself. i keep listening for your voice in the factory bell and in the radio at night when a singer leans out of the speaker to pat our heads.

do not stay angry with me for leaving. i brought the harmonica you taught me to bend. sometimes, between the rails and the sky, i find our old tune—crooked, friendly, breathing. i play it into the dark until the dark says uncle.

save me a seat on the stoop. i will return with my pockets full of wind.

always your brother,
Lew

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
