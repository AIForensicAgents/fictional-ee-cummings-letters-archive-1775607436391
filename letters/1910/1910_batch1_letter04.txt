Small Revolutions (After the Meeting)

Date: April 30, 1910
Recipient: Aunt Clarissa
Location: Providence, Rhode Island
Year: 1910
Themes: suffrage, courage, family, hope, social change

Dearest Aunt,

We gathered in the back room of the milliner’s shop where feathered hats pretended to listen. Someone had brought lemon cakes and a brass bell, and when Miss Turner read the petition her voice was not loud but it traveled as far as any train. We signed, our names practicing their future importance like new shoes.

I know you fear the world punishes women who stand up, but it punishes us also when we sit too long. I am not angry, or perhaps I am, but the anger has learned to dress politely and take tea with strangers. Do not think I have become ungrateful. I still admire the exactness of stitches, the mercy of a clean floor. But I want, too, to vote for the weather and for bread and for our daughters’ knees which deserve to run without apology.

On my way home the sky wore the color of a bruise healing. The street lamps blinked their astonishment and I felt as tall as any lamppost, as necessary as the moths that troubled them.

Kiss Uncle for me and tell him I am still the girl who mends stockings—only now I ask who cut the holes.

Your affectionate niece,
R.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
