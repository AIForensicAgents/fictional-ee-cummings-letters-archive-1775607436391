On the Capacities of Snow

Date: February 3, 1910
Recipient: Brother Samuel
Location: St. Paul, Minnesota
Year: 1910
Themes: family, winter, resilience, memory, rural life

Dear Sam,

Your letter arrived like a mitten in the postman’s mouth, warm and a little surprised. You say the barn leans more each year, as if the north wind has been whispering too many difficult truths. Here the rail-yard snaps and smokes, and boys with red ears carry coal as if carrying their small, burning futures.

I have discovered snow’s many occupations: seamstress (hemming the curbs), philosopher (erasing what does not matter), and juggler (keeping our footsteps in the air until they fall into meaning). When I lift it with my boot, a pale silence rises, the sort we used to hear when Father untied the bread.

I think of you mending harness with your careful thumb, the lantern doing its slow arithmetic across your knuckles. It is not always easy to be brave, especially when February shakes its serious head. But I believe, with an almost foolish faith, in the spring that hides behind the stove, counting down in warm breaths.

Tell Mother the quilt’s corner finally behaved; I sewed it with that crooked needle of hers and the thread learned to obey me like a patient dog.

Your sister in wool and stubbornness,
E.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
