Ferns That Remember Rain

Date: April 4, 1910
Recipient: Dr. Helen Moore
Location: Blue Ridge, North Carolina
Year: 1910
Themes: nature, science, wonder, spring, correspondence

Note: This letter adopts an experimental, lyrical voice without emulating any specific author's style.

Dear Dr. Moore,
i have made the acquaintance of a ravine that keeps its own calendar. mornings begin here with a polite mist—the sort that asks permission to become memory. the creek articulates stones until each has learned a wet grammar; i listen from the bank and take notes with my cold, enthusiastic fingers.

polypodiums spring from red clay as if remembering a promise made in an earlier rain. their fronds uncurl like small green declarations, and each sporangium seems a bronze punctuation persuading the sentence toward spring. i labeled specimens with a pencil bitten thin and felt (ridiculously, usefully) that i was being corrected by the forest.

a pair of thrushes practiced invisibility and nearly perfected it. only the idea of their song was audible, like music coming from the next room of a well-built house. i ate an apple and thought about your letter regarding root systems: how they make a democracy of dirt, quiet and exacting. somewhere between my boots an ant carried a seed with the serenity of a librarian.

i confess to an unscientific joy. forgive it. or better, classify it for me—give me genus: wonder; species: immediate; family: rain-born. i pressed a violet not because it is rare (it is not) but because it resembles a thought you sent last winter, folded in lamp-light.

when i return, i will bring you the fern that most resembles laughter and the moss that holds the softest shadow. until then, i remain under a sky knitted of pale wool, content to be a letter written by hills to the idea of distance.

faithfully and with mud on my cuffs,

—R.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
