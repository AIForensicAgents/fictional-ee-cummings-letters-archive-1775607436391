Timberline of Sleep

Date: March 19, 1910
Recipient: Mother
Location: Union Pacific Car, Nebraska
Year: 1910
Themes: travel, music, homesickness, landscape, hope

Note: This letter adopts an experimental, lyrical voice without emulating any specific author's style.

Dear Mother,
midnight wore a gray coat and rode beside me awhile—then nodded off against the window. the train has its own heartbeat, a big mechanical animal patience, and we live inside it like two brass buttons. between Council Bluffs and Kearney the fields lay down, humbly, like a congregation of brown coats.

i played for the porter after supper—some wandering hymn that kept forgetting its hat. the notes went walking along the aisle and peeked into other people’s dreams. a child laughed in her sleep (three times, quick) which i took as an omen for safe bridges.

have you seen wheat at dawn, mother. it is a choir rehearsing under its breath. the sky here is a long thought that nobody interrupts. now and then a solitary windmill lifts its one iron arm and speaks circles to the air. i keep thinking of your kitchen light, a small faithful star soldiering above the peelings and the cracked blue cup.

my violin behaves like a homesick bird, but she sings nevertheless. in the station at North Platte i scalded my tongue on coffee and forgave the cup immediately. a man told me about an orchard he intends, and i believed him as if he’d already planted it in my chest.

i will send money when the engagement at Cheyenne concludes. tell Ruth the music box is safe; it sleeps at the bottom of my suitcase like a respectful cat. if the river climbs its banks, nail the porch rug against the door and say a stern word to the water—it respects a voice that is not afraid to be small.

with my love threaded through each mile,

your son,

—E.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
