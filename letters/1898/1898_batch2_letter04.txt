Mother, concerning the moon and money

Date: 1898-10-28
Recipient: Mrs. Margaret Hale
Location: Boston, Massachusetts
Year: 1898
Themes: poverty, family, work, urban night, tenderness, resilience

Mother,

The moon nicks itself on the black chimneys and bleeds a little on our street. I walk home under its pocket-handkerchief of light and count the week's wages by touch: two silver coins, a proud procession of coppers, a thumb finally clean of indigo thread. I have put three dollars and a half in the matchbox with the paper violets; it will go to you Saturday, folded among the saints of newsprint.

At the shop the machines talk all day in iron syllables and I answer with stitches, small prayers I can hold between forefinger and thumb. Mr. Greeley says my hem is the straightest in the room. When the bell lets us out, our dresses lean together on the wall like tired soldiers. I wash my wrists in the pump out back until the water admits I am a person again.

Do not worry your good heart (it is a kettle that never boils over). I eat well enough: bread spread with molasses, sometimes an egg, once a pear that tasted like a bell rung very softly. Mrs. Devlin downstairs plays hymns badly and with such devotion the mistakes seem part of the believing. I save a seat by the window for the sun in case it comes late.

If Father coughs, make him the onion syrup exactly as Grandmother taught us. Tell him his old overcoat here keeps me warm two ways—by weight and by memory. When I hold its cuff to my cheek all the winters behave.

Your daughter,
Alice

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
