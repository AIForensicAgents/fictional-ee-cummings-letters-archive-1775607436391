wires like winter vines

Date: 1898-01-28
Recipient: Mr. Arthur Bell
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1898
Themes: modernity, communication, city, loneliness, art

Arthur,

midnight is a careful clerk, stamping hours paid in full. the office is a dim aquarium of sparks; i swim among messages, my fingers small fish nibbling the brass keys. tap tap—some banker in Omaha begs a number to become a promise; tap tap—two sweethearts in St. Louis kiss syllables they cannot speak aloud at home. electricity is a gossip with a wonderful memory.

outside, Chicago's streets are bones of ice, but these wires—these winter vines—bear fruit even now. you asked, once, if i feel lonely in the hive. yes and no. the clock's tin heart keeps me company. the last freight complains to the rails like a tired god. toward morning the coal men sling dawn over their shoulders and it falls in small black suns. i love all of it and long to escape it simultaneously: contradiction, your old friend, writes with my hand.

bring your new poems when you come. bring also your old patience for my complaints. i have written a verse or two myself (forgive the treachery), nothing that rhymes correctly with the times. perhaps, in a world polished to a mechanical brightness, one must scratch a little window with a crooked nail to see the stars.

our kettle coughs. i call that a sign to end.

yours in cinders and syllables,

Clara
