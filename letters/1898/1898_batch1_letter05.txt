absinthe-green noon

Date: 1898-10-04
Recipient: Cousin Alma
Location: Montmartre, Paris, France
Year: 1898
Themes: art, exile, city, yearning, courage

Dear Alma,

Paris has the peculiar habit of putting a hat upon even its shadows. afternoons turn their brims down and pretend to be night while painters throw colors like dice. i am a small apostle of this unruly church—my chapel a drafty studio where the window sulks and the stove forgives.

i purchase bread, a questionable pear, and a length of light each morning. then i arrange them so the pear can confide in the light and the bread can decode the pear's confession. when my brush believes me, a corner of the canvas learns to breathe. when it does not, i sweep the floor and discover that dust is a more honest artist than i am.

there is a café at the corner that tastes like ink. one gentleman insists on telling me the future each time he forgets his own. i let him point at my palm, which is not a map but tries so hard to be one. he says you will cross the ocean. i say i already have. he says no, the other one. do you think there is another ocean in a person, Alma, one no steamer can bully?

i am well, i am poor, i am filled with the precise ache of attempting. if i return in the spring, i shall bring you a handful of Montmartre's ridiculous air and the knowledge that even the wrong brushstroke can be a kind of courage.

kiss Aunt's stern teapot for me. send me news of your city—does it still climb upward like a young vine pretending at patience?

affectionately,

Lydia
