a small lantern for your window

Date: 1898-02-03
Recipient: Eleanor Finch
Location: Savannah, Georgia
Year: 1898
Themes: longing, river, southern winter, hope, intimacy, distance

My dearest Eleanor,

The river before dawn speaks in a hushed cough, as if some steamboat were clearing its throat of stars. I write by a tallow stub; each word is a moth risking itself against our small flame. The live oaks unbutton their gray lace and the fog combs through, slow as a widow's breathing.

I have hung your ribbon at the north window (so the wind may read us). Every hour is a small lantern I set upon the sill, and still the dark comes—patient, unoffended—teaching me to listen. I am stitching the days with the thread of your name. If the world is a porch, let me be the slant of evening there, warming the steps you climb.

I walk the squares and measure my hope by footsteps, brick to brick. Sometimes a carriage rattles emptily, and I think it is my heart changing addresses. Do not scold; I am all right. Bread and oranges, a letter from my aunt, the tailor has mended my cuffs. What I cannot mend is also a kind of music, and tonight it plays softly of you.

Should your window remember me, lift it a finger's width. I will believe that breeze my summons.

Always,
Nathaniel
