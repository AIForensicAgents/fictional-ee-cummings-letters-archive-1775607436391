To My Brother Beside the Cornfields

Date: 1913-06-12
Recipient: Thomas Bell
Location: Near Davenport, Iowa
Year: 1913
Themes: family, work, landscape, time, machines, homecoming

Brother Tom,—

The wheat puts up its rust-colored prayers and the sky answers with a brass mouth. I turned the hay rake until my hands burned and came in with a ring of dust about the wrists like a poor man’s jewelry. Nights the river loosens the tin songs of frogs, and the oaks move their shoulders against the stars as though trying on a larger coat.

There’s a boy in town learning the telegraph. He says the world can be told in dots and threads and we only need to listen with the right kind of ear. We laughed, but later I sat at the kitchen table and heard the clock tick a code I nearly understood. Maybe distance is shyness; maybe it just needs a wire.

Mother gets about as best she can; the rheumatism courts her like a gentleman with bad intentions. Father has begun measuring sunsets with the same tape he uses on boards, as if he might trim them level. The yards along Third Street have bottle trees, and when the wind comes up they catch little blues that chime against the heat.

Come home by the Fourth if the mill allows. We will split melons on the porch and spit the seeds at the hens (they never learn). Lucy’s boy is walking like he has negotiated new terms with gravity. Write me of the machines you keep—how they behave when watched and when alone.

Your brother,
Elias

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
