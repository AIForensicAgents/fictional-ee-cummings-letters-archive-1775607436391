On a February Streetlamp

Date: 1913-02-21
Recipient: Eliza Hart
Location: Greenwich Village, New York
Year: 1913
Themes: art, city, longing, winter, modernity, friendship

Dearest Eliza,—

This afternoon I stood before a painting where the sky was a shout. The room smelled of damp wool and varnish and everyone was very polite about disagreeing with the future. A man kept saying shapes should behave themselves; a woman with a fox collar laughed until her earrings trembled. Somewhere inside all that seriousness, a small bell started ringing in me and would not stop.

After dark, thin snow began. The streetlamp stitched brightness into the falling gray; each flake tried on a little gold before forgetting it. I walked home counting the warm windows, which—like faces at a ship rail—leaned out as if to wave. The fruit seller on Bleecker had only wooden crates left, emptiness stacked like stages awaiting players. Soon it will be oranges again. Soon, perhaps, your letter.

I think of your laugh traveling along my ribs like the slow electric of a streetcar, blue sparks nicking the air beneath its wheels. Tomorrow I shall go and stand by the river where a steamer breathes as if mending a torn lung, and gulls write loose alphabets above it. Send me a single word (any word) and I will wear it in my buttonhole, a small garden pinned to a gray coat.

Forgive this hurry of sentences; the lamp outside has turned my window into a page and the snow keeps writing.

Yours, always—
A.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
