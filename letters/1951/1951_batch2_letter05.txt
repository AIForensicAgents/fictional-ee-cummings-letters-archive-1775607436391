On lullabies and ashtrays: a note to Mother

Date: 1951-03-27
Recipient: Mother
Location: New Orleans, Louisiana
Year: 1951
Themes: forgiveness, family, music, home, nostalgia

Mother,

i have learned to make coffee in a pan when the kettle sulks; the trick is patience
and a wooden spoon that believes in you. outside, the river practices its slow handwriting.
a streetcar clangs like a polite argument. magnolias are scandalously sure of themselves.

i am writing from a table lacquered with years of elbows. an ashtray holds
failed comets. the radio, saint of static, leans toward a trumpet and confesses joy.

forgive me for leaving as if the door had always been open. it was, of course,
but it also had your hand on it. i see now how you swallowed thunder for us,
how you stitched months together with grocery string, never bragged.

i keep your lullabies in my pocket. sometimes the city gets loud and i let one out;
it walks beside me, small and stubborn, until the sidewalk remembers childhood.

i have your stubborn jaw (it rents space in my reflection, pays in dimes).
i have your way of hiding worry inside a joke and then forgetting which is which.

please write. tell me if the garden forgave the frost, if the neighbor's dog
still howls at trains it cannot possibly catch. tell me you are sleeping.

i will come home when the calendar stops pretending to be busy.
until then, i am yours in the way smoke belongs to its match—
briefly, brightly, and with a smell that never quite leaves.

love,
R.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
