dear mother, please find my heart between laundries

Date: 1954-04-11
Recipient: Mother
Location: Brooklyn, New York
Year: 1954
Themes: family, poverty, city life, homesickness, resilience

mother,
this letter smells like soap flakes and subway heat. i write between cycles, the machines turning shirts into weather.

the landlord says the roof is not a roof, only an idea with tar. i say my ceiling weeps when it rains. we compromise by putting a bucket under the place where hope leaks.

i send six dollars and seventy-three cents. forgive the stingy arithmetic. the city eats quarters like a bright-mouthed slot machine and pays me back in tiny claps of neon.

i pass a brownstone with a stoop that looks like a tired queen. someone is always humming. i pretend it is you, measuring flour by feeling, forgiving the recipe because you know better.

i have learned that loneliness is loudest at lunchtime. the radio argues about senators and sins. i would trade all that noise for the soft banging of your pots.

when i am brave, i stand by the bridge and let the wind rearrange my face into something truer. it works for a moment. then the city puts me back on like a glove and i am small again, but still trying.

i will come on sunday if the trains behave. keep the stove warm. keep your hands warm. keep me in the pocket where you hid the peppermint when i cried.

trying and trying,
mina

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
