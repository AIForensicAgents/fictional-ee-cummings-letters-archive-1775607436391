jukebox catechism

Date: 1954-03-29
Recipient: Nora, at the corner diner
Location: Detroit, Michigan
Year: 1954
Themes: working-class life, desire, music, industrial city, faith

dear nora,
the factory’s breath fogs the morning and refuses to apologize. i clock in, then i unclock in my head, where your laugh keeps counting to forever.

you slid the nickel like a small moon across the counter and the jukebox made a stained-glass of sound. hymn number seven: the one where the trumpet leans against midnight and refuses to sober up.

what i am trying to say is, your hands are the only blueprint i believe. when you pour coffee, time stands so still even the cream forgets its gravity.

the men at my line talk baseball and tires. i nod, i nod, then i remember the way you look out the window as if the day were a dog you are teaching gentle.

if there is a church, it has chrome edges and red vinyl pews. you pass the plate and it is full of nickels and your smile and frying eggs doing their own hallelujah.

meet me after your shift by the alley where the steam vents read palms. i will bring a bouquet of wrenches and one small courage. if you say no, i will still keep dancing on the inside, where no foreman can clock me out.

faithfully in grease and sugar,
leo

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
