margins where the future writes itself

Date: 1954-06-01
Recipient: Miss Lila Grant, Librarian
Location: Charleston, South Carolina
Year: 1954
Themes: hope, change, community, books, courage

miss lila grant,

today the radio climbed down into the quiet and said a sentence that clicked all afternoon in the spine of the shelves. i shelved atlases while the air practiced standing taller. a small boy traced the word together with his finger along the binding of an old civics book and did not know he was rehearsing a prayer.

i thought of your desk bell, its silver moon, and how you ring it once when someone forgets to whisper. forgive me: i wanted to ring it for the whole town. there are notices on the door polite as fences; there are habits that creak like starched skirts. but the dust lifted differently at three o'clock as if letters themselves were loosening their collars.

the street outside smelled of rain and chalk. two girls in blue socks argued about punctuation and then shared a pencil like a popsicle. i carried returns to the back and in the dim i felt my name grow new edges. not sharp, just honest.

if books are boats, then let us launch by handfuls. if rules are ropes, let us tie them to better posts. i will be there tomorrow at opening light, and the day after, and the day after that, returning each book with an extra heartbeat tucked inside.

please keep me a seat where the sun lands and makes everything legible. the future is practicing its handwriting and i would like to watch.

in hope and library dust,
 a.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
