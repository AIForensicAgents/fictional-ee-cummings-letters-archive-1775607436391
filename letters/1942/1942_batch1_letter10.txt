winter milk of constellations

Date: 1942-12-24
Recipient: Father
Location: New York City
Year: 1942
Themes: family, winter, city, memory, faith

father,

the roof is a quiet country and the city hums its many-chinned lullaby. i have brought a bottle of milk that believes in morning and two oranges that think they are suns. below, the river turns slowly like a page, and the bridges learn carols in a language of wheels.

i remember the year you taught me to read the sky. you pointed and the stars admitted their names with shy bravery. tonight i try to hear them through the sirens practicing their cold voices far away. light-ups and light-outs, we live by instructions now. yet someone two buildings over strings a zigzag of paper snow and the window approves.

a small flag hangs in the neighbor’s window, a blue star looking out like a bright worried eye. i tip my hat to it, and to the woman who waters a plant on the sill as if watering a boy. the war swallows and swallows, but still the bakery opens at five and sells its brown astonishments.

i wish you were here to tell the moon where to stand. i wish i could bring you the exact sound of my mother’s hands on the sink, washing sadness from apples. i am trying to be the careful man you meant when you said my name. i fail often but the failures are learning to sing.

on this eve i send you my pocket’s quiet, the heat from my gloves, and the map we once drew of a summer lake with a wild crooked shore. may morning arrive as softly as forgiveness.

your son under the patient stars,
Noah
