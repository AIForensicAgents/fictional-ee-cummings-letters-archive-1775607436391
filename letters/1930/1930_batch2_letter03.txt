a letter with bicycle bells

Date: 1930-05-03
Recipient: Miss Afton, Librarian
Location: Cambridge, Massachusetts
Year: 1930
Themes: books, spring, yearning, urban life, gentleness

dear miss afton,

i returned your book and kept its weather. the pages smelled of rain that never arrived. that is, perhaps, what we borrow from the library most: weather. you stamped the card with your small thunder and slipped it back like a secret into its stiff little bed. may is rehearsing petals outside, and the boys in caps throw words at each other the way light throws itself at water.

i ride my bicycle past the river (its polite cough of oars) and practice the delicate science of not arriving. each corner promises a new alphabet. the brick buildings remember things i don’t—each window is an eye that has decided not to blink, for our sake.

last night i thought of your voice saying hush the way a lighthouse hushes a storm. some books won’t stay closed, even asleep—i can hear them rustling their dreams. you always know which ones want to go home with which hands. how do you do that? is there a cataloging system for hunger?

in the reading room a gentleman dozed over economics until his spectacles fell into the depression of his lap. i wanted to lift them gently back and charge him a small fee for the rescue. instead i copied a line from a poet and then forgot whose line it was. does ownership matter if the heart is a common room?

if the budget allows, buy that slim volume of translations. it will teach the timid air to sing in a borrowed throat. and should you shelve me—by whim or accident—please keep me near the atlases. i get lost so beautifully.

ringingly,

p.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
