instructions for breathing beside machines

Date: 1930-06-12
Recipient: Mr. J. Wren
Location: Near the River, Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1930
Themes: labor, dignity, modernity, survival, hope

mr wren,

the whistle here is a schoolmaster with no patience. at seven it smacks the morning until all the windows confess. we punch the clock and the clock punches back. steel learns its song faster than a man, but a man, sir, has lungs that can remember a meadow even while swallowing smoke.

you once told me a factory is an orchestra of bolts and we must play in time or be replaced by better listeners. i try. yet there are moments when the belt forgets itself and the whole row shivers into near-music. i try not to be happy then. i fail.

my neighbor jim lost a finger to a rumor of carelessness. they wrapped it like a small truth and sent him home with a shrug. he laughed, said he had too much hand anyway, and taught his remaining fingers a new way to hold joy, which is a bottle, which is not in the rules. the lake was merciful blue last sunday and i forgave it for pretending to be the sea.

if there is room, keep me on. if there is not, i will take my breath and its stubborn heart to some other noise. i can carry weight. i can remember flowers without stopping the line. i send you this letter as a promise: that a man is not a part until he chooses to belong.

respectfully,

h. crane
