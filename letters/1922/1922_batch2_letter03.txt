brief treatise on umbrellas & courage

Date: 1922-06-19
Recipient: Mother
Location: Cambridge, Massachusetts
Year: 1922
Themes: family, weather, self-invention, work, tenderness

Dear Mother,

June bleeds its gentle rain and the lilacs open like soft arguments nobody must win. i have purchased an umbrella second-hand; its ribs are a polite cage, and when it flowers above me, i feel briefly forgiven for all my stenographic failings. yes, i am still at the office some days, at my own desk others (the one that is a suitcase). the city keeps enrolling me in its university of accidental lessons.

i learned: courage is not a trumpet, but the steady seamstress who mends the morning. i learned: a letter can walk across a bridge while the bridge is being invented under it. and i learned: tea simply asks for your quiet, and offers its own.

i know you worry about my thin shoes, but i have discovered the philosophy of dry socks carried in the coat pocket. i also know you count my meals across the miles; please count these: a warm loaf shared with the landlady (whose laughter fits the stairs exactly), a bowl of cherries each with its own tiny eclipse, and a slice of sky at dusk which tasted like salt and permission.

there is a certain indifference that tries to live in offices—i counter it by placing a wildflower in an inkwell, by mislaying frowns, by remembering the shape of your handwriting (it stoops to pet the words). if the promotion refuses to approach, i shall approach myself: i will invent a title and grow up into it quietly.

i send my love, which has both hands and is learning not to drop anything.

Your daughter,

M.
