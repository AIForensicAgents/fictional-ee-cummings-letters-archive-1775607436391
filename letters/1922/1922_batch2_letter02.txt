ink telegram from a small piano

Date: 1922-04-03
Recipient: Brother Milo
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1922
Themes: music, family, movement, work, city

Milo,

i found a tavern beneath the steel elbow of the tracks where a piano the size of a suitcase keeps telling the truth. the man who plays stamps rhythm like visas into the night; by closing time we are all permitted to enter a country made entirely of brass and breath. i have been teaching my fingers to stutter honestly—when they fail the keys, the keys forgive them with a blue grin.

work is scarce and the streets are a marching band of hunger, yet even the smokestacks lift their eyebrows at dawn. i earned two dollars carrying crates for a grocer who calls everyone "professor." he has a pet scale that lies sweetly for women. we are comrades, the scale and i—we refuse to weigh the improbable.

there’s a girl with a red scarf who leaves laughter behind the way a train leaves cinders. we spoke through the window’s reflection (conversation with a ghosted second self); she said the river isn’t moving, we are. i agreed out loud and the reflection nodded for both of us.

i miss your cornfield quiet, the moths like holy punctuation. send news of the tractor’s mood and how father argues with the moon over fences. tell mother i am eating—bread, beans, a fine stew of noises—and that i keep my hat honest. when i come home i will bring you a pocket of city sparks to release among the crickets.

salute the sky for me, brother. i am well enough to keep listening.

—J.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
