small prayers in a big rain

Date: 1895-02-12
Recipient: Brother Thomas
Location: Dublin, Ireland
Year: 1895
Themes: grief, family, faith, weather, memory

Dear Tom,

It has rained for three days with the sort of devotion a parish might envy. The gutters speak Latin and the cobbles reply in sighs; even the sparrows go about like wet idées, too shy to finish themselves. I went by the church and left a short prayer on the pew, folded like a reluctant map. Do you think God reads these? Or does He simply put His ear to them and hear the ache the way one hears the sea in a shell.

Your letter about Father arrived, trimmed in the silence you could not afford to write. I understand. There is a humility in certain griefs, the way a hat is held when a procession passes. I find him in small corners: the tobacco jar; the coat-peg with its lonely hook; the floorboard that remembers a familiar weight. Yet I am not undone. Some absences are so heavy they teach the room how to lift.

Mother counts weather as if it were prayer beads. She says the rain has good manners, steady and not too loud. I boil the kettle and let the window fog your name. It feels like conversation.

If you are tired, rest. The river does not argue with its banks. When it stops raining, I will bring violets to the plot and call it a beginning only because there is no word for what comes between ending and light.

Your sister,
Maud

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
