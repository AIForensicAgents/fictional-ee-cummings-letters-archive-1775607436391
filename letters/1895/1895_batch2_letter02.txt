Concerning a pocketwatch & the stubborn stars

Date: 1895-04-03
Recipient: Brother Thomas
Location: Kansas City, Missouri
Year: 1895
Themes: work, family, machines, restlessness, faith

Brother—

the yard smokes like a sermon. i have learned the grammar of coupling: how iron persuades iron, how a pin (small as a prayer) holds the thunder together. the foreman scolds the sun to rise earlier; i think the sun obeys only out of laughter.

i carry the old watch you sent. it ticks with Mother’s patience, though the glass is spidered. when the eleven o’clock freight goes west, the watch grows solemn; when a sparrow lands on the telegraph wire, it giggles (very quietly, a saint remembering a joke). these are not machinist’s observations, but you know how i am: a fool who oils the hinge of heaven with kerosene and sentiment.

at night i lie on a sack of barley and count the stubborn stars that refuse to be scheduled. tom, they keep their own ledger. i tried to reconcile accounts: the cindered wind, the lantern’s trembling arithmetic, the breath of the man two bunks over (he whistles in his sleep, a song older than trains). the totals will not come even. perhaps that is why we love: to forgive the column that will not add.

you asked, in your last, whether i intend to stay. my answer wobbles like the needle finding north. i want a roof that remembers my name, and a small patch of indifference where peas can climb their ladders toward a friendly nothing. but when the locomotive shouts my spine straight, i become an arrow again. perhaps, as Father used to say, the world will meet me halfway, if i keep walking.

keep the farm’s gate fastened and your temper slow. kiss the calf between her new horns for me. and if God has mislaid our fortune, tell Him Kindly we are willing to be found.

your brother in soot & stubbornness,
eli

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
