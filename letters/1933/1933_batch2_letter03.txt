Concerning Commas and Constellations

Date: 1933-01-17
Recipient: Mr. Calder
Location: Greenwich Village, New York
Year: 1933
Themes: art, publication, humor, ambition, urban winter

To Mr. Calder,

You wrote that my commas should stop loitering. They have taken this to heart and now parade in tidy lines at the edges of my sentences, caps in hand, each hoping to be hired as a breath.

I submit, therefore, a small constellated bundle: six poems and one apology disguised as a train ticket. They were written between midnight and the widow-hour when coffee forgets to be brave.

A city rat climbed my fire escape last week and sat like a punctuation mark on the sill. We looked through each other the way strangers look through shop windows after closing. Consider this my philosophy of publication.

You asked for clarity. Permit me a lantern made of paper, thin as an onion skin, but no less a lantern. The light will show the rafters and also how much dust refuses authority. The reader may sneeze, which is sometimes a blessing.

If you cannot use the poems, keep the apology; it folds neatly and can be used as a bookmark for a book nobody admits loving. In exchange please keep a chair warm for a future version of me with cleaner boots.

Outside, January is a custodian with a key ring of moons. If you publish me, I will salt the steps and answer to any name.

Obediently disobedient,
L. D.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
