Desert Letter with Blue Edges

Date: 1933-10-09
Recipient: Mother
Location: Santa Fe, New Mexico
Year: 1933
Themes: family, landscape, art, gratitude, radio

Dear Mother,

The desert has taught me how to drink light. Morning is poured blue from a tall pitcher and all the small adobe houses put out their tongues. By noon, everything is a bell without a clapper, ringing anyway.

I rent a room with a floor that remembers other footsteps. The woman downstairs dries red chilies on a line and the sun keeps licking them. When I pass, the air bites and forgives.

A boy showed me a roadrunner that outran his sorrow. He laughed with all his teeth and left two of them at the corner store for candy. This is how commerce survives here.

I am painting badly and happily, making skies that sag and then quietly lift themselves when I look away. There is a mountain that leans like a shoulder I thought I had lost.

Do not be alarmed if I return with dust braided into my hair; it is only the desert’s handwriting. It writes Mother across my scalp, so I remember where to send my thank-you.

At night the radio, traveling far, brings Washington as a trembling moth against the screen. Men promise futures with sturdy knuckles. I put a saucer of faith out and let it drink what it can.

All my love, and a small painted sky in an envelope if paint could dry faster.

Your daughter,
Miriam

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
