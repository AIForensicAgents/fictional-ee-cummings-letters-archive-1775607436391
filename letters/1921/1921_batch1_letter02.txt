postcard without a stamp (but with sky)

Date: 1921-02-03
Recipient: My Sister L.
Location: Cambridge, Massachusetts
Year: 1921
Themes: family, education, playfulness, winter, wonder

L—

classes are bricks; the day tries to build a sensible house with them, but then a blue wind comes tearing off the Charles and the whole architecture decides to be a kite. i suppose this is what learning means: holding the string without pretending the wind is your idea.

i have adopted a snowflake as my professor; it lectures in silence on the art of not-quite-falling. today during chapel i discovered the hymnal has margins large enough for doodles of constellations we have not met yet. (when we do meet them, remember to introduce yourself as a well-behaved astonishment.)

your letter spoke of chores and the stubbornness of the back door; i propose we bribe it with a song. sing slightly off-key (the truth is so often a little crooked) and promise it the first apricot of june. doors understand apricots.

Mother worries that my coat is thin; tell her i keep warm by thinking of home at unreasonable hours. the kitchen clock is a choir. the kettle is a tiny parliament of steam. i miss the way you laugh as if the joke were a bird and you had just let it go.

in case this postcard fails to arrive, look up—consider that the sky, unburdened by postage, is delivering all messages at once.

write soon, with exaggerations (they are more honest),

your brother,

n.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
