postcard-sized faith from red earth

Date: September 18, 1921
Recipient: Brother Eli
Location: Santa Fe, New Mexico
Year: 1921
Themes: travel, landscape, brotherhood, faith, silence, self-discovery

dear eli,

i have arrived where the road decides to be sky for a while. the mesas hold their breath and you can hear it (a large, polite silence). evening keeps its pockets full of copper. a single cloud walked across the desert at noon—barefoot, careful—and the lizards applauded by vanishing.

you would like the way distance behaves here: honest as a ledger, extravagant as a promise. last night a coyote footnoted the moon and the stars pretended not to notice. i wrote a few pages that smelled of juniper and improbable bravery. perhaps i will send them, perhaps i will keep them under my tongue until they sweeten.

there is a woman who sells turquoise from a small blanket. each stone is a cool blue vowel. i am tempted to buy a necklace of vowels and speak my way home wearing it.

i sleep in a room that looks west. the hills fold like clean shirts. a windmill turns and turns as if it were politely reconsidering a difficult idea. i wake before dawn because the horizon insists on rehearsals. this morning, faith felt like an ordinary spoon in my ordinary hand—but when i lifted it, the light gathered there as if to be stirred.

tell father i do remember the harvest. tell mother i am eating peaches that make promises and keep them. tell yourself, brother, that the world is larger than our caution and remarkably patient with us.

i send the dust on my boots as a blessing you mustn’t clean off too quickly.

yours in red earth,

— a

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
