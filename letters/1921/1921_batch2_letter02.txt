to my mother, with the small thunder of trains

Date: January 7, 1921
Recipient: Mother
Location: Cambridge, Massachusetts
Year: 1921
Themes: family, winter, memory, home, trains, tenderness

dear mother,

january’s breath keeps tapping on my window like a polite traveler who has come very far only to borrow a little warmth. i light the stove and let it tell its orange story; the kettle approves with a faint agreeable ghost of a whistle.

the trains go through the evening like useful dreams. i stand on the bridge and count them until my counting feels large enough to hold a year. each carriage a page. each window a face. i do not know them, but i wave anyway (the way you taught me: to greet what is passing as if it were staying).

i have been writing—nothing anyone would call sensible. but perhaps sense is a winter hat that covers the ears too well, and i wish to hear the thin music behind all things. i bake a little bread sometimes and it fails in beautiful ways: too brave with yeast, too impatient with rising. you would laugh and teach me again with your flour-white hands.

when i was a child you stitched my pockets so they wouldn’t lose my buttons. now the world keeps handing me unbuttonable mornings. i fasten them as best i can with a needle of attention, a thread of believing, and i think of you—how you made ordinary light sit still for a minute on the kitchen table, simply by placing a bowl of water in it.

if the thaw comes early, i will send you a postcard from the first puddle. until then, i am your son, walking carefully on the careful ice, practicing the art of not-falling, and loving you in every slow deliberate step.

kiss father’s newspaper and tell him it rustles like dry leaves i wish to save.

your affectionate,

— a
