unsent note to an editor concerning a hopeful book

Date: July 12, 1921
Recipient: Mr. P—, Editor at Beacon Review
Location: Boston, Massachusetts
Year: 1921
Themes: artistic defiance, publishing, playfulness, modern poetry, uncertainty, hope

sir,

forgive the acrobatics of my sentences; they are learning to walk on their hands. i enclose a manuscript which refuses to salute in the expected uniforms. words like to wander—mine especially. they return with pebbles in their shoes and a sky in their pockets (i let them keep both).

i have found that commas, when trusted, will learn to swim; periods prefer to be small round doors. adjectives misbehave and i reward them with a corner seat by the window. in short, i have not trimmed the hedge to please the neighbors, yet there are paths through it. sunlight knows them already.

perhaps you will say: the times favor clarity. i agree. but clarity is not the same as straightness. rivers are perfectly clear and look at their beautiful reluctance to obey.

a title? not yet. titles arrive late, like competent aunts bearing church cake and practical wisdom. i am patient because the pages themselves are impatient for oxygen; i open them like umbrellas in weather and listen for rain.

if you decline, decline with extravagance. it will help. i have learned from the theater that even a no can wear a velvet coat.

regardless, i am grateful for your eyes which must be full of strangers all day and yet remember how to recognize the honest face of a sentence when it enters without a hat.

yours in the hopeful disorder of print,

— a

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
