from a railcar stitched to the prairie (for A.)

Date: 1941-04-09
Recipient: Adele
Location: Between Omaha and Cheyenne
Year: 1941
Themes: travel, anticipation, America, draft, distance, chance encounters

adele,

the country keeps unfolding like an accordion nobody asked to play, and i am listening with my forehead on the glass. we pass fields where the wind is busy teaching wheat its careful bow; telephone poles pace us, tall and deliberate, like monks of distance.

the conductor punches holes in our tickets (constellations wearing their sunday best). across the aisle a young man with a paper carnation of a draft number pinned inside his hat sleeps with his jaw open to receive a dream—whatever dream chooses him. an old woman carries a pie the way a general carries time. i count towns by their water towers and the cinnamon of their depots; each one arrives like a word i cannot quite pronounce but somehow believe.

in the dining car i met a man who sells buttons and tragedies; he has a catalogue for both. he says the first thunder of the year happened in kansas last week and ran all the way to his bones. i tell him you prefer rain that looks like handwriting, each drop a long thin hello. he laughs and gives me a brass button engraved with an anchor and says keep still enough and you'll feel the sea in it.

when the train curves it feels the way your letter did when it said wait for me—how paper became horizon, how ink was a compass. the wheels repeat a soft arithmetic: soon, soon, soon. i have put your photograph in the window and we are traveling together (your eyes learning miles, my hands unlearning clocks).

if the west is a question, let our answer be another mile. i am coming nearer to the version of me that knows your breathing by heart.

with the speed of something gentle,
H
