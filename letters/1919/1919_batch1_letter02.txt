after the armistice: a small confession

Date: 1919-02-08
Recipient: Mother
Location: Boston, Massachusetts
Year: 1919
Themes: war, home, healing, memory

dear mother,

i came back with a pocketful of quiet. it slips out in the mornings, when the kettle shivers and the coat remembers another cold. people say peace like a door that will stay open. i nod and help mr. kelley shovel the stoop. the snow is clean as unasked questions; i taste iron when the shovel bites the curb.

am i different? sometimes i tie my shoes as if they might explode. sometimes a pigeon’s wing knocks my heart crooked. sometimes a child’s laugh fixes it again (simple as bread).

i cannot tell the stories straight. they turn corners without warning and everyone is suddenly someone’s son. what i can tell: mud learns all our names quickly; dawn wears the same dress every day and never grows tired of us; i still know the smell of oil on a rifle the way you know cinnamon in december.

but tonight there is your kitchen. it remembers how to be warm without hurting. your hands are a geography where maps are unnecessary. i am not asking you to understand the dark—only to sit with me where the light forgets to be afraid.

i will bring home oranges (bright, foolish suns) and try not to flinch at the lids of pots.

your boy,

—n
