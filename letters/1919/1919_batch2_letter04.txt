the river writes through us (to Mother)

Date: 1919-11-02
Recipient: Mother
Location: Dayton, Ohio
Year: 1919
Themes: homecoming, memory, motherhood, autumn, healing

Mother,

after church i walked by the river and it kept its old habit of pretending to hurry. leaves went past with the dignity of letters we forgot to send. November's breath wetted my cuffs and i remembered how you fold quilts as if tucking in weather itself.

i am learning silence again. not the cruel kind we carried home in our boots, but the kitchen kind where a kettle teaches the room to listen. you asked if i sleep; i do, and when i do not, i inventory the darkness: one chair, two shoelaces, three foolish hopes. the counting helps until it doesn't, and then i allow myself to be a small boat unmoored inside my own chest.

Mr. Halpern says business improves with every day that forgets to be yesterday. i sweep his shop and sometimes the broom remembers poppies; then i lean on it until i'm only a man and not a field. you would scold kindly if you saw me then; you would pour tea over the roar of it and the roar would become a purr made of steam.

there is a girl who sells apples. her sleeve is mended twice in the same place and this seems a promise: that a thing can break where it is strongest and still be worn. i am trying to learn from her sleeve without speaking to it. i think you understand.

if you have kept my blue scarf, do not wash away the last of France from its corner. a little foreign dirt is a talisman against the kings who live in memory and demand taxes. i send instead a jar of late pears and a foolish drawing of our gate, which refuses to be straight, like all useful gates.

kiss father on his good ear. the other ear can have my unkissed attempt. tell the stove i am coming when the frost learns my name.

your son,

M.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
