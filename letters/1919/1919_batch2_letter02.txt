dust & saffron: to my brother on a westward noon

Date: 1919-06-07
Recipient: Brother Nathan
Location: Santa Fe, New Mexico
Year: 1919
Themes: siblings, travel, healing, landscape, becoming

Nathan,

the day here is a skillet turned toward the sun. dust moves in low prayers, and every adobe wall remembers a palm. when noon climbs the church bell, its sound falls like a ribbon of copper, thin and hot. i think of you under midwestern trees, their sensible shade like mother at her mending.

men at the market sell chiles strung like small comets. a woman laughed and the laughter was saffron; i caught some on my sleeve and will keep it there until you shake my hand in August or October or whenever the wheels say yes. i have learned the taste of distance: it is sweet on the first lip and then it is thirsty work.

i have found a room whose window is a square of unbuttoned sky. evenings collect their blue coins and spend them slowly one star at a time. i draw until my wrist forgets its bones. the mesa pretends to be a thought i once had and am nearly remembering.

you asked if the war is finished inside my head. some nights a train passes and all the rails become a helmet, a canteen, a name i cannot lift. other nights the coyotes stitch a far-soft music and something mends. i am not new, brother, but i am not ruined; perhaps i am a road that will be made by walking.

send news of the river, whether it still keeps the same lazy grammar; tell me if our father's hat finally learned the curve of his patience. i carry you in my left pocket with my pencil stubs and in my right pocket with a folded horizon.

from your sunburned and unbecomingly hopeful sibling,

E.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
