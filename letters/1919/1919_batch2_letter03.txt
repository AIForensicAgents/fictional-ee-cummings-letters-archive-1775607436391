small prayer for a typewriter (addressed to Miss Effie)

Date: 1919-09-14
Recipient: Miss Effie Morton
Location: New York City, New York
Year: 1919
Themes: modernity, work, machines, affection, city life

Dear Miss Effie,

forgive the irregular ink; the city keeps bumping my elbow. upstairs a phonograph is teaching the night a new skip and downstairs a door slams like a period handed its hat. my own machine (the black-shouldered one with the silver grin) coughs out letters obedient and brave, tiny soldiers marching toward meaning. i give them coffee; i salute.

i have discovered the key that is also a window: it is the one that refuses to be ordinary when asked. strike it, and somewhere electricity looks up from its newspaper. strike it again, and a small republic is declared in the margins. i trust you know the one i mean.

you showed me once how the ribbon stains your fingers and becomes a kind of proof: hands as grammar, wrists as prepositions. today i typed the word tender and the room leaned closer, as if to ask for a demonstration. i offered it my pulse. which is to say: i have been thinking of your laugh that arrives like carriage-return and sends everything back to the shore of beginning.

on Broadway the wind herds newspapers; headlines chase their own tails. men speak prohibition like a magic trick that will pour more of less into less of more. i keep a bottle of patience where a bottle of gin would go. some nights i drink it all and wake almost good.

should you tire of dictation and the official stamp, remember: our letters are wild animals pretending manners. secretly they eat from the palm and sleep in our shoes. if this reaches you while the office fan is slicing September into reasonable pieces, look away and let one unreasonable minute touch your cheek.

faithfully disreputable,

R.
