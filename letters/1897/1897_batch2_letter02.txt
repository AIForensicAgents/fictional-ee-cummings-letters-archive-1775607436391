Railyard Psalm (for T.)

Date: 1897-03-02
Recipient: Mr. Thomas Avery
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1897
Themes: industry, ambition, sibling, city, motion

Dear Thomas,

Coal dust keeps its calendar on my cuffs; I am dated and re-dated by each coupling. The engines—Number 412 sneezing like a bull, little Ida with her shy whistle—have taught me their iron grammar. Pistons conjugate hurry; tracks decline the case of away. I study them till my ears are a blackboard where steam scrawls its whitest answer.

You asked if the city is kind. No, brother. It is not kind; it is awake. It holds its eyelids open with cranes and shouts its name into the river till even the ice coughs. Yet in that racket I have found a pocket of music, a narrow room between boxcars where my heart hangs its hat and sits with a tin cup of tea.

Tonight a boy leapt from the rear platform, laughing at the chase of his own breath. I wanted to caution him—as I always want to caution myself—but the laughter outran us both and went flying past the signals into the low blue mile. There is a sermon in that, if sermons are permitted in a yard where bells already preach.

Do you remember the field behind the school where we learned to spit through our teeth like heroes? This is the same lesson, only the prize is heavier and moves on steel. I am not afraid, Thomas, but I am not brave; I am simply moving with the day, which clanks and glows and stops for no letter.

Write me of your small victories—how the bread rose, how the gate held. Send me a pocket-knife of news to whittle the hours.

Your brother,

J.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
