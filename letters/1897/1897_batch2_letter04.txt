Salt-ink to a Captain

Date: 1897-08-10
Recipient: Captain Ezra Boone
Location: Nantucket, Massachusetts
Year: 1897
Themes: ocean, mentorship, courage, navigation, yearning

Captain,

The harbor keeps its shoulders lit with minnows, and a gull stitches the sky with a needle of hunger. I watched the men coil rope as if they were winding the clocks of the sea. Your name stood on the pier with its hat off, respectful of weather.

Teach me, if you will, the alphabet of wind. I can already pronounce a small breeze (it says hush and means go), but your storms have syllables I cannot yet lift. There is a chart in your pocket that knows the way between this breath and the next. Do maps ever doubt? Mine do. They were printed inland and smell of corn.

Last night the lighthouse winked like an old conspirator. Between its blinks, I saw August open its throat to swallow one more tide. The moon went down with a polite bow, the way you leave a room when it is finished talking. And all the while my hands rehearsed knots against my knees—reef, bowline, that fabled simple bend which binds two stubborn ends into a middle.

If you take me out, I will not argue with the compass. I will learn the mercy of a corrected course and the pride of a sail that listens. I will keep my fear in a tin and rattle it only when laughter is needed.

I send this with the salt still on it. Paper crinkles like a gulls laugh; forgive its shore-born accent. When you return, bring me the name of a far color—the green that exists only three miles past courage.

Your willing novice,

H.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
