Snow Letter with Lamp-Heart

Date: 1897-01-14
Recipient: Miss Eliza Whitcomb
Location: Providence, Rhode Island
Year: 1897
Themes: winter, longing, distance, light, tenderness

Dear Eliza,

The snow has learned a small language tonight and every roof replies in white. I stood beneath the gas-lamp on Benefit Street and felt the warm hum of its little sun, a secret chrysalis over my foolish hands. Your name walked beside me, softly, as if it wore woolen boots and kept my breath from breaking.

Do not scold me for the late hour; midnight in January is a shy gate and I must lean upon it. The houses here grow a kind of fur; eaves bristle with icicles, silver combs in the hair of the dark. I think of your window two towns away, where curtains sail in their teacup breeze and you—stitching or not—smile at a book that refuses to be tamed.

I am learning to fold minutes like napkins, to set a table for absence. Each plate holds a small pear of silence, and I salt it with my laughter so you will not starve. What a ridiculous feast, you say. Yes, but I am (ridiculous) and you are the sensible miracle who drinks the lamp and gives it back as morning.

There is a carriage-track in the snow like two black veins, and a star is late for its appointment with the river. Imagine me skimming the page of the night with a mittened thumb, turning it toward our next meeting, which is nearer than the next breath and farther than the next mile.

Be kind to your pulse. Wrap it well. If the snow speaks to you, write me its smallest word.

Ever and always,

A.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
