Slow Magnolia Letter to Mother

Date: 1897-05-21
Recipient: Mrs. Agnes Bly
Location: Savannah, Georgia
Year: 1897
Themes: family, convalescence, memory, spring, care

Dearest Mother,

Evening has unbuttoned the day, and the magnolia by the gate loosens one enormous handkerchief at a time. I sit beneath it like a traveler who has at last forgiven his suitcase. The air has that spoonful of sugar you always said belonged to May.

I have been walking in a circle of quiet, Mother. Since my fever, the clock looks gentler; its face has learned to lean. The doctor speaks in commas, which I prefer. It is a small mercy that language can teach a body to soften.

Do not trouble for the rent; the landlord is a moth and I have burned a polite candle for him. Miss Corbett from next door left a dish of peaches and a stern talk about posture. I ate the peaches and kept the talk for later. The cane you sent taps out a foolish rhythm on the veranda and the sparrows dance according to its nonsense.

I thought of Father when the church bell startled the Sunday sky. How he would lift his hat and set it like a buoy upon his hair. He was a careful sailor of sidewalks. I am trying to walk as he did, as if each inch were a promise not to be hurried.

If you come before the heat grows bold, bring your blue thimble. I have two shirts that wish for mending and a heart with the same bright wish. Do not be alarmed at the thinness you will see in my face; it is only the paper on which I am writing myself again.

With slow love and faster gratitude,

Your child,

E.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
