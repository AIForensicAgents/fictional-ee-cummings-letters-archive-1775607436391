Paper cranes among ash-blue mornings

Date: 1947-05-07
Recipient: Brother Theo
Location: Yokohama Harbor, Japan
Year: 1947
Themes: rebuilding, distance, family, war aftermath, small beauties

Dear Theo,

the harbor exhales a tea-steam of tar and gulls; ropes bite the dock like hard bread and the water carries its slow alphabet of oil. mornings here are ash-blue, then sudden—sunlight opens like a fan in a geisha's hand and you can hear buildings learning to stand again. a child folded me a crane from a candy wrapper; it is both lighter than my pocket and heavier than last year.

I walk past stalls of persimmons (small suns) and fish like silver arguments. the air holds a thousand kinds of hammer, yet the temples keep time—bells that were bent now breathe, and pigeons practice forgiveness on their steps. sometimes trucks with English painted on their flanks whisper through dawn; I taste home in the exhaust and then it is gone.

I fix generators and pretend I am mending miles of sky. the men laugh when I curse in two languages; we trade words the way sailors trade matches. when I cannot sleep I send you letters I never post—tucked in the sleeves of my notebook, they rustle like a forest of thin hopes. tell Ma the cranes here are everywhere and nowhere: you open your hands and find flight and paper.

what do our trees look like now by the creek; do the tadpoles still wag their black commas in the muddy grammar of spring. I am trying to be useful, not just busy. in the market a woman sews a blue patch into a blue kimono and somehow it is more beautiful for confessing its wound. if I make it home by autumn, I will bring you a pocketful of coins with square holes (tiny windows to a sky you can keep).

keep the porch light awake for me,
James

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
