unsent telegram folded into rain

Date: 1949-01-29
Recipient: Brother Samuel
Location: Cleveland, Ohio
Year: 1949
Themes: family, winter, work, memory, hope

sam—

this is not a telegram because the clerk would never allow me all these commas and small prayers. besides, the wires between here and you seem tired; they keep shrugging at the snow.

lake erie keeps its knives under the table of wind. the steel mill whistles make a cathedral of noon (everyone kneels to time, everyone rises smoking). mother leaves father's old coat over the chair as if midnight will come back for it; she pats the shoulders before bed, and i cannot say this is not a kind of faith.

i carry in my pocket a streetcar token smooth as a wishbone. yesterday, a woman with a grocery sack lost an apple—bright as an unbuttoned thought—rolling down the aisle; three strangers chased it into an apology and then a laugh big enough to keep us warm all the way to public square. such kindnesses are really furnaces, you know. we stand around them pretending it is weather.

you write of distances turned into degrees on your map. i write of the small blizzards inside quiet things: a cracked teacup, a button missing from wednesday, a letter with a thin ache of stamp. if father were here he would say, keep your boots honest and your hands with purpose. so i mend the torn lining of january with thread from the hardware store—yes, the shop sells thread because winter asks everyone to be complicated.

i am working nights at the press. the ink crawls into my fingers; i wear black moons for nails. when the machine pauses, there is a silence like a door someone might come through. perhaps you. perhaps no one. either way, i keep the hinge oiled.

if you come by easter, we'll walk the viaduct and teach the thaw our names. if not, i'll send your birthday socks (dark green, stubborn) and pretend the postage knows where you keep your smile.

tell the horizon i said please.

your brother,

ben

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
