a sister is a quiet cathedral

Date: 1949-02-21
Recipient: Ruth
Location: Cleveland, Ohio
Year: 1949
Themes: family, recovery, winter, memory, home

ruth,

i keep your last laughter in my coat pocket, a small bell with salt on it. lake erie is practicing thawing at the edges, like a person learning to forgive. the streetcars complain politely (they know every bruise of this winter), and i walk home with bread under my arm as if bread were a poem too warm for recitation.

do you remember mother's blue apron, the way its strings were maps that kept the kitchen from coming apart? yesterday i found one of father's hatpins in the junk drawer. the metal smelled like rain from a different decade.

you asked in your telegram how the nights are. sometimes they are a door i can open. sometimes they are a door that opens me. the house keeps one foot in dream and one in linoleum. i am well enough (by the small scale that measures tea against silence). i sleep in the room with the yellow wallpaper that thinks of butter and summer and doesn't quite forgive february.

if i sound strange it's because the world is still returning from the war, piece by piece, like a child remembering a song while washing dishes. i met a boy at the bus stop who keeps his medals in a cigar box; he said the hardest thing now is names. i gave him ours and they sat between us, warm as pennies.

write soon. fold your letter into the shape of our old porch swing and i will sit in it until the ink creaks.

your brother,
ben
