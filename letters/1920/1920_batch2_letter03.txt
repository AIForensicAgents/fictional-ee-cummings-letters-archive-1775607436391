Unposted letter to the sea (via you)

Date: 1920-07-05
Recipient: Samuel
Location: Gloucester, Massachusetts
Year: 1920
Themes: brotherhood, sea, memory, homecoming, summer

Brother Sam,
this morning the harbor wore its Sunday shirt, plain blue with a stubborn wrinkle near the wharf. I walked past the nets sleeping like tired hands and the gulls conducting an opera of appetite. Salt keeps finding the soft architecture of my mouth; it is a geography I know from childhood and other small eternities.

You always trusted tide to translate you, boat to rib, rib to weather. I trust paper, perhaps foolishly, to carry my shoulder against yours across the thick summer. The fireworks last night left a bouquet of soot in the sky, and we cheered like our throats were lanterns. I wanted to tell you then that the world is large but not larger than our shared silence, which has ropes and knots and a compass that points to a particular laugh you invented.

There are days the city pretends to be ocean and fails with great success; its waves are carts and faces and the newsboy's alarm. On those days I am a poor sailor. On these days here, I am rich with tar and sunlight, sudden wind in my pockets. If I could send you this smell I would not need language.

Keep your feet sure on the deck and your stubborn mouth kind to the green way water insists. When the fog parks itself on the hill, remember: there is a lighthouse in our blood that no weather extinguishes.

All my salt to you,
who knows how to come home by listening
