a lighthouse letter stitched to November

Date: 1920-11-21
Recipient: Father at the mill
Location: Portland, Maine
Year: 1920
Themes: family, reconciliation, work, winter, election

Father,

The lighthouse keeps its lesson: blink, breathe, continue. I stand on the breakwater and the wind scolds me into patience. Newsprint says the nation has chosen; in the barbershop a boy recites it in exchange for nickels and astonishment.

I think of your hands, decades of cotton and steam, and how every whistle at the mill wrote its small law across our suppers. Forgive me that I left. The city is a crowded book, but sometimes a clean page is a kind of hunger and I could not stop turning.

If you ever care to visit, I will show you the harbor learning to be winter, boats buttoning their coats, gulls practicing the virtue of staying. We might sit without cleverness and let the sea assemble our sentences for us.

Your son, with more stubble than answers,

Henry.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
