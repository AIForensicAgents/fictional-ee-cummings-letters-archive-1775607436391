about the theater of breathing

Date: 1920-10-09
Recipient: Miss Ada Penfield, Editor
Location: Greenwich Village, New York
Year: 1920
Themes: art, publishing, experimentation, modernity, humor

Miss Penfield,
I enclose a small troupe of poems which, despite rehearsal, insist on improvising. They prefer the low laughter of margins to the strict applause of punctuation. If you find them barefoot on your desk, forgive their toes. They were born in rooms where windows argued with weather and always won by opening.

I am told the times enjoy straight lines and sensible hats. Yet I have witnessed a comma perform a miracle and a dash leap a river. The words arrive as citizens and leave as acrobats; I pretend to direct them but mostly they elope together when I glance away. If your pages need order, I can teach them to bow. If your pages need oxygen, let them climb in the fire escape of my voice and look down at the street until dizziness becomes song.

What do these pieces mean, you might ask, and I might answer: a door means what it refuses and what it allows. Love means its echo. New York means the exclamation marking its own breath at dawn. I hope for nothing impossible except perhaps a reader who forgets to breathe on the third line and remembers by the fifth.

Should you find room for them, they will be punctual. Should you not, they will go on touring the interiors of envelopes, charming moths and patient dust.

With unruly regards,
a conspirator of syllables

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
