Black Ribbon, White Lilacs

Date: 1908-10-19
Recipient: Mother (unsent)
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1908
Themes: grief, memory, family, resilience, autumn

Mother,

The city has put on its black gloves; even the sky is dressed for the service. I brought white lilacs to your absence and the air remembered how to ache. The minister spoke, but it was the old kitchen clock that made the truest sermon—tick by tick—reminding us that grief is simply time wearing a heavier coat.

What shall i do with your thimble, bright as a syllable? It sits upon my desk like a small crown for a kingdom of thread. I try to sew the day back together—morning to evening—but the seam shows and sometimes i prefer it so. People offer condolences, which are coins with holes; i string them and make a quiet necklace of politeness.

On the walk home, first frost practicing. The world rehearsed its glitter. I thought of the way you would tuck my scarf and how that simple choreography saved me from so many colds, so many foolishnesses. If you are anywhere (and everywhere is an honest rumor) i hope it is a place where bread forgives hunger and sleep forgives sorrow.

I will not be brave the ordinary way. I will be brave like a teacup—holding, though scalded; chipped perhaps, but ringing when touched. When i laugh again, and i will, it shall be because you taught my mouth that music.

Your son, quietly,

Edward
