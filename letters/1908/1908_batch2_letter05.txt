Lessons in Unruly Grammar

Date: 1908-12-28
Recipient: Mr. Whittemore, my most patient mentor
Location: Paris, France
Year: 1908
Themes: art, mentorship, Paris, experimentation, new year

Esteemed Mr. Whittemore,

Paris unties its ribbons every evening and lets the streets spill their perfumes and arguments. I write from a café where the spoons gossip and the lamp makes a small moon of every saucer. My French limps but my astonishment runs marathons.

You once told me to distrust grammar whenever it behaved like a jailer. I am trying. Today i composed a sentence that refused to end; it wandered down the page collecting adjectives like stray cats until, exhausted and purring, it slept inside a period. The painter at the next table said my commas looked like ears and then proved it with a sketch—everything listened.

There are galleries where color shouts and then apologizes, and a bridge that understands how to keep secrets for two people walking in opposite directions. I attend lectures in the afternoons; even when i disagree, i am educated by the windows—their clear insistence that outside exists.

The new year waits behind the curtain, breathing. I intend to bow, then improvise. If i return with a different gait, know it was the cobblestones tutoring my feet; if my lines grow stranger, know it was joy stretching them awake.

Thank you for teaching me to be precise when I am wild.

Your obstinate pupil,

Lucien
