To the Small God of Ink

Date: 1908-01-18
Recipient: Mr. Hollis, Printer
Location: New York City, New York
Year: 1908
Themes: art, labor, ambition, humility, creation

Sir,

Your shop breathes at a steady, mortal pace; the press is a heart that never learned exhaustion. I stood in your doorway today and learned the beautiful grammar of oil. Letters slept in their little drawers—n’s beside o’s—like neighbors who share a backyard fence and a moon.

I send you these pages (not yet brave enough to call them poems) and ask if your steel mouth might kiss them into the world. Set them loose between the margins to run with their knees skinned and their pockets full of thumbtacks. If they are clumsy, forgive them; I have taught them only to fall forward.

I can pay little. A handful of coins. An offering of gratitude and a pocketwatch that keeps impractical time—sometimes slow, sometimes wild—like a boy late to summer. But I promise to bring you the first copy, warm as bread, and to listen while the press speaks, that low thunder of yes which makes paper remember it was once a tree and before that an idea of green.

If you refuse, I shall keep writing anyhow; but my words—unprinted—are moths in a coat; they will eat me.

With respect, and an ink-stained thumb,
R.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
