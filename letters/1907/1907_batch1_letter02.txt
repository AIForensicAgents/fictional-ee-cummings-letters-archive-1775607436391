Lantern and Tide

Date: 1907-02-24
Recipient: Mr. Abel Briney, Keeper of the North Pier Light
Location: Portsmouth, New Hampshire
Year: 1907
Themes: sea, duty, solitude, light, strangers

Sir,

i have never met a lighthouse keeper and so i am writing to the idea of you: a man walking the spiral of his duty, windows salted with night. Forgive a stranger; i am a pilgrim of shorelines this week, having surrendered my city habits to the discipline of gulls and ropes and the blunt grammar of anchors. The tide makes and unmakes the harbor with an old patience. Between its comings i have learned to hear a seam in the wind where a prayer might fit.

This morning your lantern sulked against the fog and then brightened as if remembering its own birth. I thought: yes, we all do that. A boy on the quay whistled a tune i knew in another life, and a woman stitched bait to hooks with holy indifference. In the seam between them your beam kept folding and unfolding, a handkerchief waved by a gigantic, invisible aunt.

Tell me, is it always like this—time whittled down to the thickness of glass you must polish, the wick you must encourage, the steps you must count (thirty, then thirty, then thirty again) until counting turns into a careful music? I envy your arithmetic. Mine has too many ifs and too few certainties; the city keeps changing its currency of hearts.

When the foghorn sounded—a cow grown into thunder—I thought of those at sea, how the unknown must be a room they carry inside, with a chair rocking though no one sits. Your light is the door in that room. Doors do not choose who passes; they simply are brave. May we all be doors.

In gratitude for the evening you saved from drowning,
A passerby with salt in his pockets

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
