Solstice Specimen

Date: 1907-06-21
Recipient: Nathaniel Voss, Botanist
Location: St. Louis, Missouri
Year: 1907
Themes: nature, time, friendship, summer, observation

Nathaniel,

today i discovered the prairie keeps a ledger. Every stalk writes itself against the sky, inked by sun, signed by wind. On the solstice you must forgive me a little pagan honesty: i knelt to a milkweed and admired its brave extravagance. The pods like tiny lutes, the silk rehearsing departure before any ship was built.

Your letters teach me to press what will not be held. I carried a tin of paper and a promise. The bloom i chose had the bluest gossip. When i arranged it under wax paper the air learned my secrets and forgave them. Is that what your herbarium does—tenders the scandals of chlorophyll until they become law?

At noon i closed my eyes and heard one hundred grasses performing custody of light. The train in the distance pretended to be thunder; the actual thunder followed, as if embarrassed by its lateness. A boy rode past on a bay pony and saluted something invisible. I saluted it, too.

You once wrote that plants are clocks that keep God’s time. If so, i am late to most days. Teach me the hours a seed owns. Teach me to be quiet when the earth speaks with its green mouth full.

I enclose—carefully—the flattened summer of a single wild bergamot. Should it arrive torn, imagine its purple still arguing with noon.

Yours, pollen-stained and improved,
A.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
