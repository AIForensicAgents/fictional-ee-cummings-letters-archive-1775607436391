Dust Drill

Date: 1907-08-16
Recipient: Private Oscar Hale
Location: Fort Riley, Kansas
Year: 1907
Themes: friendship, military, heat, resilience, homespun wisdom

Oscar,

your postcard arrived with a bootprint—authenticity is your best ink. You say the sergeant believes in mornings like a religion and that the sun here has no manners. I believe you. Even the postmaster looked sandblasted when he handed me your message.

I walked out beyond town where the rails ate horizon for supper. A grasshopper auditioned for trumpet at my heel; i passed him with reluctant praise. The sky was a sheet iron promise. In such weather words shrink from their own shadows, yet i kept a few stubborn ones to send you: drink, breathe, laugh (quiet if they must, but laugh).

Do the rifles learn your name? Teach them not to. Keep it for a person with hands gentler than metal. At night, when you arrange your blanket like a map of mercy, count backwards from the day to the boy who skinned his knees on my front steps and would not cry because i was watching. I am watching still.

If the bugle grabs dawn by the collar and the parade ground decides to become a storm, remember this: clouds are only soldiers who forgot their drills and wandered up. Even generals look small from a tall enough forgiveness.

Write soon. Send dust if you send nothing else; i will sprinkle it on the mantel and call it Kansas.

Your friend in the undeclared peace,
A.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
