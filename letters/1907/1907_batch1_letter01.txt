Snow-telegram to a window

Date: 1907-01-08
Recipient: Eleanor Whit
Location: Cambridge, Massachusetts
Year: 1907
Themes: winter, longing, city, silence, forgiveness

Dear Eleanor,

tonight the snow is practicing its tiny alphabets upon the sill; crisp little hushes broken by the faintest bell of a sleigh far down Brattle. I leaned my forehead to the pane and felt the city breathe—a careful animal of lamps and coal, coughing warm smoke into the midnight. The telegraph wire outside makes a thin violin of wind; it sings your name in a stubborn minor, again (again) until i nearly believe it is a letter addressed to you pressed against my glass.

I walked home through a street of gaslights that puddled their yellow on the white world. Boots spoke a grammar of crunch, and behind each curtained square a private planet spun—china clinked, a kettle’s brave heartbeat, an argument unthreading into laughter. I wanted to knock at every door and say, hush; listen: the whole night is writing.

Your last note still keeps its ache. Will you forgive me for the clumsy noon when i said the wrong courage and the right cowardice? Sometimes a mouth is an untrained horse. Even so, i keep a small stable of apologies and let them breathe until they are strong enough to carry flowers.

If you were here, i would show you the way snow takes the steps—how each rise wears a froth of brightness like petticoats. We would leave a pair of commas where our heels paused, and the moon would finish the sentence for us with a gentle period upon the river.

I enclose only my breath, which fogs the window and, for a blink, invents summer. When it clears, there is the quiet city again, and there, quite impossibly, your face in the pane where mine had been.

Yours—carefully, foolishly,
A.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
