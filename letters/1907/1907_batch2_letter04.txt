to Mother, concerning the moon's occupation

Date: 1907-07-08
Recipient: Mother
Location: Cape Ann, Massachusetts
Year: 1907
Themes: family, nature, home, tenderness, seaside

dear Mother,

tonight the moon has taken a summer job on our porch: it rocks steadily in Father’s chair and listens to the crickets say their careful prayers. the lantern hums like a jar learning to swarm. gulls sew and unsew the dark with white thread until the shoreline holds.

i should be sleeping; the fishermen will tug the morning up by its wet sleeve at four. already their nets are dreaming of fish with coin-bright eyes. i walked to the harbor and put my ear to a barrel; it remembered last winter (salt thinking slow). a boy on the wharf sold me a miracle for two pennies: a splinter that once believed it was a mast.

our kitchen smells of bread even when empty; i am sure your apricots are to blame. you have taught the jars to keep summer like a secret—tight-lidded, patient, shining a little when no one looks. i confess i opened one last night in the dark so the sweetness would not see me and be shy. i spoilt the spoon with moonlight and it did not complain.

this afternoon i wrote a letter and the paper wrinkled exactly where your hand would steady mine. do you ache when it storms? the doctor says the sea changes its mind three times before breakfast; perhaps we do too. i am trying to change only once (toward kindness) and keep that shape like the lighthouse keeps its flare: regular, believable, seen even by stubborn ships.

supper was the quietest part of the day. Father counted boats with eyebrows; i counted swallows; our neighbor counted the reasons her husband should stop chewing tobacco (an infinite number disguised as seven). everyone here is an abacus held together by weather.

i send you a packet of beachgrass tied with a blue thread from my sleeve, and this exact wind folded four times. open carefully in the pantry and the flour will remember to become snow in January. please kiss the clock over the stove; it works less when i am gone.

faithfully your wrong-handed child (who writes crooked and loves straight),

i
