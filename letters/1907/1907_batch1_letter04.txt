Coal Bloom

Date: 1907-04-12
Recipient: Mother
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1907
Themes: family, city, work, homesickness, industrial

Dearest Mother,

the trains here invent afternoons from iron. At the station i watched a cinder settle in a girl’s hair and become a star before she brushed it away—there are galaxies in our small neglects. Chicago is all blunt knuckles and smoke; yet, as dusk lowers its shawl, the monstrous mills begin to purr like barn cats and the streets remember that they once were prayer.

I am eating well enough (please do not send the tonic again; the last bottle befriended the drain). Mrs. Adler’s boarding house smells of boiled potatoes and suspicion. The wallpaper is green with ambitions of being a garden. Each night a violin from the room above convinces a pack of chairs to dance. In such conditions, even loneliness keeps polite hours.

I brought the handkerchief you hemmed—its corner finds my wrist whenever i am near the dangerous edges of doubt. Your stitches speak, Mother: do not be grand; be true. I try. Work has been mostly letters for others, hammered from a rented typewriter whose keys are small iron gravestones. Even grief becomes neat when i line it up in rows.

If the lilacs in our yard bloom early, write me of their scandal. Tell Father i have learned to knot a tie without swearing (mostly), and that Chicago geese are uglier than ours but travel with enviable impudence.

Kiss the cat on the slippery part of his forehead. Keep the kettle moral.

Your son who keeps coal where his roses should be,
A.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
