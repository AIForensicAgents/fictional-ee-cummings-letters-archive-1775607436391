River-sorry, lantern-warm

Date: 1894-09-05
Recipient: Mother
Location: New Orleans, Louisiana
Year: 1894
Themes: apology, music, home, river, forgiveness

Dear Mother,

I have held back from writing, to see if silence might do my confessing for me. It did not. The river does not keep secrets; it tells them in a low, repeated music that makes a fool of pride. So here I am with ink on my fingers and your patience on my mind.

You were right: a good coat and a sure heart are both kinds of shelter. Mine has been thin some nights. The cornet pays for coffee, for a corner in a room that smells of lantern oil and chicory. When I play, the brass warms, and what leaves the bell is half-prayer, half-laughter. Men who will not speak of home put their hands on the table and remember a little. I put my coins in this envelope—they clink like small apologies. Take them, however poorly I have earned them.

If I disappointed you by stepping out of the good black shoes you chose, forgive me. The boat whistles and my bones answer; I cannot help answering. Yet every note wants to be a letter to you (dear, and dear again). I bought a handkerchief with blue thread, the exact blue of your eyes when you would not scold me. It lives in my pocket and knows the names you called me as a child.

Tell me of the cat, of the clean line of laundry against the sky, of whether the lilacs remembered to bloom without me there to count them. I will come by Christmas if the river loosens its hand. I am your son still; the proof is the ache.

Ever your son,

Nathaniel

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
