A margins-light manifesto

Date: 1894-06-21
Recipient: Madame Lys Valade
Location: Montmartre, Paris
Year: 1894
Themes: art, rebellion, city, submission, summer

Dear Madame Lys,

This quarter hour just before the lamps are persuaded to blossom is my favorite: the city holds its breath and I take it for my own. I have written until my wrists shine with graphite and impatience. Please accept these pages that smell faintly of tobacco, ink, and a window I could not resist opening.

I am done with obedient lines. Let the sentence stumble where it must to discover its true gait. The cobblestones have taught me that nothing straight is entirely honest; even the steps up to Sacré-Cœur confess a curve if you watch them with a night in your pocket. The baker downstairs bangs his pans like cymbals and the dough rises without our permission: this too is a manifesto.

Your last letter asked for clarity. I offer instead a lantern. Hold it low. You will see the shoes of the poem hesitating, then choosing a puddle as if it were a mirror. Between the street violin and the butcher's shouts, a thin ribbon of quiet ties the morning to itself. I have written there (on the ribbon) where only the careful eye can read.

If you wish to print them, do. If not, keep them near your kettle and let steam make the words more tender. Either way, I continue. Everything I love has scratches on it: tables, faces, phrases, sky. I trust the scratched things; they have wrestled with their names.

Respectfully and recklessly,

Julien Ardent

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
