a letter to the river that taught us to breathe

Date: 1940-08-19
Recipient: Ruth
Location: Santa Fe, New Mexico
Year: 1940
Themes: nature, art, sisterhood, memory, summer

dear ruth,

the afternoon here is a wide hat thrown onto the desert and everything sits in its shade—the lizards, the painter with sunburned ears, the tin roof that reads rain the way a palm reader reads cities. even the dust has a grammar; it conjugates thirst in every tense.

i took the path down to the arroyo where water sometimes remembers itself. today it was a rumor, silver in a few careful places. i thought of the river at home, the one that taught us how to float by pretending to be careless. do you remember how we would let our bodies be opinions the current was having? i swear i heard it speaking here through cottonwoods, a green whisper like a letter without stamps.

my studio is a room with a window that eats mountains. i paint with colors that make their own weather: ultramarine (cool elbow), ochre (bread heel), a red that refuses to apologize. i think you were right—canvas is a good argument to have with one’s heart. every brush is a soft knife, every line a way of asking the sky to sign its name.

the nights are a silver grammar too; the train sighs over the far tracks and coyotes punctuate the dark with commas. i cook beans, i lose track of the moon, i learn to mend with turquoise thread because the plain kind seems untrue. sometimes a storm steps over the mountain like a tall aunt come suddenly to visit, her skirts heavy with opinions and rain. afterward the whole town smells of clean tin and apology.

if you come (please come), bring the sound our mother makes when she laughs even though she is annoyed—i cannot mix that color without you. also, the recipe for those cakes that pretend to be clouds. i have a spare cot and a jar that always believes it will be filled with flowers again.

until then, i send you a small packet of sand that insists on being sunlight even in an envelope. hold it up to your ear; you will hear our river breathing in another language and still saying our names.

with paint on my wrists and patience in my pocket,
—e.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
