blackout hymn (for the room next door)

Date: 1940-10-07
Recipient: Elias
Location: London, W.C.1
Year: 1940
Themes: war, fear, neighborliness, resilience, night

dear elias,

the wardens have hooded the streetlamps and every window has learned to hold its breath. paper crosses the glass like a quiet prayer; even the kettle whispers. you cough (two rooms away) and the whole house nods—yes, we are here, yes, we are not alone.

sirens practice their terrible music, and then the night lowers like a helmet. i pour tea into a chipped cup that remembers summer; it tastes faintly of dust, slightly of courage. the building across the lane is a dark tooth, and we are the tongue flexing around it, careful, alive.

there is a hum above the roof (it might be bees if bees had swallowed matches). i find myself counting not seconds but soft things: the buttons on my coat, the freckles on my left hand, the thoughts that go nowhere and keep me warm. i think of you leaning against the wall with your sleeves rolled and your eyes learning each creak of the stair. we are ferocious small animals dressed as people.

if the worst should fall (it may, it may not), remember the trick of mornings: when the all-clear unties the sky, we will open the curtains like wounds remembering how to become windows. the milkman will arrange his ghosts of bottles on the stoop, and the paper will confess to more than it can explain. we will laugh too loudly at nothing. we will sweep the stairwell as though it were a stage.

in case the ceiling decides to change geography, i have placed my name on a folded scrap under your door—pocket-sized proof. keep it with your matches. and if the night asks whom you belong to, tell it: i belong to the little lamp three doors down that refuses to die, to the shared wall that keeps our two breaths arguing sweetly, to your cough that sounds (forgive me) exactly like a shy dog at the sea.

hush now. listen: even fear has a lullaby when we hold it by both hands.

in the blackout’s gentle mouth,
—m
