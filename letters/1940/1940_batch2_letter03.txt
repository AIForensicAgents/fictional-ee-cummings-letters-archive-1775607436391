unsent telegram to my mother (held like breath)

Date: 1940-06-12
Recipient: Mother
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1940
Themes: family, distance, news, work, becoming

dear ma,

i meant to wire you (STOP) but the clerk’s window was full of afternoon and i could not bear to break it (STOP). so here is a slower electricity: a page, my hand, the lake keeping its blue shoulders still as if posing for a photograph.

the shop hums—linotype iron bees, paper drifting down like tired snow. i set someone else’s words into neat rows and In Between my fingers i try to smuggle my own: how are the beans, and is the cat still vain, and does the porch remember june? if you listen hard you can hear the radio climbing the stairs; it drags a sack of names. dunkirk. amiens. a city i cannot pronounce. and paris (you always wanted to go)—paris is holding its coat with both hands and the wind wants it.

i am eating well enough. the boardinghouse serves a stew that could fix a watch. i have mended two shirts and the hole in my thinking where home used to whistle through. sometimes, at the stoplight on clark, i practice my face for being the sort of person who does not break when spoken to sharply. it is a work in progress; the light changes; i cross.

please do not worry about the draft of my future—i have weighted the corners with small honest things: the orange you sent (i kept the peel for its brave perfume), the way you taught me to fold a sheet so it fits the drawer like a well-behaved cloud, the recipe for never saying all of what hurts. i am alone but not lonely, which i think is the city’s secret handshake.

if the news grows teeth, i will bite back with a postcard and a train ticket. until then, let’s share a superstition: whenever the nine o’clock whistle startles the pigeons, it means we have been spared one more hour of foolishness. kiss your knuckles and put them in your apron pocket for luck.

milk the morning for me. tell the hawthorn not to rush. and if the dog from ramsey street sneaks over, let him in; he always knew the way to forgiveness.

with both sleeves rolled,
—your son who is trying to grow correctly

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
