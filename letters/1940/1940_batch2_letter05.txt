dispatch with a thistle enclosed

Date: 1940-12-03
Recipient: Mr. Caldwell
Location: Glasgow, Scotland
Year: 1940
Themes: submission, industry, winter, war-shadow, craft

dear mr. caldwell,

i am sending three poems typed with a ribbon the color of last week’s bruise. forgive the smudges; coal dust has its own editorial policy and cannot be reasoned with. the shipyards yawn before dawn and the men go in with their shoulders like doors—stout, necessary, quietly heroic. i count rivets by their sound and later the words arrive with the same hammering grace.

there is a thistle pressed between page two and three (do not touch too quickly; it remembers being fierce). i include it not as a threat to your fingers but as a small biography. we are tidy enough in winter, the city cinched with blackout curtains, but underneath we are purple and stubborn and slightly laughing. the sirens dress the streets in silence and the silence fits badly; seams let through singing. someone in our tenement owns a harmonium that leaks hymn in the stairwell. praise be to air that insists on music, even rationed.

you asked last spring for lines “less private, more public.” i have tried to widen my voice without thinning it; to speak as a we without telling lies about the i. these pieces carry the river clyde in their boots and a little engine grease beneath the nails. they are about kettles and widows and the way hope prefers work clothes. if they still seem too much like letters to a friend, perhaps it is because that is what the city feels like at four a.m.—a large tired friend who will not stop making ships.

payment can be in copies, or in your good opinion, or in neither; i will continue regardless. but if you print them, please keep the line breaks the way a spine keeps its secrets. each gap is a held breath. each small space says: wait, there is another footstep on the stair.

i will collect whatever answer you send at the post office where the clerk’s moustache believes in monarchy. should you refuse me, i will plant the refusal on my sill like an onion and let it grow green and honest. should you accept, i will buy sardines to celebrate, and feed a cat that doesn’t belong to me but visits like weather.

with a thistle, and respect,
—j. m.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
