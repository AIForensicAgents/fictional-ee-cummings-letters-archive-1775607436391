schedule of stars

Date: 1935-07-30
Recipient: Mr. Lionel Crane, stationmaster
Location: Flagstaff, Arizona
Year: 1935
Themes: night, travel, work, wonder, solitude

mr crane,

i have misplaced the timetable but kept the whistle. perhaps that is all right; the night has its own schedule, printed on the undersides of crickets. a train and a constellation are cousins, both arriving where they are expected, both understood by points of light and small, exact noises.

the platform boards keep a ledger of every heel and luggage sorrow. tonight a woman with a green hat waited with a patience that bruised my chest to look at. when the limited slid in, the hat trembled and the station did something i would call prayer if stations believed in anything but clocks.

i have discovered that chalk writes more beautifully after midnight. i write our departures with a tilted tenderness; the g in chicago droops like a tired shoulder. i hope you will not scold me. sometimes the towns need a little mercy in their spelling.

you asked why i linger after last car. because the tracks keep singing a long, thin note that knows my name. because the red lamp at the end of the train blinks twice (as if to say, you matter) and then bites the dark clean in half. because the sky over the freight yard is a low auditorium full of excellent listeners.

when you lock up, leave me one key of silence, top row, third from left. i will return it in the morning, polished by a few bright dreams.

respectfully (and a little late),

h.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
