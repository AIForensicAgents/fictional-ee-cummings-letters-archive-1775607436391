nocturne with streetcar

Date: 1935-03-03
Recipient: Miss Lucille Hart, pianist
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1935
Themes: music, city, intimacy, night, humor

lucille,

i rode the streetcar to the river's edge where the wind keeps its metronome buried deep in the iron. the conductor had a modest mustache and the sort of hands that understand tickets are only paper disguises for people's destinations. each bell the car rang was a small moon hitting a window in the dark.

your letter smelled faintly of varnish, as if the piano itself had written me with two wide, careful thumbs. you say the c is sulking and the d sharp has developed an opinion about weather. i approve. notes should be citizens with messy hair and scandalous socks. tonight i tried to whistle a melody for your left hand but the wind kept breaking it into friendly accidents.

bring your piano a bouquet of traffic, will you? the tires hiss and the corners bristle. i can almost hear how your hands might persuade this loud city to speak low and intimate, as if confessing to a lamppost. i would like to sit near that lamppost, collar up, pretending not to listen.

a boy selling peanuts said to me, ma'am, the night eats what it cannot pronounce. so here: take this little tin of silence, seasoned with streetlight and a pinch of river. open it before you play. if anything in it trembles, it is my listening.

faithfully (and slightly off key),

a.
