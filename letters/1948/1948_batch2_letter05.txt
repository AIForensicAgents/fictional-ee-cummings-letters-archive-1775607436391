desert letter with a blue door

Date: 1948-11-21
Recipient: Lydia
Location: Santa Fe, New Mexico
Year: 1948
Themes: solitude, art, landscape, healing, transformation

dear lydia,

the desert is a clock that forgot the numbers and kept the hands. days turn and turn, a quiet ballet of shade, and the mountains stand with their sleeves rolled up, patient as carpenters. i rented a small house with a blue door that keeps learning to be sky; when i close it, the afternoon stays politely on the step.

i bought pigments from a man whose shop smells of juniper and superstition. he wrapped the colors in newspaper that still argues about politics, and when i grind the red it remembers some volcano far away from headlines. my brush is a humble animal; it drinks, it speaks, it sleeps. when it dreams, your face appears like rain arriving where no one thought to plant.

at night the radio spills a river of voices across the table and i cup my hands to drink. somewhere a saxophone invents a new word for winter; somewhere a DJ mispronounces my future. coyotes step into the sound between stations and make a country of it. i hang my coat on a nail and the wall accepts the weight the way a good friend does, without commentary.

if you came here, i would take you to the hill where telephone wires sing. we would listen until silence was something we wore easily, and then we would speak, not to fill it, but to decorate. bring the scarf with the moth-hole. i have discovered how wind can mend what needles cannot, by teaching it to move.

i am well. i am becoming the person my shadows trust. write when the first snow in the city surprises you like a gentleman with flowers. i will answer in turquoise, in sage, in the long yes of the road.

yours in the clear grammar of distance,
j.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
