Widow's Apron, Unstarched

Date: 1899-04-16
Recipient: Mrs. Ines Duarte
Location: San Antonio, Texas
Year: 1899
Themes: grief, consolation, household, faith, neighborliness

dear mrs. duarte,

today i passed your gate and the hinge kept his manners: it did not cry out. perhaps the world is being kind because your house cannot carry more sound. i send this letter instead of footsteps, so you may open it when the day becomes too heavy to lift whole.

grief is a needle that refuses a thimble. it pricks, then pauses, then pricks again when you are pouring coffee or tying your hair. still, even a stubborn seam comes true by patience. i know you will not starch your apron for a while. let it hang, a soft flag of truce with the mornings.

his pipe on the sill looked as if smoke might return by habit. the chair has remembered the exact depth of his laughter; chairs do that. the sun crossed your floor at noon and did not hurry, and i took comfort in that—light has its duties and keeps them, even now.

when people come, permit them to carry the small tasks: the coal, the peels, the folding. when people go, permit the silence to sit beside you without explaining itself. speak to him, if speaking is a kind of breathing (it is). the ear of heaven is not made of brass; it is warm and near.

sunday i will bring oranges and no advice. if you wish, we shall peel them and make a bright snow on your plate. we will sit, and not improve the sorrow, only accompany it until it decides its own housekeeping.

your neighbor, with stitched-up care,

rosalía.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
