Under the Gaslamp, the Smallest Courage

Date: 1899-01-12
Recipient: Miss Eliza Hart
Location: Boston, Massachusetts
Year: 1899
Themes: courtship, winter, city, courage, anticipation

dear eliza,

this evening the common wore its snow like a hush, and the gaslamps braided their gold into the quiet. the trolley bell was a thin coin of sound, spent and spent again, and still the night did not grow poor. i walked from the printing office with ink on my cuffs, the warm breath of the presses still inside my coat, and your last note folded like a white bird in my pocket. my hands, shy birds themselves, kept flying back to it.

i have thought a quantity of unnumbered thoughts about our last conversation. how the window fogged when we spoke of nothing in particular, and how that nothing became a sort of cathedral. i am too small for such architecture, and yet i go on kneeling in it. if courage were a coin, i would spend it at your door; if courage were a hat, i would tip it every time your name passed my lips (eliza, eliza, the syllables are a procession of lanterns along a winter street).

i will come sunday, if the weather is not frankly murderous. the market may have violets; if it does not, i will bring the thunder of my quiet heart. we might walk where the river keeps its little black secrets under the skin of ice, and you may laugh at my earnestness which is not a disease, only a habit of soul. somewhere between our steps the future will lace her boots and look away, pretending she is not listening.

i asked the typesetter to save the smallest letters for me, because i intend to speak to you not loudly but precisely. sometimes the smallest letters say the most faithful things.

yours, deliberately and with small astonishments,

nathan
