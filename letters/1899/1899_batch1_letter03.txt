Small Astronomy in a Parlor

Date: 1899-03-28
Recipient: Professor Elena Marlowe
Location: Baltimore, Maryland
Year: 1899
Themes: science, wonder, time, imagination, intimacy

dear professor marlowe,

i have made a small astronomy in the parlor. the teacup receives a moon (a slice of lemon) and the teaspoon becomes a silver comet i persuade into elliptical manners. dust—respectable citizens all—float into constellations when the sun of the window leans its shoulder through the curtains.

the pendulum of the tall clock does not swing; it argues in a language i almost understand: now/then, now/then. i sit between them with a notebook and a biscuit (half eaten, like a waning gibbous), and i swear time lowered its voice to confide the orbit of my own heart.

forgive my play; your lectures wake a child in me who ties planets to buttons and calls it learning. outside, the alley cat recites equations with his whole body, arched and certain, while the newsboy announces disaster and penny-love in the same brave pitch. even calamity has regular seasons.

i long for your next letter with figures that kiss the margin and run away laughing. send me the star you cannot name yet, or the mistake that turns into discovery while your lamp blinks. i will keep both as one keeps violets in a book: pressed, fragrant, a little blue persistence.

should you visit, i will brew tea until venus rises in the kettle. we will put our faces to the glass and watch winter’s breath invent nebulae.

in admiration, and with a parlor full of planets,

agnes.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
