winter telegram without a wire

Date: 1952-12-05
Recipient: Samuel Reed, Editor
Location: Boston, Massachusetts
Year: 1952
Themes: art, winter, courage, editing, individuality

samuel,
forgive the train-stained paper; the conductor punched my ticket into cold confetti, and it is snowing even in my pockets. my manuscript sits beside me like a small animal i am trying to keep warm under brown paper and breath.

the city this morning wore a tie of slate; its buildings cleared their throats into a gray handkerchief. at the station, men in careful coats discussed the acceptable sizes of thoughts. i smiled politely and tucked my unsizes deeper inside. you once told me a draft is a house with all the doors open—i have left them so, but tacked bells to the hinges that jingle when cowardice tries to come in.

enclosed you will find the courage of a second draft (creased, but breathing). i have kept the sentences that know their own names and taken the dull scissors to those that only wanted to be famous. the ending remains a shy animal; it will let you touch its ears, not yet its back. i thought of telegramming you: stop. but there is hardly a stop in me tonight, only small soft commas settling like birds on a wire that does not exist.

if there are committee rooms for minds like this, let them keep their lamps. my lamp is a coffee cup and a window where the winter has written yes in its breath three times.

should you find on page nineteen a paragraph that dares the weather, do not punish it. it is my proof that december can be taught to speak human. i await your marginalia, those thin, brave roads you draw through snowfields.

faithfully and half-frozen,
marian

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
