unsent (but i send it anyway)

Date: 1952-10-21
Recipient: Miss Evangeline Hart, Librarian
Location: Santa Fe, New Mexico
Year: 1952
Themes: longing, books, desert, restraint, transformation

miss hart,
i will likely fold this and hide it between atlas and atlas, where the deserts keep their vowels under glass. if it finds you, it will be by accident's careful hand. the afternoon grew high and blue as a bell, and the adobe walls wore their old, kind heat; even the dust seemed to be writing something with its small, bright feet.

your hands at the desk are two quiet republics. when you stamp the cards, each date lands like a heartbeat, a promise that paper can remember. i have been learning to be a citizen of your aisles: index fingers blackened by soft alphabets, spine after spine bowing and unbowing like reeds at a river that only looks like silence.

i tried to say this with the cold machinery of a typewriter, but the o key lost its nerve and made moons instead of letters. besides, the ribbon turned the sentence into an evening, and who can read an evening without turning their head. it is easier to confess with a pencil that smells of cedar and running away.

yesterday you shelved the poets and a single hair escaped your careful storm. it hung in the light like a small road leading into a country where no one is lonely. i wanted to follow. i did not. instead i borrowed a book that teaches patience and returned it empty, having used it all on your doorway.

if this never reaches you, it has already arrived: i am different. some combination of sun and dust and the sure architecture of your gaze has changed the weather in me. should i see you tomorrow (and i will, because the card catalogue keeps telling me we are neighbors), i will say only good afternoon, which in the old languages meant: the heart opens like a window and does not break.

unposted but true,
a pilgrim of the stacks

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
