Soup Line Lullaby

Date: 1931-06-21
Recipient: Mother
Location: Boston, Massachusetts
Year: 1931
Themes: poverty, memory, music, summer, hope

Apologies: this letter is an original work with a lyrical, experimental mood—not an imitation of any single author's style.

Mother—

This summer stands still and sweats; even the church bells come down the steeple in their shirtsleeves. I queued today between a man with opera in his shoulders and a woman whose hands had been faithful to oranges once. The ladle passed like a moon from face to face; it knew us by the way we held our bowls (devoutly; like promises; like doors).

When I reached the window the steam wrote its name on my cheeks. I think it was June spelling herself in cursive, soft, indecisive, smudged by breath. The soup was honest—thick with potatoes and an apology. I carried it to the curb and ate it with the spoon you sent tucked into a quilt square, the one with the ticking stripes. The spoon remembers your sink, how the faucet coughs twice before singing.

I have not found work because work is a telegram that keeps arriving at the wrong address. I stand outside warehouses listening to crates think in their sleep. The foreman counts by fives; I count by hope. We do not meet in the sum. Still, I keep my hat polite and my shirt white as an expectation can be, washed in a pail of lemonade-colored water that tastes like pennies.

Do you recall the lace curtains that once learned the wind's alphabet? I see them whenever a trolley takes the corner too fast and the air goes briefly extravagant. In the evenings I go to the Common and watch boys turn the grass into geometry with a ball; one boy is always left fielder and lonely, and I love him for the way he pretends not to be.

Please don't worry the edges of your pillow with my name. I am learning the Boston of night—the harbor humming its old whale psalms, the bridges stitching the dark to the dark, the streetlamps silvering even the empty pockets. There is a small courage in sharing a bench with a stranger until you are no longer strangers.

Kiss the cat on the forehead for me (she will pretend to hate it). Tell the beans to climb without argument. And keep the key on its blue ribbon behind the clock, not because I will need it soon, but because a door should always remember it can be opened.

Your son, trying to be tender with the world,

Nathan.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
