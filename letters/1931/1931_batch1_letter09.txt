cornsilk elegy

Date: 1931-10-12
Recipient: Aunt Mae
Location: Iowa City
Year: 1931
Themes: family, loss, harvest, memory, consolation

dear aunt mae,

the fields have put their gold away and wear only the brown workdress. a wind with a dry mouth keeps telling the same old story to the barns. i walked past uncle's scarecrow; he has finally learned the posture of a sermon.

your letter arrived with the news that sits down before it speaks. i read it at the depot among suitcases that pretend patience. grief is a tidy word for such a complicated room. i wanted to sweep, to arrange, to polish the silence, but the silence had other plans.

do you remember when we shelled peas on the porch and the bowl became a green moon? you laughed your low laugh and the dusk leaned closer, eager for lessons. i keep that night folded in my pocket, the cloth worn thin where i worry it.

i cannot come before the frost, though i ache toward you. the trains are busy carrying other people's emergencies. take this instead: a small parade of sentences, their hats in their hands. let them pass your window and wave. let them fail, as they must, and forgive them, as you have always forgiven the crooked and the earnest.

i send my arms by way of this paper. i send my name to fetch water and wood. say it once, quietly, and it will go do the chore.

your nephew,
n.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
