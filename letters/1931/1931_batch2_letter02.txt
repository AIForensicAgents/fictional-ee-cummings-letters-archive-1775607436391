Dust Prayers

Date: 1931-04-03
Recipient: Eli (my brother)
Location: Garden City, Kansas
Year: 1931
Themes: family, hardship, dust, resilience, midwest

Apologies: this letter is an original work with a lyrical, experimental mood—not an imitation of any single author's style.

Eli—

The land has learned a rough song: wind, and the grammar of wind, and the dark punctuation of crows. Yesterday I watched the horizon swallow a granary like a patient animal—no hunger, only the steady mouth of March turned April. There are no real edges here, only the idea of edges, which the sky argues with until we forget to answer.

Ma asked after you. I told her your hands still smell of motor oil and rain that hasn't fallen yet. I didn't tell her about the sound the barn makes at night, like an enormous book being closed slowly by no one. We keep our jars we thought were for peaches and fill them with dust, just to measure how much of the world has decided to visit.

It is not all dry. This morning, Ruth laughed and the kitchen went briefly green. We wore our good shoes to the well (ceremony of leather; congregation of mud that isn't). I turned the crank and thought of trains: how they claim the whole country in sections, metal hymns laid down through thirst. The timetable you sent is thumb-soft in my pocket. Every departure looks like a promise breaking into useful pieces.

You said once you could fix anything that turned in circles. Fix me, then: my thoughts wheel and wheel; my breath has become a small bicycle for a child who does not yet exist. I want to say: come home, we will plant and pretend. I want also to say: don't, the pretending is bitter as chicory, the planting is a deck of cards the wind knows how to shuffle.

If you write, make the paper carry some of your city soot. I will shake it over the seed sacks, a new kind of blessing. Tell me how the streetlights in Topeka argue with the moths. Tell me whether the diner on Fourth still salts the eggs with laughter. And if the river there remembers its curves, send us that memory in a jar; we'll open it when the sky forgets itself again.

Your brother who has become a thin brown prayer,

Sam.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
