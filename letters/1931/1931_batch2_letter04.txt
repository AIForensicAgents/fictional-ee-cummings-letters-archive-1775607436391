Seine Postcard in a Brown Envelope

Date: 1931-09-10
Recipient: Vera L.
Location: Paris, France
Year: 1931
Themes: art, friendship, travel, autumn, ink

Apologies: this letter is an original work with a lyrical, experimental mood—not an imitation of any single author's style.

Vera—

Consider this a long postcard. The river is a long answer. Every bridge says yes and no simultaneously, which is to say—Paris. I saw a woman cross the Pont Neuf with a red umbrella though the day was arid; perhaps she was watering the sky. I followed her shadow until it led me into a bookshop where the air tasted bravely of paper and coffee and a bell that rings when you don't deserve it.

I am painting streets that refuse to hold still. They keep winking at the sun, which keeps flirting with the windows, which keep gossiping to the floors. Pigments: bone black, vermilion that behaves like opera, a green insincere as pistachio ice. My canvas is a polite riot. I have learned to leave some mistakes uncorrected; they become windows as soon as you stop scolding them.

Evenings: cheap wine in tall glasses, a loaf that remembers wheat, and a table we pretend is ours. The waiter knows all our modest lies ("we are important later"), and forgives them with a shrug like a velvet curtain. Once, a violin began somewhere you could not see and ended in everyone's pocket at once.

Write and tell me if the leaves started falling in your street like well-bred letters when September remembers its manners. Tell me if the night train still bothers your roof with its late confessions. I want to send you the smell of this city when it rains on stones—the nose has its cathedrals too—but the envelope would give up.

There is a pale blue scarf in a shop window I do not enter. It has the same almost-color as the morning we watched the ocean refuse to end. If I buy it, it will become a scarf; if I don't, it can remain that morning's quiet argument with forever. Some things work better as weather than as clothing.

All my affection, folded carefully along the creases of this page,

Clara.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
