widow's letter folded like wings

Date: 1896-08-09
Recipient: Mr. Arthur Vale (in memoriam)
Location: St. Louis, Missouri
Year: 1896
Themes: grief, memory, domestic life, hope, ritual

arthur,—

the undertaker has put away his formal hands, and still i hear them closing around the afternoon. i have been courteous with my sorrow for a month, setting it a plate, asking about its day; it answers only in creak and weight. our bed (obedient ship) keeps the shape of voyages we will not make, so i lie upon the shore of its silence and let the headboard translate the tides.

i took your hat from the peg and wore it through the market like a small brown moon upon my hair. people nodded to the hat's memory and not to me; it was a relief. peaches this year are so sincere they bruise at the idea of kindness. i bought six and arranged them on the windowsill where they could watch the afternoon improve itself. one leapt (suicidal optimist) and met the floor with fragrance, which is a kind of forgiveness.

our neighbors—bless their good intentions braided with noise—arrive with custards, with advice to travel, to pray, to embroider. i embroider, yes: your name into the edges of towels you never saw; the thread gleams briefly then returns to itself, like a small obedient future. travel? i open the parlor door and the rug becomes a foreign country. pray? i have the excellent habit of arguing with God until the argument equals love.

i went to the river at dusk where boys set fireflies into jars as if courting constellations. the bridge (stubborn violin) held a caged music until the first star unscrewed it. i said your name aloud and the water altered its skin. hear me: i am not alone; i am accompanied by your precise absence, which arranges chairs, declines tea, sets down and takes up the newspaper with infuriating grace.

the morning after the funeral, the clock forgot itself, and for one perfect unpunctuated hour the house was a field. i walked through that tall grass of untime and felt you walking beside me—just the sleeve of you, brushing. then the bell remembered and every blade became a minute again. i am obedient to minutes; i am also their contrarian.

sleep, if sleep is a word where you are; be unbuttoned of all pain. i will keep the porch lamp low and the door only mostly latched. should you find yourself with an armful of wind and a very patient moon, do visit—as a moth, perhaps, or as that thin sound the kettle makes just before boiling. i shall leave you room in the cup.

always (a word that misbehaves, and i love it),

clara
