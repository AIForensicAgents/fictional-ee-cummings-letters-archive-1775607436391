a cartographer's small defiance (to the printer)

Date: 1896-10-21
Recipient: Mr. Horace Biddle, Printer
Location: Portland, Maine
Year: 1896
Themes: art and craft, naming, nature, precision, imagination

dear mr. biddle,—

to the matter of names: your tidy press prefers obedient letters, but coastlines are all confession and no grammar. in the proofs you returned, the bay has been persuaded into a sober parenthesis; yet yesterday, under a weather practicing its italics, i watched the water steal out of its margins and annotate the rocks with foam. accuracy, then, must include astonishment.

please adjust the legend accordingly: where it reads "shoal," let it say "shoal (when tired)"; where "sound," add "(listens)." i request also, in the lower right, a little white unprinted place for fog to sign its name (it is a reliable correspondent). some will call this frivolous; i call it a map that believes its own breathing.

i enclose, between sheets of waxed paper, a leaf collected from a salt meadow west of biddeford. the veins are so certain they embarrass my rulers. imagine, if you will, an atlas that consents to be a little leaf—its countries connected by something more articulate than border, its borders permitting a very polite blur. you will say (rightly) that merchants cannot sail by metaphor; i answer that they already do, calling it rumor, calling it weather.

your last letter suggested a darker ink. i agree. the ocean will benefit by gravity of tone. but allow the islands a paler courage; they are shy and prefer to be discovered only after the eye has learned to whisper. and the compass rose—oh keep it slightly crooked, as if bowing to the wind that will not read it.

on the harbor this morning, a woman with red gloves sold newspapers proclaiming fog, then sunshine, then election results, then fog again. the gulls edited all this into a single margin note: hunger. i am attempting an edition that admits hunger without apologizing for appetite. let the paper hold this.

when you strike the press, listen: the gears recite a psalm of steel and ink, and somewhere in that machinery a cartographer repeats (softly stubborn): here, and here, and here. he is not drawing land but the agreement between footsteps and the dreamed distance.

yours in the margins,

edgar s. millay (no kin to the poet; only a cousin to the sea)

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
