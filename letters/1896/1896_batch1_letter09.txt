on blue stationery (with violets)

Date: 1896-10-02
Recipient: The Editor of The Beacon
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1896
Themes: art, ambition, industry, critique, night, printing

dear Editor,
i submit three poems and a confession: i have never seen a printing press without wanting to kneel. its teeth are honest, its breath metallic but persevering; it believes that darkness can be persuaded into speaking legibly. if that is not faith, name me a better.

the city roars in its sleep, soot on the pillow; somewhere a violin argues with a fence. i write in the blue hour when pigeons become polite and the lake rehearses stillness. these verses are ordinary birds which, despite their feet, have remembered the sky.

you will say, perhaps, that the lines are improper, that commas behave like children just released. you are right. nevertheless consider the heart’s punctuation—the dash between two strangers on a train, the colon that promises an answer and then, shamelessly, delivers another question. i work in that grammar.

if you must decline, do so beautifully; i collect refusals the way sailors gather charts, not to worship the reefs but to know where not to land twice. and if you accept, i will not pretend indifference. i will walk past your window at midnight and thank the ink, though it sleeps.

respectfully and with unruly hope,
T.
