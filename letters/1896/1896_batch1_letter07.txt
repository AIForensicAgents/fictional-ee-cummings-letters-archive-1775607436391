a mourning dove at noon

Date: 1896-07-09
Recipient: Cousin Margaret
Location: St. Louis, Missouri
Year: 1896
Themes: grief, family, heat, memory, ritual, consolation

dear Margaret,
the noon bell wore black today. i had not known a sound could veil itself. Aunt Ellen’s room keeps the shape of her; the mirror refuses a face, offering instead a window my heart is not yet ready to open.

i am learning the choreography of casseroles and condolence—how to place a hand so it says here without saying hold on forever. in the yard a mourning dove keeps practicing the same syllable, a tender failure that feels like success. the heat leans upon the porch rail, heavy as a cousin you love and cannot move.

do you remember when she taught us to fold napkins into swans? my fingers were clumsy; hers forgave. now the linen learns water and becomes brief clouds in the basin. grief is a similar laundering—the world wrung and wrung until the drip admits a cleaner air. i am not there yet.

come if you can. bring the small stories that make spoon-noises: her red thread; the way she read recipes as if summoning weather; the hatpin with the missing pearl (we will pretend it simply became the moon). if you cannot come, place your thought upon this page. i will feel it, cool as a church step.

i keep the windows open at night; perhaps the dove will tuck itself inside and sleep.

with love and a quiet yes,
N.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
