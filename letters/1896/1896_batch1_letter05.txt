wildflowers in the ledger's margin

Date: 1896-05-27
Recipient: Mr. Ezra Pike, Bookseller
Location: Concord, Massachusetts
Year: 1896
Themes: literature, commerce, desire, spring, ink, margins

dear Mr. Ezra Pike, Bookseller,
i enclose a list, though what i desire may be unlistable. still: a Ruskin for rainy contrition, a thin Shelley for pocket weather, any little heresy printed with clean type and the smell of brave ink. if you have a book that has taught itself to breathe quietly in the presence of doubt, send that as well.

forgive the pressed petals between the lines. they are not payment, only proof that margins bloom when no one is looking. the clerk at the bank frowned as my arithmetic leafed; nevertheless the ledger accepted two buttercups and one sigh. spring is extravagant, and i am its minor accomplice.

when i stepped into your shop last week the bell made a sound like a timid blossom, and you, with your spectacles rowing your eyes toward me, asked if i sought novels or truth. i said yes. you did not laugh; thank you. a person who will not bruise a joke is rare.

invoice me gently; send by the slow post that tattles of other hands. and if by chance a note should hide among the chapters (no accounting entry, only a sentence in your wrist’s weather), i will consider the debt increased to a very pleasant seriousness.

most sincerely, in a disorder of violets,
S.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
