whistle, then prairie

Date: 1943-09-08
Recipient: Inez
Location: Troop Train, Nebraska
Year: 1943
Themes: love, journey, anticipation, america, war

inez—

we are a long iron sentence crossing the open book of grass. every telegraph pole is a comma that refuses to end us. nebraska keeps happening: barns like red knuckles, windmills arguing with the sky, cows standing in the exact shape of patience.

in the car the air tastes of wool and cardamom—someone's mother hid cookies in a sock. we play cards for imaginary peaches. a boy from ohio keeps polishing his boots as if tomorrow is a mirror. when we sleep, it is with our names pinned inside our collars, in case the night forgets.

this train writes your name on the dark every time its whistle turns into a hand. i have folded your photograph so the crease is your smile; it opens like a door when the light finds it. the chaplain says we are all instruments, and god is learning our stubborn music. i think i am more like a match—useful, sudden, small.

somewhere ahead a place will teach us new verbs. until then: fence, silo, cloud. i tuck new mexico inside my boot like a secret map—the courtyard shade, chile ristras like red punctuation, the old woman who sells sweet tamales and calls me son though i am not, the way your hair keeps the smell of piñon smoke and rain.

if we pass through denver by midnight i will count mountains in my sleep; if we do not, know that the flat world is wide enough to hold my promise. marry me when the lilacs forgive us. or sooner, if the paper will have us and the judge has a pen.

write where i can hide your words—inside my helmet, perhaps, like a second skull that believes in flowers.

all my roads to you,

d.
