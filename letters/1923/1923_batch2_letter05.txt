winter telegram to an unborn niece

Date: 1923-12-21
Recipient: Niece-to-be
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1923
Themes: family, anticipation, winter, city, tenderness, whimsy

Little comet not yet named,

Today the lake is iron and the sky keeps striking it to see what sound it makes. The stockyards exhale their tired steam and the streets write footprints in a grammar of boots. Christmas lights practice being small suns without heat. I think of you in the quiet place before breathing, cradled in your mother's winter, and the city leans closer to listen.

When you arrive, every clock will pretend it planned you. I am saving a button from my first good coat to give you, because buttons know two important tricks: how to hold things together and how to get lost. You will be brilliant at both. I promise to teach you the alphabet of puddles and the arithmetic of pigeons and the geography of trees that move in the wind without going anywhere.

If anyone tells you to be sensible, lend them your mitten and keep your hands for wonder. The world is a large conversation that needs your particular interruption. Remember: a whisper is only a shout in its pajamas.

I have bought you a tiny blue cap because winter is fond of foreheads. Your father says you kick like a telegram. Good. News should move quickly when it is good.

Arrive when you like; we will practice patience by making cinnamon toast and practicing names for snow. I suggest the following: hush, glitter, and finally.

With absurd and necessary love,
Aunt M.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
