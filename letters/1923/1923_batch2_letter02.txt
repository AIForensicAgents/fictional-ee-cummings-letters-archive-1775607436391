lilac telegram to my mother

Date: 1923-05-12
Recipient: Mother
Location: Cambridge, Massachusetts
Year: 1923
Themes: family, art, independence, nostalgia, spring, letters

Dear Mother,

The Charles has been all wrists and glitter today, waving at the poor rowing boys who cannot quite convince water to behave. The lilacs behind Mrs. Ellis's boardinghouse decide the air for everyone, which is a mercy. I write with paint under my nails because the afternoon turned into a wall I could not help but color.

Please do not worry about the rent; a small sketch of mine (a silly thing of rooftops and their private conversations) was accepted by a little paper for three dollars and the astonishment of seeing my lines walk in public. I know what you will say: that bills prefer arithmetic to astonishment. But even arithmetic must sometimes sit under a tree and count leaves, and I have promised my hands they may become what they are.

I pass the old brick every morning and its windows are full of patient professors. They speak fine words like theology and economics. Meanwhile, a sparrow on the sill knows exactly how to be a poem without syllables. I am trying to learn from both.

Do you remember the soft slap of the kitchen screen door in July? I carry it with me, a small percussion of home. When I am lonely (which is a foolish way to be in a city full of strangers, but still i manage it) I open my notebook and out tumbles the orchard and your voice saying, Eat another peach before summer forgets you.

I will come down in June if the freight of days allows. I will bring you a bouquet made of bridges and rain and the good exhaustion of finally daring. If Father asks about the bank, tell him I am making deposits in light. Interest compounding rapidly.

Your son,
Nathan

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
