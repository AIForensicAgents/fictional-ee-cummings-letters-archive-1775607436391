window, window, fast country

Date: 1923-08-30
Recipient: Evan
Location: On the Limited, between Toledo and Chicago
Year: 1923
Themes: friendship, travel, postwar, landscape, memory, hope

Evan,

Telegraph poles keep teaching the horizon its punctuation and the corn sleeps like an army that forgot which side the sun is on. Somewhere a child with a red ribbon is inventing speed out of her breath, and the train borrows it. I am pressed against the glass the way a thought leans against the skull before it jumps.

I remember us in uniforms that did not know our names, and how mud tried to write them for us. Funny thing: mud is such a slow author. The fields here are clean with wind, and I keep expecting a shell to interrupt the sentence of afternoon, but only a meadowlark bothers to be loud.

In the diner a man counts his money with the piety of a minister, and the waitress hymns coffee into all the cups. When she smiles my spoon learns to be a mirror. Across the aisle a girl is reading a novel upside-down and winning. I put my hand on the window and the prairie gives me its enormous temperature: a fever of living.

Do you still keep that scrap of blue cloth in your coat? The one that was once a flag and then a tourniquet and then, after the hospital, a sky I borrowed to sleep under? I have a foolish superstition that it keeps the world from entirely forgetting us.

We will meet in the city, you and I, where buildings practice being mountains and facades pretend not to blush at sunset. We will drink something brown and brave, and we will talk of the two or three simple things that turn out not to be simple at all. Like breathing. Like forgiveness. Like the way a whistle at 2 a.m. says both Go and Come back.

I will arrive by night, carrying the engine's heartbeat in my pockets.

Yours,
R.
