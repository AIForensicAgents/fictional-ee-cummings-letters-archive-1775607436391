Receipt for a Sunlit Afternoon

Date: 1926-07-18
Recipient: Mr. Jamison, Landlord
Location: Detroit, MI
Year: 1926
Themes: work, poverty, humor, modernity, neighborliness

Dear Mr. Jamison,

If your patience is a ledger, please turn to the page that smells of warm grease and noon. I am writing in my shirtsleeves because this city's heat broke the thermometer and is now borrowing ours.

Today the factory sang—no, ground its teeth—and I fed it bolts until the bell shook free a small ring of afternoon. Tomorrow my pay will climb the steps, coughing, and bow at your desk. This is to ask for a day, perhaps two, for it to find its breath.

In the alley a boy sold lemonade the color of a sunrise seen through smoke. I bought one, wrote your name in the condensation, and promised the glass that I would not vanish like a polite ghost when rent comes to call. There are numbers, and there is the way the light falls on the chipped sill at four o'clock; you have always trusted both better than most men.

July slicks the streets. The Fords look like beetles late for a party. We live, somehow, between the punch clock and the radio hymn, holding small uprisings of joy: a fan that remembers wind, a peach cooling in the basin, a laugh that escapes its cage at the stairwell's turn.

So I send this note ahead of me carrying what it can: the soft coin of a promise, wrapped in newspaper headlines, tied with a cord of Tuesday. Let us shake hands across Thursday. Let us say, as men who iron their own collars must, that hunger can be postponed, dignity cannot.

With respect,
Clara Finch, Apt. 3B

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
