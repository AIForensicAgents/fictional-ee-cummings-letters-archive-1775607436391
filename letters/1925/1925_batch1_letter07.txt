two a.m. with rain and electric saints

Date: 1925-09-03
Recipient: Reverend Amos Pike
Location: Kansas City, Missouri
Year: 1925
Themes: faith, doubt, urban night, mercy, patience

Reverend Pike,

i am writing from under a tin awning that sermonizes in syllables of rain. Across the street a drugstore hums with late saints—neon crosses that bless the night in pink and gold. i have been thinking about your last Sunday’s words: faith is a door without a handle on the outside.

What of those of us who wait forever on porches? Our shoes are full of weather. We knock. The only answer is thunder developing its photograph.

A woman with a hat like a careful cake just shared her umbrella. We stood two strangers under one small weather and it felt like a prayer that had learned humility. maybe that is what you mean: not certainty, but standing close enough to hear the soft gears of mercy.

There is a boy who drags a stick along the gutter, making a spilled xylophone of the curb. He is poor in the bragging way of stars—enough distance to be admired for it. i gave him a nickel which my pocket had been hoarding like a timid dragon. He nodded yes to the universe and disappeared.

i admit i am impatient with heaven’s postal service. Still, i will try your prescription: to sit in the back pew of myself and listen while the choir of dailiness warms up. Tomorrow i will sweep a floor as if it were an altar. i will forgive the mirror once.

if God is a rain that does not apologize, i will try to learn not to mind getting wet.

with a damp and honest regard,

—C.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
