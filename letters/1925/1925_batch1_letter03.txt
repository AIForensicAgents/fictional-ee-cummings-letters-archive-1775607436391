april window with blue buskers

Date: 1925-04-07
Recipient: Mother
Location: Greenwich Village, New York
Year: 1925
Themes: family, art, poverty, spring, hope

Dear Mother,

April is practicing scales on the rooftops. The pigeons applaud (softly, with their gray hands) and the organ grinder’s monkey wears a tiny coat the color of mistakes forgiven. i have rented a room like a glove—thin but mine. when the sun comes it turns the wallpaper into lemonade.

You asked if i am eating. i am eating patience and coffee. The landlady sells her soup by the syllable; i buy only the vowels. i have learned to stretch an egg into a moon by looking at it kindly.

There is a violin below my sill, played by a boy with shoes that argue with the pavement. He draws a blue ribbon from the afternoon and ties it around my throat until i sing. My poems (yes, i dare call them that today) are shy animals who creep out when the boiler snores. i catch them in a saucer, let them drink milk, and try not to name them too quickly.

Do not worry that the city will make me hard. It is a tender machine when you listen by the hinge. i stand in a doorway and watch men deliver bread like warm punctuation. The street is made of mouths. we smile with our feet.

When i miss the lilac bush by the porch at home, i close my eyes and the whole house returns, board by board. Father’s laugh like a saw; your hands kneading light into dough. i reach for that oven-warm kitchen and find my own pulse. it’s smaller but just as brave.

Send news of the hens and your wrist. is it better? i send you one blue ribbon, one egg-moon, and three kisses folded in wax paper.

Your daughter (who is learning to be a door that does not slam),

—M.
