telegram to a shoreline (unsent)

Date: 1925-06-03
Recipient: Brother Eli
Location: Provincetown, Massachusetts
Year: 1925
Themes: family, sea, belonging, courage, nature

Eli,

i did not send this by telegraph because the wires here are already burdened with gulls, and their messages are louder than ours. nevertheless: i write from the dock where salt stitches everything to itself. ropes are old sentences, knotted convincingly; boats bow and nod like kindly uncles who have forgotten our names but remember our childhood secrets.

i came to the edge to think about edges. they are elastic, it turns out. the sea pretends to end and then continues inside the ribs. one wave keeps correcting another; a grammar of blue. you would approve, having always believed that the heart is a coastline—subject to erosion, yes, but also forever re-drawn by moonlight and fishermen’s luck.

i am working, as you urged, at being unafraid of happiness. it is harder than swimming. happiness arrives barefoot and leaves no tracks to follow back. when the fog lifts, the houses look astonished by their own certainty. i have admired a thousand nets and learned this: what we catch is mostly our own reflection, and salt.

please tell Mother that the wind is not rude; it merely speaks a quicker dialect. tell her i eat, i sleep, i have not become the sort of poet who forgets to wear a coat. tell her i stand still when the lighthouse blinks, so it will not think me a storm.

when you come, bring your laugh, which works in all weathers. i will bring a jar for silence, just in case we need proof of it later.

as ever,

R.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
