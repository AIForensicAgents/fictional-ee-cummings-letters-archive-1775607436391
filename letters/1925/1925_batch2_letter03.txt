Account of a lemon-bright afternoon

Date: 1925-09-18
Recipient: Miss Araminta Doyle
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1925
Themes: art, city life, friendship, music, joy

Dear Araminta,

i kept your postcard in my pocket until it grew warm and therefore believable. today the city polished its elbows and hustled out in shirtsleeves; even the El rattled with politeness. i took a bench near the river and watched the sky peel itself like a lemon, curl after curl of light. men argued with newspapers, women argued with hats, children argued with gravity and mostly won. it was good.

i had intended to paint. instead i learned the choreography of bridges: how they rise like eyebrows, surprised into usefulness. a barge slid under with the grace of a sentence that knows exactly where to end. i made notes: steel is not cold when it remembers the furnaces; yellow is a reliable scandal; blue accepts apologies it never asked for.

i have found a studio with a slanting window (brave pane, always catching the day from the side). the landlord is wary of turpentine but less wary than despair. if you come, bring your impatient eyes; they are solvent and remove all manner of varnish. we will set a bowl of pears on a chair and interrupt them with brushes until they confess.

tonight a cornet climbed from the alley, note upon shiny note, and the alley agreed to be Paris for three minutes. i practiced your name in paint on newspaper scraps; the letters learned to dance, which felt like progress.

be uncareful with joy, dear friend. it does not break easily.

Yours in color,

Len
