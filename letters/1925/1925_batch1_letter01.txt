smoke-leaf afternoon

Date: 1925-01-08
Recipient: Lena M.
Location: Boston, Massachusetts
Year: 1925
Themes: winter, memory, longing, city, absence

Dear Lena,

Outside the brown smoke of January turns the sky into a pocket, and i keep putting my hands in it for warmth. The elms are thin-boned brides, veiled with frost, and every sparrow is a punctuation of breath. I have been walking these streets with their brick-rust memories, trying not to remember your wrists (small engines of light) but the mind keeps inventing doors and i go through them.

In my room the typewriter coughs. It is an old machine and prefers certain words, soft ones that do not bruise its keys: hush, alone, winter, you. I tuck the curtains in like a child and the radiator murmurs its iron blessing; we are a tiny orchestra rehearsing for a concert no one will attend.

Do you remember the tobacco leaf you pressed inside my book last autumn? It has become a brown moth of silence. When i touch it, the day loosens, and something like your laugh slides out and stands in the corner, shyly. Perhaps i am ridiculous. Perhaps that is the only way to be faithful.

I saw a pair of boots on Tremont, little spectacles of mud on their toes—exactly the way your boots spelled hurry when the rain began. I wanted to tell them to wait (please) but boots do not listen and neither do raindrops. Everything falls with its own certainty.

I am learning to breathe in this new grammar—one where absence is a verb and i am always conjugating it.

if by spring you grow a new city in your chest, water it well. mail me a fern of laughter (care of the half-asleep postman). i will write your name on the window and let the thaw answer back.

Yours, with the mittened sincerity January demands,

—E.
