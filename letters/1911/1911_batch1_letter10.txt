After the Factory Whistle

Date: 1911-12-05
Recipient: Mr. Lionel Hart
Location: Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania
Year: 1911
Themes: labor, smoke, friendship, compassion, winter

Dear Lionel,

When the whistle melts the day into evening, the streets open like a pocketknife. Men pour out in the color of smoke; their faces carry the gray arithmetic of hours added, subtracted, bargained. A boy in a cap runs ahead of his father with a loaf tucked under his arm as if it were a fresh idea he must protect from the wind.

I stood near the river and watched steel cool itself into obedience. The barges groaned like old saints learning new hymns. Women with red hands crossed the bridge in pairs, laughter clipped to fit the cold. The city wears its labor out where everyone can see it; even the rooftops sweat.

I think of you at your desk, that island of exactness adrift in a sea of heat. You said last month that the machines were practicing thunder. Do they still? Does your lunch pail remember your childhood?

If there is anything I can send—gloves, a book with clean snow in its margins, a scarf dyed the blue of the mercy you deny yourself—say so. I will tie it up with string and the wish that work might hurt less while meaning more.

The moon hangs over the mill like a pale coin the sky forgot to spend. Let us be rich, then, in what we keep unbroken: a letter, a hand, a slight, stubborn hope.

Always your friend,
C.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
