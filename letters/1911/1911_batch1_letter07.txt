Between Gramophones

Date: 1911-08-09
Recipient: Avery Bell
Location: London, England
Year: 1911
Themes: modernity, music, friendship, observation, city

Dear Avery,

All morning the shop across the street demonstrated a gramophone—his horn like a brass ear, the wax convinced to remember. Each record is a captured bird pretending the sky is circular. The proprietor winds and nods; the neighborhood listens in respectful astonishment. Progress is the name of the tune, and it steps handsomely.

I went walking after rain. The pavement shone with practical magic: puddles that were not water but compressed voyages. A suffrage procession unfurled in the next street, sashes crossing hearts like small flags that had finally learned where to live. I tipped my hat to a drum and felt the day accept the gesture.

You would like the tearoom near Charing Cross. It smells of oranges and ink. A girl at the next table wrote a letter on blue paper as if she were stitching a sail. I envied the thread of her certainty.

We are, all of us, devices that require winding. Yesterday I forgot and grew quiet. Today I am wound again (by a few bright words said over a counter, by a sunbeam that tried on my sleeve and found it to fit).

Come hear the gramophone argue with silence. It loses with style, which is sometimes all that matters.

Affectionately,
M.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
