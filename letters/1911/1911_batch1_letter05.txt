Harbor and Fog

Date: 1911-05-22
Recipient: Mr. Whitcombe, Editor
Location: New York, New York
Year: 1911
Themes: ambition, city, art, uncertainty, publication

Dear Mr. Whitcombe,

You asked for fewer metaphors and more truth; I have tried to send you both, held at the same angle, like two fish shining in the same net. The harbor was a patient gray this morning, the fog making a dress for each ship. I stood on the pier and felt the city editing itself—streets abbreviated into slips, buildings softened into rumor. Even the gulls seemed footnoted.

Enclosed are six poems and the apology they require. I am learning that a line must behave as a keel, steady even when the thought wants to leap. My typewriter enjoys its own weather—black snow in a small iron storm. Forgive the blots; the machine coughs like an old uncle but keeps its promises.

Print them or don’t, but know that each arrived the way most honest things do: delayed, stubborn, and suddenly. If I have failed, it is because I was listening too hard to the wind move through the rigging, and not hard enough to the word within the word.

Should you wish to speak, I am often at the café where the waiter names my hunger before I do. He is a better editor than either of us, and cheaper besides.

Yours in fog that teaches shape by erasing it,
H. Alder

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
