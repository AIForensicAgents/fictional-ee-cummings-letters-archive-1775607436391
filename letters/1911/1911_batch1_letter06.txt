Field Notes for a Heart

Date: 1911-06-30
Recipient: Eliza Morton
Location: Santa Fe, New Mexico Territory
Year: 1911
Themes: desert, love, nature, distance, heat

Eliza—

The desert keeps the hour in shadows. Mesquite scribbles on the ground until the sun reads it aloud. Clouds arrive like solemn airships and then (tricksters) spill themselves into sudden rivers. The thunder here is a clean instrument; you could eat your supper off its sound.

Your ribbon sleeps in my pocket. I unfold it when the wind forgets my name. Close one eye and the world is a coin; close the other and it becomes a bead—one must choose how to string the afternoon. I pick a cactus blossom, that improbable shout of silk, and press it into my journal. It bleeds the way joy does, a handsome stain.

A coyote trotted by with a grin made of angles. He did not stop for philosophy. I envied him his brisk unthinking. Meanwhile the telegraph line stitched distance to distance, and in each stitch I pressed your syllables (quiet, red).

Come in autumn when the cottonwoods are coins flicked up into a bright, foolish sky. We will spend them all in one day and go laughing home with empty pockets richly rustling.

Until then, I carry our argument about the color of silence. You say blue; I insist on gold. The sun is on my side, though evening favors you.

Lightly and obedient to your smile,
J.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
