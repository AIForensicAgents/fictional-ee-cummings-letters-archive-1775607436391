On the Trembling Rails

Date: 1911-02-19
Recipient: Brother Daniel Whitlow
Location: Chicago, Illinois
Year: 1911
Themes: travel, industrialization, family, restlessness, hope

Dear Dan,

The station breathes coal and intention. I could swear the rails hum a low vow before each departure, and my coat smells like the promise of elsewhere. I am not leaving (not yet), but my pockets are already full of distances.

Chicago wears a sky of iron filings. Sparks jump from the streetcar wires like small, excitable stars. The men at the loading docks move with that river-quiet strength we used to envy in the old plow horses. They lift, they nod, they continue. Continuance might be the bravest act—perhaps braver than any shout.

I think of our father’s hands, chapped maps of stubborn country. When I buy a newspaper I feel them guiding the coin, advising thrift and laughter in equal shares. Your last letter carried the sound of the farm—the wind that keeps its hat on, the bent nail that refuses to leave the fence. Dan, keep that world breathing; write its simple miracles to me before the city teaches me to forget them.

There is a boy here whose job is to oil the wheels. He whistles into the metal and the wheels answer with their smaller, shinier songs. I’d like to learn that language: the brass-throat hope of machines.

Should you come west in the spring, bring mother’s apple-knife. It makes courage of anything it touches, even an ordinary morning.

Stand straight, and when you bend, bend like a willow that knows where the river is going.

Your brother,
Thomas

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
