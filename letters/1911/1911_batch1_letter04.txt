An Apple-Blossom Telegram

Date: 1911-04-15
Recipient: Mother
Location: Cambridge, Massachusetts
Year: 1911
Themes: family, spring, education, distance, tenderness

Dearest Mother,

Today a telegram glanced off my life—thin as a reed and twice as quick. It said very little (they always say very little), and yet the day was rearranged by its short, electric sentence. The orchard near the college is rehearsing spring; the trees keep failing at restraint. Petals make snow that smells of bees.

You asked if the scholars here walk about with their heads full of thunder. Often. Sometimes the thunder is merely a furniture-moving above us, and sometimes it is the clear bell of a new thought. In lecture I scribbled a question so fast it outran its own tail; the professor raised an eyebrow, then gently set it down where it could breathe.

I miss the kitchen’s geography: the crack in the floorboards that points north, the stove that pretends to be the equator. Last night I braided the sound of your voice into my pillow (clumsy work, but effective). I woke with a ribbon of you against my cheek.

If father won’t prune the old apple tree, tell him even an old limb can learn the grammar of bloom. I would write him myself, but he always forgives me when you deliver the news.

The bells here pour the hour over us like warm milk. I drink. I learn. I keep your apron-tied courage in my pocket.

Your daughter,
R.

[This is a fictional creative work inspired by the style of E.E. Cummings and is not an authentic historical letter.]
